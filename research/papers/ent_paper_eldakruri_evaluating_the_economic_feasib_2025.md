## Overview

This paper investigates the economic viability of using robotics and automation to replace low-skilled expatriate labor in Qatar, a country whose economy depends heavily on foreign workers in construction, logistics, and manufacturing. The authors calibrate a capital-augmenting Cobb-Douglas production function using Qatar-specific data on labor composition, wage structures, and robotics imports, with elasticities and productivity coefficients adjusted from international benchmarks to Qatar's regulatory setting. They simulate three adoption scenarios—optimistic, status quo, and pessimistic—through 2030 to estimate GDP effects, labor displacement, and sector-level readiness.

Under the baseline scenario, a 5% increase in robotics capital is projected to generate an annual 1.5% GDP gain, while displacing 3–5% of workers in low-skilled sectors. Construction faces the highest displacement risk at 4.8%, whereas cultural resistance limits last-mile delivery automation to 32% adoption. The authors estimate that new jobs in maintenance and AI oversight could offset roughly 40% of displacement by 2030, but achieving inclusive growth will require training more than 850 technicians by 2027 and implementing safeguards for wages and social acceptance.

The study also addresses institutional and cultural barriers, including public resistance to robotic domestic workers and religious considerations, and proposes a phased, sector-specific policy framework involving reskilling programs, SME support, and wage stabilization.

## Key Contributions

- Quantifies the macroeconomic effects of robotics adoption in Qatar, projecting ~1.5% annual GDP growth from a 5% rise in robotics capital and 3–5% displacement in low-skilled sectors.
- Provides sector-level automation readiness and displacement analysis for construction, manufacturing, logistics, and agriculture in Qatar.
- Incorporates socio-cultural and institutional barriers, such as 68% resistance to robotic domestic workers and religious constraints on automation.
- Proposes a phased, sector-specific policy framework emphasizing workforce reskilling, SME support, and wage stabilization.
- Highlights ethical and macroeconomic risks associated with large-scale expatriate displacement and reduced remittance flows.

## Relevance to Humanoid Robotics

Although the paper focuses on robotics and automation broadly rather than on humanoid robots specifically, its economic feasibility framework, sector readiness assessment, and analysis of socio-cultural barriers are directly relevant to future humanoid robot deployment. Humanoid systems are likely to enter first in labor-intensive service and logistics roles similar to those analyzed, and the paper's estimates of displacement risk, cultural acceptance thresholds, and policy safeguards provide a useful reference for emerging-economy deployment contexts.
