## Overview

This paper addresses the question of when and why physically embodied robots should be preferred over simpler, cheaper virtual agents for social interaction tasks that do not require physical manipulation. The authors systematically review 65 empirical studies on embodiment published between 2003 and 2017 in major peer-reviewed robotics venues, grounding their analysis in related fields such as psychology, philosophy, and sociology.

To compare and synthesize the reviewed work, the authors introduce three taxonomies: one for types of robot embodiment, one for robot social roles, and one for human-robot tasks. These taxonomies are used to deconstruct the design and interaction spaces of socially interactive robots, which provide entertainment, information, and/or assistance. The paper also presents a characterization of the design space of robot embodiments as a starting point for discussing how physical form relates to social capabilities and affordances.

The synthesis reveals topics in embodiment research for social interaction, assistive robotics, and service robotics where further exploration would improve understanding of how embodiment affects human perception and evaluation of human-robot interactions. The authors discuss how existing findings can inform design decisions for future socially interactive robots.

## Key Contributions

- Three taxonomies classifying robot embodiments, social roles, and human-robot tasks
- A characterization of the design space for socially interactive robot embodiments
- A critical synthesis of 65 empirical embodiment studies across social, assistive, and service robotics
- Design recommendations and identified research gaps for future socially interactive robot development

## Relevance to Humanoid Robotics

The paper is highly relevant to humanoid robotics because it provides empirically grounded taxonomies and a design-space characterization that directly inform the selection of robot embodiments, including humanoid forms, for mass-deployed social, assistive, and service robots. By systematically comparing physical robots with virtual agents and other embodiments, the review helps designers understand when a humanoid body is justified and when simpler or non-physical forms may suffice.

The identified gaps and design recommendations can guide future humanoid robot development, particularly in balancing social capabilities, cost, and physical form. The taxonomy framework can also support standardization and benchmarking of humanoid social interaction systems.
