## 核心内容

人形机器人在真实场景中的自主移动需要同时解决两个层次的问题：宏观上，机器人必须能够感知环境中的可通过区域并规划出无碰撞的全局路径；微观上，它需要根据脚下地形的几何与物理特性（如台阶高度、斜坡倾角、窄梁宽度）实时调整步态与身体姿态。传统方法通常将这两个问题解耦，先由导航模块生成路径，再由运动控制模块跟踪路径并适应地形，但模块间的信息传递延迟与目标冲突（如导航追求最短路径而运动控制追求稳定性）常常导致系统在复杂地形中失效。GuideWalk的核心动机正是打破这种模块化壁垒，实现导航与运动的联合优化。

GuideWalk的框架设计围绕“复合教师蒸馏”展开。首先，分别训练两个专家教师策略：一个负责可通过性感知的导航规划，能够基于地形语义与几何信息生成避障路径；另一个负责地形自适应运动控制，能够针对楼梯、斜坡、窄梁等典型地形生成稳定的全身运动。随后，通过知识蒸馏技术，将这两个教师策略的知识压缩到一个统一的学生策略网络中。该学生网络以机器人本体感知（如关节角度、IMU数据）与环境感知（如深度图像、地形分类标签）为输入，直接输出全身关节控制指令，从而在推理阶段无需显式的模块切换或路径重规划。

在技术创新方面，GuideWalk的关键在于蒸馏后的联合微调阶段。研究者采用强化学习与行为克隆相结合的混合训练策略：强化学习部分以导航成功率、行走稳定性（如躯干俯仰角方差、足底滑移量）以及能量效率为奖励函数，鼓励策略探索更优的联合行为；行为克隆部分则利用教师策略的示范数据防止策略偏离有效区域，加速收敛。这种设计使得最终策略不仅继承了教师各自的专长，还能在两者冲突的边界场景（如窄梁上需要绕开障碍物）中自主权衡，生成平滑且鲁棒的复合行为。

实验验证方面，GuideWalk在仿真环境中对楼梯、斜坡、窄梁以及它们的随机组合地形进行了系统评估。结果表明，统一策略在导航成功率、行走稳定性以及地形适应速度上均显著优于分模块基线方法，尤其在窄梁与陡坡的组合场景中，传统方法因模块间协调失效而频繁跌倒，而GuideWalk能够保持稳定的步态并完成避障。此外，通过消融实验证实，复合教师蒸馏与联合微调两个环节缺一不可：缺少蒸馏会导致策略无法同时掌握两类技能，缺少微调则会使策略在未见地形上泛化能力不足。

从应用价值看，GuideWalk为人形机器人在灾害救援、家庭服务、工业巡检等需要穿越复杂地形的场景提供了可行的技术路径。其统一策略架构降低了系统复杂度与部署成本，且蒸馏-微调范式使得模型能够通过少量真实数据进一步适配特定环境，具备较强的实用潜力。未来工作可进一步探索将视觉语言模型引入可通过性感知，以提升策略在未知环境中的语义理解能力。

## Overview

Humanoid robots face two major challenges in autonomous navigation and stable locomotion within complex unstructured environments: navigation planning and terrain-adaptive motion control are typically designed as independent modules, leading to difficulties in balancing global obstacle avoidance with local stability on mixed terrains such as stairs, slopes, and narrow beams. This paper proposes the GuideWalk framework, which integrates passability-aware navigation planning and terrain-adaptive motion control into a unified policy through composite teacher distillation, and employs joint fine-tuning with reinforcement learning and behavior cloning. This enables the robot to achieve efficient obstacle avoidance and robust walking simultaneously across diverse terrains. This work provides a new paradigm for building end-to-end autonomous mobility intelligence for humanoid robots.

## Content

Autonomous mobility of humanoid robots in real-world scenarios requires solving two levels of problems simultaneously: at the macro level, the robot must perceive traversable areas in the environment and plan a collision-free global path; at the micro level, it needs to adjust its gait and body posture in real time based on the geometric and physical properties of the terrain underfoot (e.g., step height, slope angle, narrow beam width). Traditional methods typically decouple these two problems, first generating a path via a navigation module and then tracking the path and adapting to the terrain via a motion control module. However, information transmission delays and conflicting objectives between modules (e.g., navigation pursuing the shortest path while motion control prioritizes stability) often cause system failures in complex terrains. The core motivation of GuideWalk is to break this modular barrier and achieve joint optimization of navigation and locomotion.

The framework design of GuideWalk revolves around "composite teacher distillation." First, two expert teacher policies are trained separately: one responsible for passability-aware navigation planning, capable of generating obstacle-avoiding paths based on terrain semantics and geometric information; the other responsible for terrain-adaptive motion control, capable of generating stable whole-body motions for typical terrains such as stairs, slopes, and narrow beams. Subsequently, through knowledge distillation, the knowledge of these two teacher policies is compressed into a unified student policy network. This student network takes the robot's proprioceptive perceptions (e.g., joint angles, IMU data) and environmental perceptions (e.g., depth images, terrain classification labels) as input and directly outputs whole-body joint control commands, eliminating the need for explicit module switching or path replanning during inference.

In terms of technical innovation, the key to GuideWalk lies in the joint fine-tuning phase after distillation. The researchers adopt a hybrid training strategy combining reinforcement learning and behavior cloning: the reinforcement learning part uses navigation success rate, walking stability (e.g., torso pitch angle variance, foot slip), and energy efficiency as reward functions to encourage the policy to explore better joint behaviors; the behavior cloning part leverages demonstration data from the teacher policies to prevent the policy from deviating from effective regions and accelerate convergence. This design ensures that the final policy not only inherits the respective expertise of the teachers but also autonomously balances conflicting boundary scenarios (e.g., needing to avoid obstacles on a narrow beam), generating smooth and robust composite behaviors.

In terms of experimental validation, GuideWalk was systematically evaluated in simulation environments on stairs, slopes, narrow beams, and their random combinations. Results show that the unified policy significantly outperforms modular baseline methods in navigation success rate, walking stability, and terrain adaptation speed. Particularly in combined scenarios of narrow beams and steep slopes, where traditional methods frequently fall due to coordination failures between modules, GuideWalk maintains a stable gait and completes obstacle avoidance. Additionally, ablation experiments confirm that both composite teacher distillation and joint fine-tuning are indispensable: without distillation, the policy cannot simultaneously master both types of skills; without fine-tuning, the policy lacks generalization ability on unseen terrains.

From an application perspective, GuideWalk provides a feasible technical pathway for humanoid robots in scenarios requiring traversal of complex terrains, such as disaster rescue, household services, and industrial inspection. Its unified policy architecture reduces system complexity and deployment costs, and the distillation-fine-tuning paradigm allows the model to further adapt to specific environments with a small amount of real data, demonstrating strong practical potential. Future work could explore incorporating vision-language models into passability perception to enhance the policy's semantic understanding in unknown environments.
