## Overview

The paper addresses pose prediction for mobile ground robots with active flippers operating on rough terrain. It models the environment as a Euclidean Signed Distance Field (ESDF) generated by Voxblox, which encodes the Euclidean distance to the closest surface and enables sub-voxel surface localization. Given an SE(2) position and heading plus the robot's joint configuration, the algorithm iteratively predicts the missing z coordinate, roll, and pitch of the settled SE(3) pose.

The method consists of two stages. In the Falling Stage, the robot is translated downward along the world z-axis until at least one contact point is found and no contact candidate is below the ground. In the Rotation Stage, the robot is rotated around the least stable axis of the support polygon until a stable pose is reached; stability is assessed with the Force-Angle Stability Margin (FASM). The authors assume a rigid robot and environment and no slippage between the robot and the surface.

The approach is evaluated on two tracked robots—the Asterix and the DRZ Telemax—in four RoboCup Rescue Robot League (RRL) arenas in simulation and on the real Asterix platform with Qualisys motion-capture ground truth. Compared to a Heightmap Pose Prediction baseline, the SDF method achieves lower errors, especially on multi-level terrain, with an average real-world accuracy of 3.11 cm and 3.91°.

## Key Contributions

- Novel iterative geometric pose prediction on ESDFs with sub-voxel surface accuracy.
- Joint-configuration-aware prediction supporting robots with active flippers.
- Support for multi-level 3D environments beyond single-layer heightmaps.
- Open-source ROS1 implementation released to the community.
- Evaluation on two distinct tracked robots in simulation and on a real platform with motion-capture ground truth.

## Relevance to Humanoid Robotics

Although the experiments use tracked robots, the core techniques—ESDF-based contact estimation, iterative pose settlement, and online whole-body pose prediction—are conceptually transferable to humanoid locomotion. Humanoid robots likewise require accurate foot-terrain contact and balance prediction over rough terrain, and the sub-voxel surface representation provided by SDFs can support precise foot placement and stability estimation.

The assumption of rigid contacts and no slippage is also common in early humanoid pose-estimation literature, so the method's formalism could inform humanoid contact planners and whole-body controllers. Direct validation on legged or humanoid platforms would be needed to confirm transferability.
