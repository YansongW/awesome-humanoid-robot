## Overview

ActiveUMI is a study on operational skill learning for humanoid robots, with the core motivation of addressing the reliance on expensive robot platforms and fixed perception systems in traditional robot demonstration learning. This work proposes an active perception data collection paradigm that does not require robot involvement, enabling humans to record high-quality operational demonstrations using only a handheld device, which can be directly transferred to humanoid robots for execution. This method significantly lowers the barrier to data acquisition, offering a new technical pathway for large-scale learning of dexterous operational skills in robotics.

## Content

### Research Background and Problem

Current operational skill learning for humanoid robots heavily depends on the "robot demonstration" model, where researchers must control a real robot platform to perform task demonstrations, followed by policy training through behavioral cloning or imitation learning. This approach is not only costly and time-consuming to deploy but also limited by the robot's range of motion and perception capabilities, making it difficult to obtain diverse and highly flexible demonstration data. Meanwhile, although human natural demonstrations are rich in content, they lack perception information aligned with the robot's execution, leading to difficulties in transfer. ActiveUMI addresses this contradiction by exploring how to collect demonstration data containing active perception information without using a robot, thereby balancing data richness with transfer feasibility.

### Method or Model Framework

ActiveUMI proposes a lightweight handheld data collection device that integrates visual sensors and inertial measurement units, capable of recording hand motion trajectories and first-person visual information during human demonstrations. The core idea is to convert the "active perception" behavior in human demonstrations—naturally occurring viewpoint adjustments and attention shifts during operation—into perception-action coupling data that robots can learn from. During the training phase, the system uses cross-morphology alignment techniques to map human demonstration data into the motion space of humanoid robots, enabling the robot to replicate the fine-grained operational strategies from the human demonstrations.

### Key Technical Innovations

The technical contributions of this work are primarily in three aspects: First, it introduces the concept of "active perception" into a robot-free demonstration learning framework for the first time, ensuring that the collected data includes not only end-effector movements but also task-related visual exploration behaviors, which enhances the robot's adaptability to dynamic environments. Second, ActiveUMI designs an efficient cross-morphology data alignment mechanism that handles differences in degrees of freedom and range of motion between human hands and humanoid robot end-effectors, ensuring the precision of policy transfer. Finally, by simplifying hardware requirements, this work makes large-scale, multi-task data collection feasible, laying a data foundation for subsequent general operational skill learning.

### Experiments/Validation and Application Value

Although specific experimental data are not detailed in the metadata, according to the project homepage description, ActiveUMI has been validated on various typical operational tasks, including grasping, placing, and screw tightening, which require fine control. Experimental results indicate that policies trained with active perception demonstrations outperform traditional static demonstration methods in terms of success rate and robustness. From an application perspective, ActiveUMI is expected to advance the practical deployment of humanoid robots in areas such as home services and precision assembly, particularly in scenarios requiring rapid deployment where robot demonstration data is difficult to obtain. Additionally, the openness and scalability of this framework provide interfaces for future integration with large language models and visual foundation models.
