## 概述

本文提出了一种面向人形机器人的渐进式感知-行动框架，旨在解决足球技能学习中多模态感知与复杂动作生成的耦合难题。研究将动作生成重新定义为条件生成问题，利用扩散模型或流匹配方法从多模态动作分布中采样可执行轨迹，从而提升机器人在动态环境中的适应性与鲁棒性。该框架通过整合视觉、运动捕捉与仿真数据，为机器人足球技能的学习提供了一条系统化的技术路径。

## 核心内容

人形机器人在足球场景中面临感知与行动的双重挑战：一方面，机器人需要从相机图像、多视角观测或人类视频中实时恢复场景、目标与自身运动表征；另一方面，生成的足球技能动作（如带球、传球、射门）必须满足物理约束、运动学可行性以及动态对抗环境下的实时性要求。现有方法往往将感知与动作生成分离处理，导致信息传递损失与动作模式单一化。本研究的关键洞察在于，足球技能本质上属于多模态动作分布中的条件采样问题——给定当前场景状态与任务目标，机器人需要从大量可能的执行轨迹中选出最优解。

该框架采用渐进式分层架构，首先通过视觉闭环模块从多源观测数据中提取场景语义与运动特征，包括目标位置、队友与对手的轨迹预测以及自身关节状态。随后，利用扩散策略或流匹配技术，将动作生成建模为条件概率分布的学习过程：在训练阶段，模型从人类演示或仿真交互数据中学习动作轨迹的分布特性；在推理阶段，通过逐步去噪或流变换从随机噪声中采样出符合当前场景约束的可执行轨迹。这一设计避免了传统确定性策略在复杂多模态场景下的模式坍塌问题。

关键技术方面，框架引入了三项创新：第一，基于逆运动学与动作重定向的跨模态对齐机制，使得从人类视频或动捕数据中提取的运动模式能够适配人形机器人的物理结构差异；第二，分层技能与专家策略的融合，将复杂足球技能分解为子技能（如控球、转向、加速），并通过上层规划器协调子技能的切换与组合；第三，接触规划与移动操作的一体化建模，在生成动作轨迹时同步考虑足部接触点与手部操作任务，确保运动稳定性与任务完成度的平衡。

实验验证方面，该框架在仿真环境与实体人形机器人平台上进行了足球技能测试。仿真实验表明，相比基于规则或确定性策略的基线方法，扩散策略生成的轨迹在成功率、动作平滑度与对抗鲁棒性上均有显著提升。实体实验进一步验证了视觉闭环在动态场景中的实时性：机器人能够根据对手位置变化在线调整带球路径，并利用流匹配的快速采样特性实现毫秒级动作更新。该工作为机器人足球竞赛与通用移动操作任务提供了可迁移的技术范式，其核心思想——将动作生成视为条件生成问题——对于其他需要多模态适应性的机器人应用（如家庭服务、灾难救援）同样具有参考价值。

## Overview

This paper proposes a progressive perception-action framework for humanoid robots, aiming to address the coupling challenges of multimodal perception and complex action generation in soccer skill learning. The study redefines action generation as a conditional generation problem, leveraging diffusion models or flow matching methods to sample executable trajectories from multimodal action distributions, thereby enhancing the robot's adaptability and robustness in dynamic environments. By integrating visual, motion capture, and simulation data, the framework provides a systematic technical pathway for learning robotic soccer skills.

## Content

Humanoid robots face dual challenges of perception and action in soccer scenarios: on one hand, robots need to recover scene, target, and self-motion representations in real time from camera images, multi-view observations, or human videos; on the other hand, generated soccer skill actions (e.g., dribbling, passing, shooting) must satisfy physical constraints, kinematic feasibility, and real-time requirements in dynamic adversarial environments. Existing methods often separate perception from action generation, leading to information transmission loss and monotonous action patterns. The key insight of this study is that soccer skills are essentially a conditional sampling problem within multimodal action distributions—given the current scene state and task goal, the robot must select the optimal solution from a vast set of possible executable trajectories.

The framework adopts a progressive hierarchical architecture. First, a visual closed-loop module extracts scene semantics and motion features from multi-source observation data, including target positions, trajectory predictions of teammates and opponents, and the robot's own joint states. Subsequently, using diffusion policies or flow matching techniques, action generation is modeled as a learning process of conditional probability distributions: during the training phase, the model learns the distribution characteristics of action trajectories from human demonstrations or simulation interaction data; during the inference phase, executable trajectories that conform to current scene constraints are sampled from random noise through stepwise denoising or flow transformation. This design avoids the mode collapse problem of traditional deterministic policies in complex multimodal scenarios.

In terms of key technologies, the framework introduces three innovations: first, a cross-modal alignment mechanism based on inverse kinematics and action retargeting, enabling motion patterns extracted from human videos or motion capture data to adapt to the physical structural differences of humanoid robots; second, the integration of hierarchical skills and expert policies, decomposing complex soccer skills into sub-skills (e.g., ball control, turning, acceleration) and coordinating the switching and combination of sub-skills through a high-level planner; third, integrated modeling of contact planning and mobile manipulation, simultaneously considering foot contact points and hand manipulation tasks when generating action trajectories, ensuring a balance between motion stability and task completion.

In terms of experimental validation, the framework was tested for soccer skills in both simulation environments and on physical humanoid robot platforms. Simulation experiments show that, compared to rule-based or deterministic policy baselines, trajectories generated by diffusion policies achieve significant improvements in success rate, action smoothness, and adversarial robustness. Physical experiments further verify the real-time performance of the visual closed-loop in dynamic scenes: the robot can adjust its dribbling path online based on opponent position changes and achieve millisecond-level action updates using the fast sampling characteristics of flow matching. This work provides a transferable technical paradigm for robotic soccer competitions and general mobile manipulation tasks, and its core idea—treating action generation as a conditional generation problem—also holds reference value for other robotic applications requiring multimodal adaptability, such as home service and disaster rescue.
