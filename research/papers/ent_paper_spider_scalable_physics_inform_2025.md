人形机器人在复杂环境中执行全身协调的灵巧操作任务，仍面临运动重定向（retargeting）过程中物理一致性与可扩展性不足的挑战。现有方法往往依赖离线优化或简化动力学模型，难以兼顾实时性与全身运动的高保真度。SPIDER（Scalable Physics-Informed DExterous Retargeting）提出了一种融合物理信息与可扩展架构的全身控制框架，旨在实现从人类示范到人形机器人灵巧操作与移动操控（loco-manipulation）的高效、鲁棒映射。该工作通过将物理约束嵌入重定向流程，显著提升了机器人执行复杂任务时的运动自然性与稳定性，为人形机器人的全身自主控制提供了新的技术路径。

## 核心内容

人形机器人的全身控制（whole-body control）与灵巧操作（dexterous manipulation）是当前机器人学领域的前沿课题，其核心难点在于如何将人类丰富的运动技能高效、安全地迁移到具有不同运动学与动力学特性的机器人平台上。传统的运动重定向方法通常采用几何映射或基于逆运动学的优化，但往往忽略了接触力、关节力矩约束以及动态平衡等物理因素，导致生成的运动在实际执行时出现滑移、失稳或关节超限等问题。此外，现有方案在面对不同机器人构型或任务场景时缺乏良好的可扩展性，难以实现从单一操作到全身移动操控的平滑过渡。

SPIDER方法的核心在于构建一个物理信息驱动的可扩展重定向框架。该框架首先将人类示范的运动数据（包括肢体轨迹与手部姿态）作为输入，通过一个轻量级的物理仿真层对运动进行实时校验与修正。具体而言，SPIDER引入了一个“物理一致性损失函数”，该函数同时考虑地面反作用力、关节力矩极限、自碰撞避免以及质心轨迹的动力学可行性。与传统方法仅关注运动学匹配不同，SPIDER将上述物理约束直接嵌入到重定向优化过程中，从而在保持运动风格相似性的前提下，确保生成的运动序列在真实机器人上可执行且稳定。

在技术创新方面，SPIDER提出了两项关键设计。其一，采用分层式优化策略：上层负责全局运动规划，利用简化模型快速评估运动可行性；下层则针对手部与足部的精细操作进行局部微调，兼顾计算效率与精度。其二，设计了一种“可扩展接口”，允许用户通过配置参数灵活切换任务模式（如纯上肢操作、下肢行走或全身移动操控），而无需重新训练模型。这种模块化架构使得SPIDER能够适配不同型号的人形机器人平台，显著降低了部署成本。

实验验证部分，SPIDER在多个标准人形机器人仿真环境与真实硬件平台上进行了测试。结果表明，与基于纯运动学重定向的基线方法相比，SPIDER生成的运动在接触稳定性（如足底压力分布均匀性）和关节力矩裕度上分别提升了约30%和25%。在灵巧操作任务（如抓取不规则物体）中，SPIDER成功实现了多指手与手臂的协调运动，且未出现因动力学冲突导致的执行失败。此外，在移动操控场景（如行走中开门）中，SPIDER展示了全身运动规划与实时重定向的协同能力，验证了其在实际应用中的鲁棒性与可扩展性。该工作为人形机器人从实验室环境走向复杂真实任务提供了重要的技术支撑，尤其在需要高动态平衡与精细操作结合的领域（如家庭服务、工业装配）具有广阔的应用前景。

## Overview

Humanoid robots performing whole-body coordinated dexterous manipulation tasks in complex environments still face challenges of insufficient physical consistency and scalability during motion retargeting. Existing methods often rely on offline optimization or simplified dynamic models, making it difficult to balance real-time performance with high-fidelity whole-body motion. SPIDER (Scalable Physics-Informed DExterous Retargeting) proposes a whole-body control framework that integrates physical information with a scalable architecture, aiming to achieve efficient and robust mapping from human demonstrations to dexterous manipulation and loco-manipulation for humanoid robots. By embedding physical constraints into the retargeting process, this work significantly enhances the naturalness and stability of motion when robots perform complex tasks, providing a new technical pathway for whole-body autonomous control of humanoid robots.

## Content

Whole-body control and dexterous manipulation of humanoid robots are frontier topics in current robotics research. The core challenge lies in how to efficiently and safely transfer rich human motor skills to robot platforms with different kinematic and dynamic characteristics. Traditional motion retargeting methods typically employ geometric mapping or inverse kinematics-based optimization, but often neglect physical factors such as contact forces, joint torque constraints, and dynamic balance, leading to issues like slipping, instability, or joint limit violations during actual execution of the generated motions. Furthermore, existing solutions lack good scalability when facing different robot configurations or task scenarios, making it difficult to achieve a smooth transition from single manipulation to whole-body loco-manipulation.

The core of the SPIDER method lies in constructing a physics-informed scalable retargeting framework. This framework first takes human demonstration motion data (including limb trajectories and hand poses) as input, and performs real-time verification and correction of the motion through a lightweight physics simulation layer. Specifically, SPIDER introduces a "physics consistency loss function" that simultaneously considers ground reaction forces, joint torque limits, self-collision avoidance, and the dynamic feasibility of the center-of-mass trajectory. Unlike traditional methods that focus only on kinematic matching, SPIDER directly embeds these physical constraints into the retargeting optimization process, thereby ensuring that the generated motion sequences are executable and stable on real robots while maintaining motion style similarity.

In terms of technical innovation, SPIDER proposes two key designs. First, it adopts a hierarchical optimization strategy: the upper layer is responsible for global motion planning, using a simplified model to quickly assess motion feasibility; the lower layer performs local fine-tuning for fine manipulation of hands and feet, balancing computational efficiency and precision. Second, it designs a "scalable interface" that allows users to flexibly switch task modes (such as pure upper-limb manipulation, lower-limb walking, or whole-body loco-manipulation) by configuring parameters, without the need to retrain the model. This modular architecture enables SPIDER to adapt to different types of humanoid robot platforms, significantly reducing deployment costs.

In the experimental validation section, SPIDER was tested on multiple standard humanoid robot simulation environments and real hardware platforms. Results show that compared to baseline methods based on pure kinematic retargeting, the motions generated by SPIDER improved contact stability (e.g., uniformity of foot pressure distribution) and joint torque margins by approximately 30% and 25%, respectively. In dexterous manipulation tasks (e.g., grasping irregular objects), SPIDER successfully achieved coordinated motion of multi-fingered hands and arms without execution failures due to dynamic conflicts. Additionally, in loco-manipulation scenarios (e.g., opening a door while walking), SPIDER demonstrated the synergy between whole-body motion planning and real-time retargeting, verifying its robustness and scalability in practical applications. This work provides important technical support for humanoid robots transitioning from laboratory environments to complex real-world tasks, especially in fields requiring a combination of high dynamic balance and fine manipulation (such as home service and industrial assembly), showing broad application prospects.
