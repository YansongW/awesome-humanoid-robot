## Overview

WT-UMI proposes a contact-aware whole-body manipulation framework based on force supervision, aiming to address the closed-loop problem in data collection and policy learning for humanoid robots. By integrating multi-view visual observations, teleoperation/exoskeleton data, and contact force/tactile signals, this research maps human manipulation and robot states into trainable whole-body trajectories and action sequences. Its main contribution lies in modeling action generation as a conditional generation problem, using diffusion models or flow matching methods to sample executable trajectories from multimodal action distributions, thereby improving data utilization efficiency and policy generalization.

## Content

(a) Research Background and Problem: Whole-body manipulation of humanoid robots faces two core challenges: first, how to efficiently collect high-quality demonstration data that includes force feedback; second, how to transform multimodal, high-dimensional manipulation information into reusable policy models. Existing methods mostly rely on visual observations or single joint angles, lacking systematic integration of contact force and tactile signals, leading to insufficient robustness in fine manipulation tasks. WT-UMI addresses this data closed-loop problem by introducing contact force as a supervisory signal into the planning process to enhance perception and prediction capabilities for physical interactions.

(b) Method or Model Framework: WT-UMI constructs an end-to-end data-driven framework whose inputs include camera images/multi-view observations, joint states recorded by teleoperation/exoskeleton devices, and contact force/tactile sensor data. These heterogeneous data are uniformly encoded as conditional variables to guide the action generation model. At the model level, the research adopts Behavioral Cloning (ACT) as the basic imitation learning paradigm and introduces diffusion policies or flow matching mechanisms, treating action generation as a conditional generation problem. By sampling from multimodal action distributions, the model can output whole-body trajectories, end-effector/wrist-hand targets, and action chunk/token sequences.

(c) Key Technical Innovations: The core innovation of this work lies in "force-supervised contact-aware planning." Unlike traditional methods that rely solely on vision or kinematics, WT-UMI explicitly incorporates contact force as a planning constraint, enabling the model to learn strategies for adjusting posture and force during physical interactions. Additionally, using diffusion or flow matching methods to handle the multimodality of action distributions allows multiple feasible manipulation trajectories to be generated from the same visual input, significantly enhancing policy flexibility and robustness. The completeness of the data closed-loop (from collection to training to deployment) is also a key contribution.

(d) Experiments/Validation or Application Value: Although the paper does not provide specific experimental data, it can be inferred from the framework design that WT-UMI is suitable for whole-body manipulation scenarios requiring fine force control, such as assembly, material handling, or human-robot collaboration. Its data collection module is compatible with various teleoperation devices (e.g., exoskeletons), lowering the demonstration threshold; meanwhile, the diffusion-based policy generation is expected to maintain high success rates in complex environments (e.g., unstructured objects or dynamic contact conditions). This work provides a practical technical pathway for the paradigm shift of humanoid robots from "visual servoing" to "force-aware integration," with potential applications in industrial automation and home service robotics.
