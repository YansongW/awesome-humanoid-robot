移动操作（loco-manipulation）是人形机器人走向实际应用的关键能力，要求机器人同时具备导航、抓取、操作与全身协调控制。然而，现有系统通常将任务规划与运动控制分离：高层规划依赖预定义规则或有限状态机，缺乏对动态场景的适应性；底层控制则多采用模型预测控制（MPC）或全身控制器（WBC），难以处理长时域、多约束的复杂任务。此外，从自然语言指令到可执行动作序列的语义鸿沟，以及多模态感知（视觉、本体、关节序列）的异构融合，进一步加剧了系统设计的复杂度。HYPERmotion 针对上述问题，提出将任务拆解为可路由的技能单元，并通过高层模块动态选择与组合，从而弥合语义规划与运动执行之间的断层。

（b）方法或模型框架  
HYPERmotion 的整体框架分为感知表征层、语义规划层与运动执行层。首先，系统从语言指令、相机图像/多视角观测、本体状态与关节序列中恢复场景、目标或运动的高维表征，形成统一的场景理解。随后，视觉语言模型（VLM）作为高层路由模块，将任务分解为一系列可复用的技能（如“移动至目标”、“抓取物体”、“调整姿态”），并依据当前环境状态规划执行顺序。在运动执行层，PPO/RL 策略网络负责生成精细的关节动作命令，而全身控制器（WBC）与 MPC 则协调腿部与臂部的运动，确保动态平衡与操作精度。这种分层架构使得高层语义推理与底层运动控制能够高效协同，避免了端到端策略在长时域任务中的训练困难。

HYPERmotion 的核心创新在于“混合行为规划”机制：它并非简单地将规划与控制串联，而是通过可路由的技能库实现任务级与动作级的动态耦合。具体而言，系统预先训练一组专家策略（如导航策略、抓取策略、避障策略），每个策略对应一个原子技能；高层 VLM 模块则根据当前场景语义与任务目标，实时选择并组合这些技能，形成可执行的混合策略。这种设计不仅降低了端到端训练的样本复杂度，还使得系统能够灵活应对未见过的任务变体。此外，多模态感知的融合方式也值得关注——通过联合编码语言、视觉与本体信息，模型能够同时理解“做什么”、“在哪里”与“如何做”，从而生成上下文感知的运动指令。

## Overview

Humanoid robots performing autonomous mobile manipulation tasks in complex environments face the challenge of deeply integrating perception, planning, and control. Existing methods often treat scene understanding, task planning, and motion control separately, making it difficult to achieve end-to-end efficient reasoning from high-level semantic instructions to low-level joint actions. HYPERmotion proposes a hybrid behavior planning framework that combines the semantic routing capabilities of Vision-Language Models (VLMs) with the fine-grained motion control of reinforcement learning (PPO/RL), enabling the synergy of task-level skill decomposition and execution-level policy composition. The core contribution of this work lies in constructing a unified reasoning pipeline from multimodal perception to whole-body coordinated control, offering a new paradigm for autonomous operation of humanoid robots in unstructured environments.

## Content

(a) Research Background and Problem  
Mobile manipulation (loco-manipulation) is a key capability for humanoid robots to move toward practical applications, requiring robots to simultaneously handle navigation, grasping, manipulation, and whole-body coordination. However, existing systems typically separate task planning from motion control: high-level planning relies on predefined rules or finite state machines, lacking adaptability to dynamic scenes; low-level control often uses Model Predictive Control (MPC) or Whole-Body Control (WBC), which struggles with long-horizon, multi-constraint complex tasks. Additionally, the semantic gap from natural language instructions to executable action sequences, along with the heterogeneous fusion of multimodal perception (vision, proprioception, joint sequences), further increases system design complexity. To address these issues, HYPERmotion proposes decomposing tasks into routable skill units, dynamically selected and combined by a high-level module, thereby bridging the gap between semantic planning and motion execution.

(b) Method or Model Framework  
The overall framework of HYPERmotion is divided into a perception representation layer, a semantic planning layer, and a motion execution layer. First, the system recovers high-dimensional representations of scenes, objects, or motions from language instructions, camera images/multi-view observations, proprioceptive states, and joint sequences, forming a unified scene understanding. Then, a Vision-Language Model (VLM) acts as a high-level routing module, decomposing tasks into a series of reusable skills (e.g., "move to target," "grasp object," "adjust posture") and planning the execution order based on the current environment state. In the motion execution layer, a PPO/RL policy network generates fine-grained joint action commands, while Whole-Body Control (WBC) and MPC coordinate leg and arm movements to ensure dynamic balance and operational precision. This hierarchical architecture enables efficient synergy between high-level semantic reasoning and low-level motion control, avoiding the training difficulties of end-to-end policies in long-horizon tasks.

(c) Key Technical Innovations  
The core innovation of HYPERmotion lies in its "hybrid behavior planning" mechanism: rather than simply concatenating planning and control, it achieves dynamic coupling between task-level and action-level through a routable skill library. Specifically, the system pre-trains a set of expert policies (e.g., navigation policy, grasping policy, obstacle avoidance policy), each corresponding to an atomic skill; the high-level VLM module then selects and combines these skills in real time based on current scene semantics and task goals, forming an executable hybrid policy. This design not only reduces the sample complexity of end-to-end training but also allows the system to flexibly handle unseen task variants. Additionally, the multimodal perception fusion method is noteworthy—by jointly encoding language, vision, and proprioceptive information, the model can simultaneously understand "what to do," "where to do it," and "how to do it," generating context-aware motion commands.

(d) Experiments/Validation and Application Value  
Although the paper does not provide specific experimental data, its technical approach suggests that HYPERmotion is suitable for typical scenarios requiring long-horizon reasoning combined with fine-grained manipulation, such as home services, warehouse logistics, and industrial inspection. For example, in the task of "taking a cup from the table and placing it in the dishwasher," the system must sequentially complete subtasks including target localization, path planning, grasp posture adjustment, obstacle avoidance during movement, and precise placement. HYPERmotion's hybrid planning framework can use the VLM to understand the semantic attributes of the "cup," invoke the navigation policy to approach the target, and then switch to the grasping policy to complete the operation, with WBC ensuring whole-body stability throughout the process. The potential application value of this method lies in: on one hand, reducing repetitive training costs through skill reuse; on the other hand, enabling robots to understand natural language instructions via high-level semantic routing, lowering the barrier for human-robot interaction. In the future, this framework can be further extended to multi-robot collaboration or real-time replanning in dynamic environments.
