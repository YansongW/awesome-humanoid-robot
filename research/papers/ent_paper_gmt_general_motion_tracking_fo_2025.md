## 概述

人形机器人的全身控制（Whole-Body Control）长期面临一个核心挑战：如何将高维的感知状态与关节序列转化为稳定、可执行的运动轨迹，尤其是在面对多模态动作分布时。现有方法往往依赖手工设计的运动模板或特定任务的控制器，缺乏对多样化运动模式的泛化能力。为此，本文提出通用运动跟踪框架（General Motion Tracking, GMT），将动作生成重新定义为条件生成问题，利用扩散策略与流匹配技术，从多模态动作分布中采样可执行轨迹，从而实现对全身运动的统一跟踪与控制。该工作的主要贡献在于提出了一种端到端的、可组合的全身控制范式，为构建行为基础模型提供了关键技术支撑。

## 核心内容

### 研究背景与问题

人形机器人的运动控制涉及平衡、行走、操作等多个子任务，传统方法通常为每个任务单独设计控制器，导致系统复杂且难以迁移。近年来，基于强化学习和模仿学习的方法虽取得进展，但在处理多模态动作分布（如同一步态下多种可能的关节配置）时仍存在采样效率低、轨迹不连贯等问题。此外，本体状态与关节序列之间的映射关系往往是非线性的，且受限于仿真与真实环境的差异，使得运动跟踪的鲁棒性不足。GMT 正是针对这些痛点，提出将运动跟踪统一为条件生成问题，以提升全身控制的通用性与适应性。

### 方法或模型框架

GMT 的核心框架包含三个关键模块：数据转换层、生成模型层与策略组合层。首先，数据转换层将本体状态、关节序列以及仿真交互数据统一编码为“可跟踪的身体目标”（trackable body targets），从而消除不同数据源之间的异构性。随后，生成模型层采用扩散策略（Diffusion Policy）或流匹配（Flow Matching）技术，将动作生成建模为从噪声到可执行轨迹的逆向扩散过程，能够在多模态动作分布中高效采样。最后，策略组合层通过分层技能（Hierarchical Skill）与专家策略（Expert Strategy）训练，将基础运动技能（如平衡、行走）组合为全身控制策略，并输出全身轨迹、动作序列以及低层控制器目标。

### 关键技术创新

GMT 的三大技术创新显著提升了人形全身控制的性能。第一，将动作生成视为条件生成问题，使得模型能够自然地处理多模态动作分布，避免了传统方法中单一轨迹拟合的局限性。第二，引入流匹配作为扩散策略的替代方案，在保持采样质量的同时降低了计算复杂度，更适合实时控制场景。第三，提出分层技能与专家策略的训练机制，允许不同运动技能独立学习后再组合，从而支持复杂任务的零样本泛化。此外，GMT 的设计天然兼容行为基础模型（Behavioral Foundation Model）的范式，为未来大规模预训练运动模型奠定了基础。

### 实验/验证与应用价值

## Overview

Whole-body control of humanoid robots has long faced a core challenge: how to transform high-dimensional perception states and joint sequences into stable, executable motion trajectories, especially when dealing with multimodal action distributions. Existing methods often rely on handcrafted motion templates or task-specific controllers, lacking generalization across diverse motion patterns. To address this, this paper proposes the General Motion Tracking (GMT) framework, which redefines action generation as a conditional generation problem. By leveraging diffusion policy and flow matching techniques, it samples executable trajectories from multimodal action distributions, thereby achieving unified tracking and control of whole-body motion. The main contribution of this work lies in proposing an end-to-end, composable whole-body control paradigm, providing key technical support for building behavioral foundation models.

## Content

### Research Background and Problem

Motion control of humanoid robots involves multiple subtasks such as balancing, walking, and manipulation. Traditional methods typically design separate controllers for each task, leading to system complexity and poor transferability. In recent years, approaches based on reinforcement learning and imitation learning have made progress, but they still suffer from low sampling efficiency and incoherent trajectories when handling multimodal action distributions (e.g., multiple possible joint configurations under the same gait). Moreover, the mapping between proprioceptive states and joint sequences is often nonlinear and limited by the gap between simulation and real environments, resulting in insufficient robustness in motion tracking. GMT addresses these pain points by unifying motion tracking as a conditional generation problem, enhancing the generality and adaptability of whole-body control.

### Method or Model Framework

The core framework of GMT consists of three key modules: a data transformation layer, a generative model layer, and a policy composition layer. First, the data transformation layer uniformly encodes proprioceptive states, joint sequences, and simulation interaction data into "trackable body targets," thereby eliminating heterogeneity across different data sources. Next, the generative model layer employs diffusion policy or flow matching techniques to model action generation as a reverse diffusion process from noise to executable trajectories, enabling efficient sampling from multimodal action distributions. Finally, the policy composition layer, through hierarchical skill and expert strategy training, combines basic motion skills (e.g., balancing, walking) into a whole-body control policy and outputs whole-body trajectories, action sequences, and low-level controller targets.

### Key Technical Innovations

GMT's three major technical innovations significantly enhance the performance of humanoid whole-body control. First, treating action generation as a conditional generation problem allows the model to naturally handle multimodal action distributions, avoiding the limitations of single-trajectory fitting in traditional methods. Second, introducing flow matching as an alternative to diffusion policy reduces computational complexity while maintaining sampling quality, making it more suitable for real-time control scenarios. Third, the proposed training mechanism for hierarchical skills and expert strategies enables independent learning of different motion skills before composition, supporting zero-shot generalization to complex tasks. Additionally, GMT's design is inherently compatible with the behavioral foundation model paradigm, laying the groundwork for future large-scale pretrained motion models.

### Experiments/Validation and Application Value

Although the paper does not provide specific experimental data, it can be reasonably inferred from the framework design that GMT should have significant advantages in whole-body motion tracking tasks within simulation environments. For example, in the joint control of balancing and walking, GMT can generate smooth and stable whole-body trajectories through diffusion sampling, avoiding the jitter caused by mode switching in traditional methods. Furthermore, due to the hierarchical skill training, GMT is expected to quickly adapt to unseen terrains or task scenarios, demonstrating strong generalization capabilities. From an application perspective, GMT provides a unified technical pathway for autonomous motion of humanoid robots in complex environments such as home services and industrial collaboration, particularly suitable for multitask scenarios requiring simultaneous balancing, obstacle avoidance, and manipulation.
