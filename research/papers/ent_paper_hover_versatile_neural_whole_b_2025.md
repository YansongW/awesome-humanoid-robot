## 概述

人形机器人在复杂动态环境中实现全身协调控制，长期面临感知-运动耦合、多任务兼容性与实时部署之间的核心矛盾。本文提出的 HOVER 框架，通过构建基于教师-学生知识蒸馏的神经全身控制器，首次将场景恢复、目标表征与运动生成统一在可部署的观测空间中。该方法在不依赖精确动力学模型的前提下，实现了平衡、运动跟踪与全身轨迹规划等多技能的协同执行，为人形机器人从实验室走向真实场景提供了可扩展的控制范式。

## 核心内容

人形机器人的全身控制长期受困于两大挑战：一是高维关节空间与复杂接触动力学带来的建模困难，二是不同任务（如行走、抓取、姿态调整）对控制策略的差异化需求。传统基于模型预测控制（MPC）或全身控制器（WBC）的方法虽能保证稳定性，但依赖精确的系统辨识与实时优化求解，难以适应非结构化环境。近年来，强化学习与模仿学习虽在特定技能上取得突破，但多技能融合与零样本泛化仍是未解难题。HOVER 正是在这一背景下，尝试构建一个能够同时处理平衡、运动跟踪与全身动作生成的统一神经控制框架。

HOVER 的核心框架采用分层架构，分为高层策略与低层控制器两个模块。高层策略首先从本体状态与关节序列中恢复场景、目标或运动表征，这一过程通过教师策略在特权信息（如地面反力、全局位姿）下训练完成。教师策略利用强化学习在仿真环境中学习全身轨迹与动作序列的生成能力，其输出作为低层控制器的参考目标。低层控制器则基于 MPC 或 WBC 方法，将高层策略生成的参考轨迹转化为实时的关节力矩指令，从而兼顾运动规划的最优性与底层执行的鲁棒性。

该方法的关键技术创新在于知识蒸馏机制：教师策略在训练时拥有完整的特权信息，能够学习到最优的全身协调策略；而学生策略仅依赖部署时可获得的观测（如关节角度、IMU 数据），通过模仿教师策略的输出分布来继承其能力。这一设计使得学生策略在推理时无需依赖昂贵的传感器或环境模型，显著降低了部署门槛。此外，HOVER 通过分层技能库（hierarchical skills）将平衡、运动跟踪与全身动作分解为可复用的专家策略，不同技能之间通过共享表征实现平滑切换，从而支持多任务协同。

实验验证方面，HOVER 在仿真环境与真实人形机器人平台上进行了系统性评估。在仿真中，该方法在平衡保持、目标跟踪与全身运动生成等任务上均优于基线方法（如纯 MPC 或单任务强化学习策略），尤其在面对未见过地形与外力扰动时展现出更强的泛化能力。在真实机器人部署中，学生策略能够以 1 kHz 的控制频率实时运行，成功实现了在复杂地面上的稳定行走与全身姿态调整。这些结果验证了知识蒸馏与分层控制相结合的有效性，为人形机器人在家庭服务、工业巡检等场景中的实际应用提供了技术支撑。

HOVER 的应用价值体现在其通用性与可扩展性。由于学生策略仅依赖低维本体观测，该方法可适配不同构型的人形机器人硬件，无需重新训练。同时，分层技能库的设计允许开发者通过添加新的专家策略来扩展机器人的行为能力，例如增加抓取或避障技能。这一框架有望推动人形机器人从单一任务执行向多技能融合的“行为基础模型”演进，为未来通用机器人控制系统的构建奠定基础。

## Overview

Humanoid robots face a core contradiction in achieving whole-body coordinated control in complex dynamic environments: the coupling between perception and motion, the compatibility of multiple tasks, and real-time deployment. The HOVER framework proposed in this paper, by constructing a neural whole-body controller based on teacher-student knowledge distillation, unifies scene recovery, target representation, and motion generation within a deployable observation space for the first time. Without relying on precise dynamic models, this method achieves the coordinated execution of multiple skills such as balance, motion tracking, and whole-body trajectory planning, providing a scalable control paradigm for humanoid robots transitioning from laboratories to real-world scenarios.

## Content

Whole-body control of humanoid robots has long been plagued by two major challenges: first, the difficulty of modeling high-dimensional joint spaces and complex contact dynamics; second, the differentiated requirements of various tasks (e.g., walking, grasping, posture adjustment) for control strategies. Traditional methods based on Model Predictive Control (MPC) or Whole-Body Control (WBC) can ensure stability but rely on accurate system identification and real-time optimization, making them difficult to adapt to unstructured environments. In recent years, while reinforcement learning and imitation learning have achieved breakthroughs in specific skills, multi-skill integration and zero-shot generalization remain unresolved issues. Against this backdrop, HOVER attempts to construct a unified neural control framework capable of simultaneously handling balance, motion tracking, and whole-body action generation.

The core framework of HOVER adopts a hierarchical architecture, divided into two modules: a high-level policy and a low-level controller. The high-level policy first recovers scene, target, or motion representations from proprioceptive states and joint sequences, a process trained by a teacher policy under privileged information (e.g., ground reaction forces, global poses). The teacher policy uses reinforcement learning to learn the generation of whole-body trajectories and action sequences in a simulated environment, with its output serving as the reference target for the low-level controller. The low-level controller, based on MPC or WBC methods, converts the reference trajectories generated by the high-level policy into real-time joint torque commands, thereby balancing the optimality of motion planning with the robustness of low-level execution.

The key technological innovation of this method lies in the knowledge distillation mechanism: the teacher policy, trained with complete privileged information, learns optimal whole-body coordination strategies; the student policy, relying only on observations available during deployment (e.g., joint angles, IMU data), inherits these capabilities by imitating the output distribution of the teacher policy. This design allows the student policy to operate without expensive sensors or environmental models during inference, significantly lowering the deployment threshold. Additionally, HOVER decomposes balance, motion tracking, and whole-body actions into reusable expert policies through a hierarchical skill library, enabling smooth transitions between different skills via shared representations, thus supporting multi-task coordination.

In terms of experimental validation, HOVER was systematically evaluated in both simulated environments and on real humanoid robot platforms. In simulations, the method outperformed baseline approaches (e.g., pure MPC or single-task reinforcement learning policies) in tasks such as balance maintenance, target tracking, and whole-body motion generation, particularly demonstrating stronger generalization capabilities when facing unseen terrains and external disturbances. In real robot deployment, the student policy operated in real time at a control frequency of 1 kHz, successfully achieving stable walking and whole-body posture adjustments on complex terrains. These results validate the effectiveness of combining knowledge distillation with hierarchical control, providing technical support for the practical application of humanoid robots in scenarios such as domestic service and industrial inspection.

The application value of HOVER lies in its generality and scalability. Since the student policy relies only on low-dimensional proprioceptive observations, this method can adapt to different humanoid robot hardware configurations without retraining. Meanwhile, the design of the hierarchical skill library allows developers to expand the robot's behavioral capabilities by adding new expert policies, such as grasping or obstacle avoidance skills. This framework is expected to drive the evolution of humanoid robots from single-task execution to multi-skill integration, forming a "behavioral foundation model" and laying the groundwork for future general-purpose robot control systems.
