## Overview
Humanoid robots performing complex tasks in unstructured environments have long been plagued by the "sim-to-real" gap when transferring simulation-trained policies to the real world. Existing methods typically rely on precise dynamics modeling or extensive real-world data collection, making it difficult to balance generalization and real-time performance. MOSAIC proposes a universal framework based on fast residual adaptation. By constructing a data closed loop, it unifies human teleoperation data, simulation interaction data, and proprioceptive state sequences into trainable whole-body trajectories, thereby significantly reducing dependence on real-world environmental data while maintaining motion tracking accuracy. The core contribution of this work lies in redefining action generation as a conditional generation problem and leveraging diffusion policies and flow matching techniques to sample executable trajectories from multimodal action distributions, providing a reusable foundation model paradigm for general motion tracking and remote operation of humanoid robots.

## Content
Research Background and Problem: Whole-body motion control of humanoid robots faces dual challenges. On one hand, policies from simulation environments are difficult to deploy directly on real robots due to non-negligible deviations in dynamics parameters, friction coefficients, and contact models. On the other hand, human demonstration data collected via teleoperation or exoskeletons is often sparse and noisy, failing to cover the full state space required for robot motion. Existing methods either rely on handcrafted motion priors or require extensive real-world robot trial-and-error, leading to sharp performance degradation when encountering unseen terrains or load variations. The core problem MOSAIC addresses is how to construct a data closed loop that enables efficient information flow and mutual enhancement among simulation training, human demonstration, and real-world deployment.

Method or Model Framework: The overall framework of MOSAIC comprises three key data streams: proprioceptive states and joint sequences provide the robot's current motion primitives; teleoperation and exoskeleton data capture human operation intentions; and simulation interaction data generates a large number of low-cost exploration samples. These three types of data are uniformly fed into a generative model based on diffusion policies or flow matching, which treats action generation as a conditional generation problem—given the current state and task goal, the model samples executable whole-body trajectories from a multimodal action distribution. Unlike traditional deterministic policies, this generative approach naturally handles the multiplicity of motion solutions, such as smoothly switching between different gaits. The generated trajectories are then converted into targets for low-level controllers, achieving an end-to-end mapping from high-level planning to joint torques.

Key Technical Innovations: MOSAIC achieves three key breakthroughs at the technical level. First, it introduces a "fast residual adaptation" mechanism, allowing the robot to fine-tune the output residuals of the generative model using only a small amount of real-world data (e.g., a few walking steps) during deployment, thereby quickly compensating for sim-to-real differences without retraining the entire network. Second, by modeling action generation as a conditional diffusion process, the model explicitly learns the multimodal characteristics of action distributions, avoiding the degradation issues of traditional regression methods when encountering out-of-distribution states. Third, the framework designs a unified data representation format, enabling heterogeneous data from different sources (simulation, teleoperation, exoskeleton) to be mixed for training, significantly improving data utilization efficiency. This design makes MOSAIC not only applicable to a single robot platform but also transferable as a "behavioral foundation model" to humanoid robots with different configurations.

Experiments/Validation and Application Value: Although the paper does not disclose specific quantitative experimental data, it can be inferred from its project page and domain tags (design engineering and AI algorithms) that MOSAIC has been validated in both simulation environments and real robots. Typical evaluation scenarios include dynamic walking on complex terrains, disturbance-resistant balance recovery, and teleoperated grasping and manipulation tasks. Compared with baseline methods, MOSAIC's advantages are expected to manifest in three aspects: lower sim-to-real transfer cost (requiring only a few residual adaptation samples), higher motion tracking accuracy (thanks to the distribution modeling capability of diffusion policies), and stronger task generalization (ability to reuse pre-trained whole-body trajectory libraries). This work has direct application value for scenarios such as industrial inspection and home service, where humanoid robots need to quickly adapt to new environments, while also providing a scalable technical pathway for building general humanoid robot motion intelligence.
