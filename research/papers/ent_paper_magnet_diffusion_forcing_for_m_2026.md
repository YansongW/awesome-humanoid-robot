## Overview

For humanoid robots to coexist naturally with humans, they must be capable of understanding and generating multi-person, long-term interactive actions. However, existing motion generation models are often limited to single-person short sequences and struggle to capture the complex causal dependencies and spatiotemporal consistency among multiple agents. The MAGNet model proposed in this paper innovatively introduces the "Diffusion Forcing" mechanism into the autoregressive generation framework, achieving high-quality modeling and generation of multi-person long-term interactive sequences for the first time. This work provides a unified and scalable sequence modeling paradigm for tasks such as human-robot collaboration and group behavior simulation in robotics.

## Content

(a) Research Background and Problem  
In robotics and computer graphics, human motion generation has always been a core topic, but most research focuses on single-person, short-term, non-interactive scenarios. As humanoid robots expand into complex environments such as homes, services, and collaboration, robots need to simultaneously perceive and predict the long-term interactive behaviors of multiple people, such as two people cooperating to carry objects, group dancing, or social gestures. Such tasks face two major challenges: first, nonlinear causal dependencies exist among multi-agent actions; second, long-term sequences are prone to error accumulation and mode collapse. Existing methods based on Transformers or diffusion models either cannot handle variable-length interaction histories or lose multi-agent consistency in long-range generation.

(b) Method or Model Framework  
The core idea of MAGNet is to combine "Diffusion Forcing" with autoregressive generation. Diffusion Forcing is a novel scheduling strategy for the diffusion process that allows the model to dynamically adjust noise injection and denoising steps at each generation step based on the current context. This enables flexible control over long-term dependencies and short-term details of the generated sequence while maintaining an autoregressive structure. Specifically, MAGNet uses a Transformer-based backbone network to encode the historical action sequences of multiple agents into latent states, and then gradually generates the joint actions of all agents at the next moment through the Diffusion Forcing module. This framework naturally supports a variable number of agents and variable-length interaction histories.

(c) Key Technical Innovations  
The main innovations of MAGNet include: First, it applies Diffusion Forcing to multi-agent interactive sequence modeling for the first time, solving the problem of error accumulation in traditional autoregressive models during long-range generation while avoiding the limitation of pure diffusion models that cannot handle variable-length sequences. Second, it proposes a "joint denoising" mechanism that allows the model to simultaneously consider the current states and historical interactions of all agents when generating each step, thereby ensuring spatial consistency and temporal coherence of multi-agent actions. Third, by introducing conditional noise scheduling, MAGNet can adaptively adjust the number of diffusion steps during training based on interaction complexity, significantly improving the model's generalization ability for sparse and dense interaction scenarios.

(d) Experiments/Validation and Application Value  
Although the paper does not provide specific experimental data, based on its research paradigm, it is likely that MAGNet has been validated on public multi-person motion datasets (such as CMU Mocap, AMASS, or custom multi-person interaction datasets).
