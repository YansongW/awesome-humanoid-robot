Its main contribution lies in constructing a unified framework that can convert heterogeneous inputs—such as proprioceptive states, joint sequences, human videos or motion capture trajectories, contact forces, and tactile signals—into trackable body targets, ultimately outputting joint commands and whole-body action sequences. Whole-body control of humanoid robots has long faced two major challenges: first, how to effectively fuse heterogeneous information from multimodal sensors such as vision, force, and touch; second, how to generate stable, physically constrained whole-body motions in highly dynamic environments. Traditional methods often rely on handcrafted motion templates or simplified dynamic models, which struggle to handle the complexity of unstructured terrain and diverse interaction scenarios in the real world. The motivation behind Mobile-TeleVision stems from this bottleneck, aiming to endow robots with the ability to learn motion priors from human demonstrations through data-driven generative methods, thereby enhancing the robustness and generalization of whole-body control.

The core architecture of this framework revolves around the "conditional generation" paradigm. Subsequently, through strategies like reinforcement learning (e.g., PPO), adversarial motion priors (AMP), diffusion policies, or flow matching training, it performs conditional sampling from multimodal action distributions constructed from historical data. This design enables the robot to generate whole-body trajectories and joint torque commands in real time that are physically feasible and task-relevant, based on the current perceived state.

Third, the framework supports extracting motion priors from low-cost data sources such as human videos, significantly reducing reliance on expensive motion capture equipment and providing a feasible solution for large-scale data collection and training of humanoid robots.
