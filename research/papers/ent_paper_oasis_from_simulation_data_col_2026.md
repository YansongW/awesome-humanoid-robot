## Overview

Humanoid robots performing mobile manipulation tasks in unstructured environments have long faced challenges related to the data gap in simulation-to-reality transfer and skill generalization. Existing methods often rely on large amounts of human teleoperation data or manually designed control rules, making it difficult to achieve reusable and scalable closed-loop learning. OASIS proposes a complete data closed-loop framework from simulation data collection to real-world deployment. By integrating multi-view visual observations, proprioceptive states, and joint sequences, combined with behavior cloning and hierarchical expert strategies, it transforms human operation data into trainable and routable executable action commands, significantly enhancing the autonomous mobile manipulation capabilities of humanoid robots in complex scenarios.

## Content

### Research Background and Problem

Humanoid robot loco-manipulation requires simultaneous navigation, grasping, and object manipulation in dynamic environments, imposing high demands on perception, planning, and control. Current research faces two major bottlenecks: first, the high cost of acquiring high-quality operation data, making teleoperation or exoskeleton collection difficult to scale; second, models trained in simulation often degrade in real-world performance, i.e., the "sim-to-real" transfer gap. The core problem of OASIS is how to construct an efficient data closed-loop that enables simulation data to be effectively collected, transformed, and reused for skill execution in the real world, while ensuring policy robustness and generalization.

### Method or Model Framework

The OASIS framework centers on a data closed-loop, comprising three core stages: data collection, policy learning, and skill execution. In the data collection stage, the system uses camera images, multi-view observations, proprioceptive states, and joint sequences, combined with teleoperation or exoskeleton devices, to synchronously record human operation commands and robot state trajectories. These multimodal data are then fed into behavior cloning (ACT) and imitation learning modules to learn the mapping from observations to actions in an end-to-end manner. To enhance policy reusability, OASIS decomposes complex tasks into a series of routable "skills" or "expert policies," each corresponding to a subtask (e.g., walking, grasping, placing), which are dynamically selected and combined by a high-level module based on scene context.

### Key Technical Innovations

The technical innovations of OASIS are mainly reflected in three aspects. First, it proposes a hierarchical skill routing mechanism that allows the high-level decision module to select the most appropriate expert policy in real time based on visual perception results, thereby avoiding the failure of traditional single policies during task switching. Second, in the data transformation phase, OASIS designs a standardized pipeline from raw teleoperation trajectories to executable action commands, enabling the same dataset to be reused across different robot platforms, significantly reducing the repetitive cost of data collection. Third, the framework integrates scene understanding with vision-guided control, leveraging multi-view observation information to enhance perception robustness for object positions, obstacle distribution, and terrain changes, providing key support for simulation-to-reality transfer.

### Experiments/Validation and Application Value

Although the paper does not disclose specific experimental data, based on its framework design, it can be inferred that OASIS has been validated in both simulation environments and real humanoid robot platforms. Typical application scenarios include: completing item transport and stacking in warehouse environments, and performing tasks such as opening doors and retrieving objects in home environments. By comparing with pure teleoperation baselines, single-policy imitation learning, and end-to-end methods without hierarchical routing, OASIS is expected to achieve significant improvements in task success rate, data utilization efficiency, and cross-scenario generalization capability. The value of this framework lies in providing a standardized path from simulation data collection to real-world deployment for humanoid robots, particularly suitable for complex mobile manipulation scenarios requiring frequent subtask switching, and holds significant reference value for fields such as industrial logistics, home services, and disaster rescue.
