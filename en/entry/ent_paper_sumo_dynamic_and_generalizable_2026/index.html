Summary

Sumo transforms the body state, joint sequence, and simulation interaction data into a trackable body target, and finally outputs a whole-body trajectory/action sequence and a low-level controller target through diffusion strategy/flow matching, whole-body controller/WBC/MPC, hierarchical skill/expert strategy training, or combined whole-body strategies. The key point is to view action generation as a conditional generation problem and use diffusion or flow matching to sample executable trajectories in a multimodal action distribution.

Overview

Humanoid robots face two major challenges when performing whole-body mobile manipulation tasks in complex environments: the multimodality of motion generation and dynamic adaptability. Existing methods often treat manipulation and locomotion separately, making real-time whole-body coordination difficult. Sumo proposes a unified framework that treats motion generation as a conditional generation problem. By employing diffusion policies or flow matching methods, it samples executable trajectories from multimodal action distributions, enabling dynamic and generalizable whole-body mobile manipulation. This work introduces a new paradigm for whole-body control of robots, balancing policy generalization with execution efficiency.

Content

(a) Research Background and Problem: Whole-body loco-manipulation for humanoid robots requires simultaneous coordination of upper-body manipulation, lower-body locomotion, and torso balance, representing a typical high-dimensional, strongly coupled control problem. Traditional methods often rely on handcrafted motion primitives or hierarchical control structures, making them difficult to adapt to dynamic changes in unstructured environments. Moreover, due to the multimodality of humanoid robot action spaces—where the same task objective may correspond to multiple feasible motion strategies—efficiently sampling from these distributions and generating executable whole-body trajectories becomes a key bottleneck limiting system practicality. Existing reinforcement learning and model predictive control (MPC) methods often struggle to balance generalization and computational efficiency.

(b) Method or Model Framework: Sumo constructs an end-to-end whole-body control pipeline. This framework first converts proprioceptive states, joint sequences, and simulation interaction data into trackable body targets. Then, using diffusion policies or flow matching techniques, it models action generation as a conditional generation problem. Specifically, the system learns the latent structure of multimodal action distributions during training, and during inference, it samples complete whole-body trajectories or action sequences from this distribution based on current observation conditions. Finally, these trajectories are transformed into low-level control commands via a whole-body controller (WBC) or model predictive controller (MPC), enabling closed-loop interaction with the physical environment.

(c) Key Technical Innovations: The core innovation of Sumo lies in introducing diffusion models and flow matching to the field of whole-body robot control. Unlike traditional methods that directly output deterministic actions, Sumo leverages the stochastic sampling capability of generative models to naturally handle the multimodality of action distributions, allowing the robot to exhibit diverse motion strategies for the same task. Additionally, the framework supports hierarchical skill/expert strategy training and combined whole-body strategies, enabling complex tasks to be decomposed into reusable sub-skills and flexibly combined through conditional generation mechanisms. This design significantly enhances policy generalization, allowing the robot to adapt to unseen objects, terrains, or task variants.

(d) Experiments/Validation or Application Value: Although the paper does not disclose specific numerical results, based on its technical approach, it can be inferred that Sumo is suitable for typical scenarios requiring whole-body coordination, such as humanoid robots carrying heavy objects, maintaining balance while crossing obstacles, or performing composite tasks like simultaneous upper-body grasping and lower-body locomotion. By combining action generation with diffusion models, this method is expected to produce smoother and more natural motion trajectories than traditional MPC in both simulation environments and real robot platforms, while offering stronger zero-shot generalization capabilities than pure reinforcement learning methods. This work holds significant reference value for advancing the practical deployment of humanoid robots in fields such as service, manufacturing, and rescue, particularly for high-difficulty manipulation tasks requiring dynamic adaptation to environmental changes.
