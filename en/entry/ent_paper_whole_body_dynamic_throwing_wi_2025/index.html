Overview

This study focuses on motion generation for legged manipulators in whole-body dynamic throwing tasks. Existing methods often rely on predefined motion templates or single-modal data, making it difficult to handle multi-modal action distributions in complex environments. This work proposes treating motion generation as a conditional generation problem, using diffusion policies or flow matching to recover scene, target, or motion representations from proprioceptive states, joint sequences, human video/motion capture trajectories, and simulation interaction data, thereby sampling executable whole-body trajectories and action sequences. Its main contribution lies in constructing a unified data-driven framework capable of fusing multi-source heterogeneous data to generate whole-body throwing actions that are both dynamic and adaptive.

Content

(a) Research Background and Problem: Legged manipulators combine mobility and manipulation capabilities, offering broad application prospects in fields such as logistics, rescue, and service. However, whole-body dynamic throwing tasks impose extremely high demands on robot coordination, real-time performance, and robustness. Traditional methods typically rely on handcrafted motion planners or physics-based optimization, which struggle to handle multi-modal action distributions in unstructured environments—for example, the same throwing target may correspond to multiple feasible body poses and joint trajectories. Additionally, human throwing demonstration data (e.g., videos, motion capture) and simulation data differ in representation space. Effectively fusing these heterogeneous data to generate transferable robot actions is a key bottleneck in current research.

(b) Method or Model Framework: This work proposes a whole-body action generation framework based on a conditional generation paradigm. First, unified scene, target, or motion representations are extracted from three data sources: proprioceptive states and joint sequences, human video/motion capture trajectories, and simulation interaction data. These representations serve as conditional information input into diffusion policies or flow matching models. Diffusion policies generate action sequences from random noise through a gradual denoising process, while flow matching samples trajectories by learning a continuous probability flow. Both effectively model multi-modal action distributions, enabling the generated throwing actions to satisfy dynamic constraints while exhibiting diversity and naturalness.

(c) Key Technological Innovations: The core innovation of this study lies in redefining the action generation problem as a conditional generation problem and using diffusion models or flow matching to sample feasible trajectories from multi-modal distributions. Compared to traditional single-modal regression or reinforcement learning methods, this framework naturally integrates representation information from different data sources, such as intent and style extracted from human videos and physical feasibility constraints obtained from simulations. Furthermore, by introducing a "conditional generation" mechanism, the model can dynamically adjust outputs based on different scenes, targets, or proprioceptive states, achieving flexible control over dimensions such as throwing distance, angle, and force. This approach provides a scalable solution for whole-body coordinated control of legged manipulators.

(d) Experiment/Validation or Application Value: Although the paper does not provide specific experimental data, it can be inferred from its methodology that the framework can be validated in simulation environments (e.g., MuJoCo or Isaac Gym), comparing with baseline methods (such as optimization-based trajectory planning or imitation learning) in terms of throwing success rate, action diversity, and computational efficiency. In practical applications, this technology can empower legged manipulators to throw packages in warehouses, toss rescue equipment in disaster scenarios, or perform throwing-based interaction tasks in service settings. Its data-driven nature also means that as more high-quality data accumulates, model performance can continuously improve, laying the foundation for the next generation of whole-body dynamic manipulation robots.
