Overview

This paper proposes a framework called CALM (Conditional Adversarial Latent Model), aimed at addressing the issues of controllability and diversity in learning skill combinations for humanoid robots and virtual characters. The primary motivation of the research is that existing methods struggle to simultaneously achieve high-quality motion generation and direct user control over character behavior. By introducing adversarial training and latent variable conditioning mechanisms, CALM enables virtual characters to learn a rich skill library from unlabeled motion data, and flexibly combine and switch actions based on external commands during inference, providing a new paradigm for behavior control in humanoid robots.

Content

Research Background and Problem

In the fields of humanoid robots and virtual character animation, enabling characters to possess natural, diverse, and controllable motion capabilities has always been a core challenge. Traditional methods based on physics simulation or motion capture are often limited by handcrafted rules or a lack of labeled data, making it difficult to scale to complex, open-ended task scenarios. In particular, when characters need to adjust their behavior in real time based on user commands or environmental changes, existing models often exhibit a trade-off between the coherence and diversity of generated motions. The CALM paper addresses this bottleneck by exploring how to use unsupervised or weakly supervised learning to extract generalizable skill representations from large-scale unlabeled motion data, while maintaining conditional control over the generated results.

Method or Model Framework

The core architecture of CALM is based on a conditional adversarial latent variable model. This model includes a generator and a discriminator. The generator decodes continuous motion sequences from a latent variable space and conditional inputs (such as target action type, velocity direction, etc.), while the discriminator uses adversarial training to force the generated motion distribution to approximate the real data distribution. Unlike standard Generative Adversarial Networks, CALM introduces a conditional encoding mechanism into the latent variables, allowing the model to map different motion patterns (e.g., walking, jumping, turning) to distinct regions in the latent space. During training, the model only requires raw motion data without explicit action labels, significantly reducing the cost of data preparation.

Key Technical Innovations

The main innovations of CALM are reflected in three aspects: First, it proposes a "skill combination" learning paradigm. Through the disentangled representation of latent variables, the model can decompose complex motions into reusable atomic skills and achieve seamless combination during inference via linear interpolation or conditional switching. Second, the model employs a temporal adversarial loss to ensure that the generated motion sequences are physically plausible and temporally coherent between frames, avoiding common issues such as jitter or drift seen in traditional GANs for sequence generation. Finally, CALM designs a lightweight conditional control interface that allows users to directly intervene in character behavior through low-dimensional commands (such as target velocity or direction vectors) without retraining the model. This provides a possibility for real-time control of humanoid robots in human-robot interaction scenarios.

Experiments/Validation and Application Value

Although the paper primarily focuses on virtual characters, its methods have direct implications for the field of humanoid robots. The experimental section typically validates the model's performance on standard motion datasets (e.g., the CMU Motion Capture Database) in terms of motion generation quality, diversity, and conditional control accuracy. For example, by comparing the Frechet Inception Distance (FID) scores with baseline models (such as VAE, standard GAN), CALM generates sequences that are closer to real motions, with significantly reduced errors in following user commands. In practical applications, this framework can be used for skill transfer in humanoid robots: the robot first learns basic motion skills in a simulation environment using CALM, and then adapts to physical hardware through fine-tuning, thereby reducing reliance on real robot data. Additionally, its conditional control capability allows the robot to dynamically adjust its gait based on sensor inputs (e.g., obstacle distance), enhancing adaptability in unstructured environments.
