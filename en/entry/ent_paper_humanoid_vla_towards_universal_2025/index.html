Summary

The implementation path of Humanoid-VLA is to first encode camera images/multi-view observations, human videos/motion capture trajectories into multimodal representations, and then use diffusion strategy/flow matching, VLA multimodal action model, and whole-body controller/WBC/MPC to predict whole-body trajectories/action sequences. The key point is to preserve the semantic understanding of VLM while adding robot states and action heads to avoid staying only at language planning.

Overview

Humanoid whole-body control faces two major challenges: how to extract high-level semantic understanding from visual input, and how to translate this understanding into precise whole-body motion commands. Existing methods often use Vision-Language Models (VLMs) for task planning while handling motion control independently, leading to a gap between semantic understanding and motion execution. Humanoid-VLA proposes a unified Vision-Language-Action framework that encodes multi-view camera observations, human demonstration videos, and motion capture trajectories into multimodal representations, and leverages diffusion policies with whole-body controllers to generate continuous action sequences, achieving end-to-end integration from semantic understanding to whole-body control. The core contribution of this work lies in preserving the semantic reasoning capability of VLM while introducing robot state encoding and action prediction heads, enabling the model to directly output executable motion commands, thereby breaking through the limitation of traditional methods that remain at the language planning level.

Content

The general control of humanoid robots has long been constrained by two independent research lines: one is high-level task planning based on Vision-Language Models, and the other is low-level motion generation based on Model Predictive Control (MPC) or Whole-Body Controllers (WBC). The former excels at understanding natural language instructions and scene semantics but cannot output fine-grained joint trajectories; the latter achieves precise whole-body dynamics control but lacks semantic understanding of task intent. This disconnection makes it difficult for humanoid robots to simultaneously handle task understanding and motion execution in complex environments, especially in mobile manipulation scenarios requiring visual closed-loop adjustments, where systems often need manually designed intermediate interfaces to bridge planning and control modules.

Humanoid-VLA proposes a three-stage processing architecture to bridge this gap. First, the model uniformly encodes heterogeneous data from camera images, multi-view observations, human demonstration videos, and motion capture trajectories into multimodal representations, leveraging the semantic encoding capability of pretrained Vision-Language Models. Second, based on diffusion policies or flow matching methods, the model learns the conditional probability distribution from visual input to whole-body trajectories in the representation space, thereby generating smooth action sequences that satisfy dynamics constraints. Finally, the generated trajectories are fed into a whole-body controller (WBC/MPC) for low-level execution, ensuring that motions meet joint limits, torque constraints, and balance conditions.

The key technological innovations of this framework are reflected in two aspects. First, the model adds a robot state encoder and action prediction head on top of the VLM, allowing semantic understanding and motion generation to share the same representation space, avoiding the information loss in traditional "plan-then-control" approaches. Second, by introducing a contact planning mechanism, the model can dynamically predict foot-ground contact patterns based on visual input, enabling stable gait transitions in mobile manipulation tasks. This design allows Humanoid-VLA to simultaneously handle task planning, motion generation, and contact scheduling without explicitly defining intermediate state machines.

In terms of validation, Humanoid-VLA was evaluated on multiple mobile manipulation benchmark tasks, including object grasping, environment navigation, and tool use scenarios. Experimental results show that the framework outperforms separated planning-control baseline methods in both task success rate and motion smoothness. Particularly, in tasks requiring visual closed-loop adjustments, Humanoid-VLA can leverage real-time visual feedback to correct whole-body trajectories, demonstrating robustness to dynamic environmental changes. Furthermore, ablation experiments confirm that retaining VLM semantic understanding capability is crucial for handling long-horizon tasks and language instruction generalization, while adding action heads significantly improves motion generation precision.

From an application perspective, Humanoid-VLA provides a feasible technical pathway for deploying humanoid robots in scenarios such as industrial assembly, home services, and disaster rescue. Its unified framework reduces system integration complexity, allowing developers to avoid designing separate planning and control interfaces for each task. Additionally, since the model can directly learn motion patterns from human demonstration videos, this opens new possibilities for rapid skill transfer and generalization in robotics. Future work could further explore extending this framework to more complex multi-robot collaboration scenarios and improving the model's ability to predict fine-grained contact forces while maintaining real-time performance.
