Overview

This paper proposes a motion generation framework for humanoid robot characters—the Robot Motion Diffusion Model—aiming to address the challenge of efficiently sampling executable trajectories from multimodal action distributions in traditional motion planning methods. The main contribution of this research is to formalize action generation as a conditional generation problem, utilizing diffusion models or flow matching techniques to recover scene and motion representations from proprioceptive states, joint sequences, simulation interaction data, and contact force/tactile signals, thereby generating whole-body trajectories and action sequences. This work provides a new paradigm for robot motion control that combines flexibility and robustness, with the potential to enhance the behavior generation capabilities of humanoid robots in complex dynamic environments.

Content

(a) Research Background and Problem: Motion generation for humanoid robots has long faced two major challenges: first, the high dimensionality and multimodality of the action space make it difficult for traditional rule-based or optimization methods to cover all feasible trajectories; second, the introduction of physical feedback such as contact forces and tactile signals in the real world further increases the complexity of motion planning. Existing methods, such as reinforcement learning strategies like PPO or behavior cloning techniques like ACT, can achieve good results in specific tasks but are often limited by the generalization ability of a single strategy, making it difficult to generate coherent and physically plausible whole-body motions in varying scenarios. Therefore, constructing a unified framework capable of learning from multi-source perceptual information and sampling diverse executable trajectories has become a key issue in this field.

(b) Method or Model Framework: The Robot Motion Diffusion Model proposed in this paper adopts a conditional generation model as its core architecture. First, the system recovers scene, goal, or motion representations from proprioceptive states and joint sequences, simulation interaction data, and contact force/tactile signals, thereby establishing a unified understanding of the environment and the robot's own state. Subsequently, the model treats action generation as a conditional generation problem, using a diffusion process or flow matching technique to progressively denoise within a conditional distribution defined by historical states and task goals, ultimately sampling a complete whole-body trajectory or action sequence. This framework is compatible with multiple training paradigms, including PPO/RL policy training, ACT/behavior cloning imitation learning, and diffusion policy/flow matching, enabling flexible switching from data-driven approaches to policy optimization.

(c) Key Technological Innovations: The core innovation of this research lies in introducing diffusion models to the field of robot motion generation and adapting them to the characteristics of humanoid robots. Specifically, the model can efficiently sample from multimodal action distributions through a conditional diffusion process, avoiding the issue of action singularity caused by mode collapse in traditional methods. Additionally, the introduction of flow matching techniques further improves sampling efficiency and trajectory smoothness, making the generated action sequences more executable in physical simulations. Moreover, the explicit modeling of contact forces and tactile signals enables the model to better handle critical aspects such as foot-ground interaction, enhancing the physical plausibility of motion generation.

(d) Experiment/Validation or Application Value: Although the paper does not provide specific experimental data, it can be inferred from its methodological framework that the model is applicable to various humanoid robot motion generation scenarios, such as walking, grasping, and whole-body coordinated manipulation. By combining diffusion policies with imitation learning, the model can generate diverse motion trajectories in simulation environments and is expected to transfer to physical robot platforms. Its application value is reflected in two aspects: on one hand, it provides an efficient motion generation tool for robot character animation and interactive tasks; on the other hand, it lays a generative foundation for subsequent research in directions such as language control and trajectory planning (e.g., language_control and trajectory_planning mentioned in the keywords). This work holds significant reference value for advancing humanoid robots from preset motion libraries to adaptive behavior generation.
