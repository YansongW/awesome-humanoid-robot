Summary

HumanX first recovers scene, target, or motion representations from human videos/move-capture trajectories, and then generates actionable action commands using ACT/behavior cloning imitation learning, IK/action redirection, and hierarchical skills/expert strategies. The key is to break down tasks into routable skills or expert strategies, which are then selected and combined in execution using high-level modules.

Overview

HumanX proposes a novel framework for learning agile interaction skills for humanoid robots from human videos, aiming to address the bottleneck in current humanoid robot skill acquisition, which relies heavily on extensive manual programming and expensive motion capture data, hindering generalization to new scenarios. The core contribution of this work lies in constructing a complete data and policy pipeline from human demonstrations to executable robot actions: by recovering scene, object, and motion representations from videos or motion capture trajectories, and then combining imitation learning, inverse kinematics retargeting, and hierarchical skill policies, the robot can flexibly compose and execute complex interaction tasks. HumanX provides systematic methodological support for enhancing the autonomy and adaptability of humanoid robots in unstructured environments.

Content

The core challenge in current humanoid robot research is how to efficiently acquire and generalize interaction skills. Traditional methods either rely on manually designed motion primitives or require large amounts of precise motion capture data, leading to high skill library construction costs and poor transferability. Humans, on the other hand, can quickly learn new actions by observing videos, inspiring researchers to explore extracting robot-usable motion representations directly from human videos. However, differences in kinematic structure and dynamic characteristics between humans and robots, along with issues of intent and scene understanding implicit in videos, make this transformation process difficult. HumanX is specifically designed to bridge this "human-to-robot" skill transfer gap.

The HumanX framework comprises three key stages. First, the system recovers scene geometry, interaction targets, and human motion representations from human videos or motion capture trajectories, using computer vision and motion capture techniques to transform raw observations into structured information. Second, based on the recovered representations, it employs ACT (Action Chunking with Transformers) and behavioral cloning imitation learning to generate initial action commands, then uses inverse kinematics (IK) and action retargeting techniques to map human motions to the kinematic constraints of the humanoid robot, ensuring the generated actions are physically feasible. Finally, the system introduces hierarchical skills and expert policy mechanisms, decomposing complex tasks into a series of routable sub-skills (e.g., grasping, pushing/pulling, gait adjustment), with a high-level module dynamically selecting and combining these skills based on the current scene state to achieve flexible task execution.

The key technological innovations of HumanX are reflected in three aspects. First, it proposes an end-to-end trainable pipeline from video to robot actions, avoiding the tedious manual feature design of traditional methods. Second, by decomposing tasks into composable expert policies, the system gains generalization ability for new scenarios—even without seeing a complete task, it can adapt by recombining existing skill modules. Third, in the action retargeting stage, HumanX integrates kinematic constraints and physical plausibility checks, significantly reducing common issues like joint limit violations and self-collisions when directly transferring human motions. These designs enable HumanX to maintain motion agility while greatly enhancing skill transferability.

In experimental validation, HumanX was evaluated on various humanoid robot interaction tasks, including object grasping, tool use, and human-robot collaboration. Results show that compared to pure motion capture-driven baseline methods, HumanX achieves significant improvements in task success rates, especially in scenarios requiring rapid adaptation to new objects or layouts. Additionally, the system demonstrates compatibility with different humanoid robot platforms, verifying the generality of its skill representation and retargeting modules. From an application perspective, HumanX provides a low-cost, high-efficiency skill acquisition pathway for scenarios such as service robots, home assistants, and industrial collaboration—requiring only a small number of human videos to teach robots new interactive behaviors, greatly lowering the deployment barrier.
