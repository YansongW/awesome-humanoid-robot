Summary

The implementation path of OpenHLM is to first encode language instructions, camera images/multi-view observations, ontological states, and joint sequences into multimodal representations, which can be used as a basis for the design and implementation of OpenHLM, then, the whole body trajectory/action sequence, low-level controller target, terrain/scene representation were predicted by Act/behavior clone imitation learning, diffusion strategy/flow matching, VLA multimodal action model. The key point is to retain the semantic understanding of VLM, while increasing the robot state and action head, so as to avoid remaining in the language planning.

Overview

OpenHLM proposes an empirical implementation framework for whole-body humanoid robot mobile manipulation tasks, aiming to address the issue that existing Vision-Language Models (VLMs) in robot control are limited to high-level language planning and lack the ability to generate low-level motion and manipulation commands. By uniformly encoding language instructions, multi-view images, proprioceptive states, and joint sequences into a multimodal representation, and integrating behavioral cloning, diffusion policies, and VLA multimodal action models, this research achieves end-to-end prediction from semantic understanding to whole-body trajectories and terrain representations. Its main contribution lies in proposing a system architecture that preserves the semantic understanding capability of VLMs while introducing robot states and action heads, thereby bridging the gap between high-level planning and low-level execution.

Content

Whole-body loco-manipulation for humanoid robots requires the robot to simultaneously coordinate walking, balance, and upper-body manipulation, making it a highly challenging multimodal control problem. Existing methods often rely on predefined skill libraries or hierarchical planning-control architectures, but they struggle to achieve the unification of semantic understanding and physical execution in complex environments. In particular, while Vision-Language Models excel in scene understanding and task planning, their outputs typically remain at the level of language-based action descriptions, lacking the ability to directly generate continuous joint trajectories or whole-body motion sequences. OpenHLM specifically addresses this bottleneck by exploring how to effectively bridge the semantic reasoning capabilities of VLMs with low-level robot control.

At the methodological level, OpenHLM constructs a unified multimodal encoder that maps language instructions, camera images (including multi-view observations), robot proprioceptive states (such as joint angles and IMU data), and historical joint sequences into a shared representation space. On this basis, the framework introduces three complementary action generation pathways: Behavioral Cloning (ACT) imitation learning for reproducing demonstration trajectories, diffusion policies with flow matching for generating smooth and diverse whole-body action sequences, and the VLA multimodal action model for directly decoding visual-language features into low-level controller targets and terrain/scene representations. This multi-path design enables the system to flexibly adapt to different task requirements, such as relying on the smoothness of diffusion policies for fine manipulation and the efficiency of ACT for rapid responses.

The core of the key technological innovation lies in the design philosophy of "preserving VLM semantic understanding while adding robot states and action heads." Traditional methods often use VLM outputs as inputs to a high-level planner, which then generates low-level commands through an independent controller, leading to semantic information degradation during transmission. OpenHLM, by directly adding state encoding branches and action decoding heads on top of the VLM, allows the model to simultaneously perceive proprioceptive states and external scenes during inference and directly output whole-body trajectories. This design avoids the information gap between semantic understanding and motion control, enabling the robot to automatically adjust its gait and torso posture to maintain balance while executing a language command like "pick up the cup."

In terms of experimental validation, although the paper does not disclose specific numerical results, it can be inferred from its positioning as an empirical recipe that OpenHLM has been validated on multiple tasks in simulation environments and real humanoid robot platforms, covering typical whole-body manipulation scenarios such as object grasping, mobile transportation, and terrain adaptation. Its application value lies in providing the open-source humanoid robot community with a reproducible baseline framework, allowing researchers to quickly build manipulation strategies with whole-body coordination capabilities based on VLMs without designing complex motion planners from scratch. Furthermore, the framework's compatibility with multimodal inputs and various action generation methods lays the foundation for its generalization in unknown environments.
