Overview

In the field of whole-body motion control for humanoid robots, existing methods are often constrained by predefined motion templates or simplified dynamic models, making it difficult to simultaneously achieve robust balance maintenance and precise motion tracking in complex, variable physical interactions. This paper proposes a general framework called "Any2Track," whose core innovation lies in redefining whole-body motion generation as a conditional generation problem. By recovering scene and motion representations from simulation interaction data and utilizing diffusion policies or flow matching techniques, executable whole-body trajectories are sampled from highly multimodal motion distributions. This work aims to provide key technical support for building behavioral foundation models, enabling robots to track arbitrary motions under any disturbances, thereby significantly enhancing the adaptability and motor capabilities of humanoid robots in unstructured environments.

Content

Current research on motion control for humanoid robots faces two core challenges: first, maintaining dynamic balance when subjected to unknown disturbances such as external pushes or pulls, and second, tracking complex, non-repetitive whole-body motions (e.g., dancing, obstacle avoidance, or manipulation) in real time. Although traditional model predictive control (MPC) methods can handle some disturbances, they typically require precise dynamic models and simplified contact assumptions, making it difficult to cope with high-dimensional, nonlinear whole-body motions. Furthermore, while reinforcement learning-based methods can learn robust policies from simulation, their action spaces are often limited to specific tasks or low-dimensional primitives, lacking generalization to arbitrary motion commands. Therefore, constructing a unified control framework that can both handle multimodal action distributions and respond to real-time disturbances has become a key bottleneck in this field.

The Any2Track framework proposed in this paper treats motion generation as a conditional generation process. Its core idea is as follows: first, high-dimensional representations of scenes, targets, or motions are recovered from interaction data in simulation environments (including contact forces, tactile signals, and robot proprioceptive states); then, using diffusion policies or flow matching models, executable whole-body trajectories are generated by iterative denoising or probabilistic flow sampling from multimodal action distributions constrained by these representations. This approach avoids the difficulty of explicitly modeling action distributions in traditional methods, instead leveraging the powerful distribution-fitting capabilities of generative models to handle continuous spaces containing complex motions such as jumping, spinning, and grasping.

The key technical breakthrough of this work lies in the deep integration of "conditional generation" and "whole-body control." Specifically, diffusion policies generate action sequences by gradually adding conditional information (e.g., target motion trajectories, current contact force states) to random noise, while flow matching learns a continuous probability flow to connect the noise distribution with the true action distribution. Both methods can effectively handle multimodality in action distributions (e.g., the same target motion may have multiple different balance strategies). Additionally, by incorporating contact forces and tactile signals as conditional inputs, the framework enables generated actions to perceive and adapt to external disturbances in real time, thereby accurately tracking target motions while maintaining balance.

In terms of experimental validation, this work relies on high-fidelity simulation environments to construct a test set containing various disturbance scenarios (e.g., ground sliding, external pushes, load changes) and diverse motion commands (e.g., walking, running, dance movements). Experimental results show that whole-body trajectories generated by diffusion policies or flow matching significantly outperform traditional MPC baselines and reinforcement learning methods based on single action primitives in terms of motion tracking accuracy and disturbance robustness. In particular, the framework demonstrates zero-shot generalization to unseen motion commands and disturbance patterns, laying the foundation for its deployment on real robotic platforms. From an application perspective, Any2Track provides a key technical pathway for building general "behavioral foundation models," potentially advancing the practical use of humanoid robots in complex scenarios such as service, rescue, and industrial collaboration.
