Overview

This paper proposes a novel proprioceptive state estimation method called GAIT, designed to address the issue of inaccurate motion state estimation in legged robots on complex terrains caused by factors such as foot slippage and joint flexibility. The method innovatively models the time-series data from an Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU) and leg joint encoders as an "inertial-leg" token sequence, and utilizes an attention mechanism to fuse multimodal information, thereby achieving high-precision, robust state estimation without the need for external visual or GPS signals. GAIT provides a crucial state perception foundation for the autonomous motion control of legged systems, such as humanoid robots, in unstructured environments.

Content

For legged robots, especially humanoid robots, accurate estimation of proprioceptive states (e.g., body position, velocity, and orientation) is a prerequisite for stable control during walking, running, or obstacle crossing. Traditional methods typically rely on fusing IMU and joint encoder data but often assume ideal rigid foot-ground contact, ignoring common real-world phenomena such as slippage, bouncing, and joint flexibility. These non-ideal factors cause rapid accumulation of integration errors in kinematic models, leading to significant deviations in state estimation over long distances or on complex terrains. Therefore, achieving robust state estimation using only the robot's own proprioceptive sensors (IMU and joint encoders) under model uncertainty and varying contact conditions is a core challenge in the current field of legged robotics.

To address this issue, the GAIT method proposes a novel framework that combines data-driven approaches with model fusion. Its core idea is to transform continuous sensor data streams into discrete, semantically meaningful "tokens." Specifically, GAIT encodes the IMU readings (acceleration, angular velocity) and the position and velocity of each leg joint at each time step into an "inertial-leg" token. These token sequences not only preserve the temporal characteristics of the raw sensor data but also automatically learn long-range dependencies between different modalities and time steps through an attention mechanism. This design allows the model to implicitly identify foot-ground contact events (e.g., transitions between stance and swing phases) and dynamically adjust the confidence in IMU integration and kinematic calculations accordingly.

The key technological innovation of this research lies in the successful transfer of tokenization and attention mechanisms from natural language processing to the field of robot state estimation. Compared to traditional Kalman filter or particle filter methods, GAIT does not require manually designing complex kinematic or dynamic models, nor does it need to predefine probability distributions for contact states. By training on large-scale simulation and real robot datasets, the attention model can autonomously discover feature patterns in IMU and joint data related to state changes. For example, the model can learn that a sudden increase in joint torque of a leg, accompanied by specific changes in IMU acceleration, likely corresponds to a foot-ground collision event, where kinematic calculations should be trusted over IMU integration. This end-to-end learning paradigm significantly enhances the adaptability of state estimation in unstructured scenarios.

In terms of experimental validation, GAIT was tested on various legged robot platforms, including quadruped and humanoid robots, under conditions such as flat ground, slopes, gravel paths, and dynamic jumps. The results show that compared to classical methods based on the Extended Kalman Filter (EKF), GAIT reduces the root mean square error in body position and velocity estimation by approximately 30%-50%. Its robustness advantage is particularly pronounced in scenarios with severe foot slippage or when the robot is subjected to external impacts. Furthermore, the method exhibits strong tolerance to sensor noise and model mismatch, validating its reliability in real-world deployment. This research provides crucial technical support for legged robots to break free from reliance on external positioning infrastructure and achieve true autonomous mobility, with application value in scenarios such as disaster rescue, industrial inspection, and domestic service, where robots need to walk stably in complex environments.
