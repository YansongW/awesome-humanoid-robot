Overview

The design of dexterous hands for humanoid robots has long faced a trade-off between high degrees of freedom, compact structure, and manufacturing cost. Existing solutions mostly adopt direct-drive motors or tendon-driven mechanisms. The former leads to bulky hand sizes, while the latter suffers from issues such as low transmission efficiency and complex maintenance. DexLink Hand proposes a compact 16-degree-of-freedom dexterous hand design based on linkage transmission, aiming to achieve human-like dexterous manipulation capabilities at a lower cost. Through innovative mechanical structure and transmission layout, this research provides a feasible hardware solution for the large-scale application of robot dexterous hands in service and collaboration scenarios.

Content

(a) Research Background and Problem
In the field of robotics, the dexterous hand is a core end-effector for fine manipulation and environmental interaction. Although various dexterous hand designs have been developed, most struggle to balance the number of degrees of freedom, size, cost, and reliability. For example, high-DOF direct-drive motor hands are typically large and expensive, while tendon-driven hands, though compact, face engineering challenges such as high friction, elastic deformation, and limited lifespan. Therefore, maintaining over 16 degrees of freedom while keeping the hand size close to that of a human hand and significantly reducing manufacturing cost remains a key challenge in current humanoid robot hardware design.

(b) Method or Model Framework
DexLink Hand adopts a fully linkage-based transmission mechanism, replacing traditional direct-drive motors or tendon-driven solutions. Each finger joint is connected to a driving motor located at the base via a carefully designed linkage group, allowing the motors to be placed inside the palm or forearm, effectively reducing finger volume. The hand structure includes 16 independent degrees of freedom, with each finger having multiple bending and lateral swing joints, and the thumb additionally featuring an opposition movement DOF to support compound operations such as pinching, grasping, and posture adjustment. The linkage mechanism design is based on kinematic optimization to ensure that the range of motion for each joint approaches the physiological limits of the human hand.

(c) Key Technical Innovations
The core innovation of this research lies in the compact and modular design of the linkage transmission system. By employing spatial linkage mechanisms, DexLink Hand achieves separation between the driving motors and the actuated joints, eliminating the need for built-in motors in the fingers, thereby significantly reducing fingertip inertia and overall volume. Additionally, linkage transmission offers advantages such as zero backlash, high stiffness, and long lifespan, avoiding common issues like slack and wear found in tendon-driven systems. The design also incorporates an adjustable pre-tensioning mechanism, facilitating calibration of joint motion accuracy during assembly and maintenance. These innovations enable the dexterous hand to maintain high performance with 16 DOFs while achieving significantly lower manufacturing costs compared to similar direct-drive motor solutions.

(d) Experiment/Validation or Application Value
Although the paper does not provide detailed experimental data, based on the design goals and structural characteristics, DexLink Hand is suitable for scenarios requiring high dexterity and low cost, such as home service robots, educational research platforms, and light industrial collaborative tasks. Its compact form factor and anthropomorphic motion capabilities allow it to interface with standard humanoid robot arms and perform common operations like grasping, rotating, and pressing. Future work could quantify its performance through grasp success rate tests, joint torque measurements, and durability experiments. The widespread adoption of this design is expected to lower the entry barrier for dexterous hands in academic research and industrial applications, advancing humanoid robots from the laboratory to real-world deployment.
