Summary

HMC mainly solves the data closed loop: using teleoperation/exoskeleton data, contact force/tactile signals to collect human operation and robot status, and then transfer it to trainable and reusable joint position/torque commands, whole-body trajectory/action sequences, and low-level controller targets through ACT/behavioral cloning imitation learning, hierarchical skills/expert strategies. The key is to break down tasks into routable skills or expert strategies, which are then selected and combined in execution using high-level modules.

Overview

Humanoid robots face challenges such as difficulty in data collection, poor skill generalization, and complex whole-body coordination when performing contact-rich mobile manipulation tasks. The Heterogeneous Meta-Control (HMC) framework proposed in this paper aims to construct a complete data loop, transforming teleoperation and exoskeleton-collected human operation data into trainable and reusable robot control commands. The core contribution of this work lies in proposing an architecture that decomposes complex tasks into routable skills or expert policies, dynamically selected and combined by a high-level module, thereby significantly enhancing the robot's operational capability and adaptability in unstructured environments.

Content

In the field of robotics, loco-manipulation for humanoid robots has always been a research hotspot and challenge. Traditional methods typically treat locomotion and manipulation separately, making it difficult to achieve whole-body coordination, especially in tasks requiring sustained physical contact with the environment, such as opening doors, carrying objects, or assembly. While existing reinforcement learning-based methods can generate complex behaviors, they often require extensive simulation interactions and are difficult to transfer directly to real robots. Meanwhile, teleoperation-based data collection methods are intuitive, but the acquired data usually lacks structured skill representation, leading to limited model generalization. Therefore, efficiently collecting high-quality human demonstration data and transforming it into composable and transferable robot skills has become a key bottleneck in current research.

The HMC framework proposes a hierarchical heterogeneous control strategy to address these issues. The framework first collects joint movements, contact forces, and tactile signals from human operators, along with real-time robot states, using teleoperation devices and exoskeleton systems. These multimodal data are then fed into an ACT (Action Chunking with Transformers)-based behavioral cloning imitation learning module, which converts raw human operation trajectories into trainable joint position/torque commands, whole-body motion sequences, and target values for low-level controllers. This process achieves an end-to-end mapping from human demonstrations to executable robot instructions, ensuring efficient data reuse.

In terms of technological innovation, the core of HMC lies in its "heterogeneous meta-control" concept. Unlike traditional end-to-end models that directly output continuous actions, HMC explicitly decomposes complex tasks into several discrete "skills" or "expert policies," such as "grasping," "moving," and "pushing/pulling." Each skill corresponds to a dedicated sub-policy network responsible for handling specific types of contact interactions. The high-level module dynamically selects and combines these skills based on the current task goal and perception state, forming a complete execution sequence. This routing mechanism not only reduces the learning difficulty of individual policies but also enables the system to flexibly adapt to unseen task variants, significantly improving generalization.

To validate the effectiveness of HMC, the research team conducted multiple contact-rich loco-manipulation experiments on a real humanoid robot platform. Experimental tasks included carrying heavy objects in confined spaces, performing push/pull operations with tools, and completing multi-step assembly processes. Results show that the HMC framework successfully converts human teleoperation data into stable robot behaviors, with its skill combination strategy demonstrating strong robustness against environmental disturbances (e.g., object position shifts, contact force variations). Compared to baseline methods using direct behavioral cloning, HMC achieves significant improvements in task success rate and operational precision, particularly in scenarios requiring continuous switching between different contact modes.

From an application perspective, HMC provides a feasible path for skill learning and deployment in next-generation humanoid robots. Its data loop design enables robots to continuously learn new skills from human operations, while the hierarchical architecture ensures modularity and scalability of skills. In the future, this framework is expected to be applied in fields requiring complex physical interactions for humanoid robots, such as home services, industrial assembly, and disaster rescue, driving a paradigm shift from pre-programmed execution to autonomous skill composition.
