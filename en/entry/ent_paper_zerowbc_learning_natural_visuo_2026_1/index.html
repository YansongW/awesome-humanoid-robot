Overview

Humanoid robot whole-body control (WBC) has long faced two major challenges: first, how to efficiently learn natural and coordinated visuomotor policies from human demonstrations, and second, how to avoid reliance on expensive teleoperation equipment or precise motion capture systems. This paper proposes the ZeroWBC framework, which directly learns visuomotor control for humanoid robots from first-person egocentric videos, achieving a "zero-teleoperation" learning paradigm without any teleoperation intervention. The main contribution of this work lies in being the first to jointly utilize visual and tactile information from first-person videos for end-to-end learning of whole-body motion policies for humanoid robots, significantly reducing data collection costs and enhancing the naturalness and robustness of policies in complex scenarios.

Content

Research Background and Problem

Traditional whole-body control methods for humanoid robots typically rely on manually designed kinematic models, precise dynamic parameters, and expensive teleoperation teaching systems, which limits their generalization ability in unstructured environments. In recent years, imitation learning-based methods have made progress, but most require multi-view cameras, motion capture suits, or force feedback devices to obtain high-quality expert data, which is not only costly but also difficult to scale to everyday scenarios. The core problem addressed in this paper is: can we directly learn the whole-body visuomotor control policies required for humanoid robots using only naturally occurring first-person videos from daily human activities (e.g., daily operation videos captured by a head-mounted camera)? Solving this problem would significantly lower the barrier to skill acquisition for humanoid robots, bringing them closer to the human cognitive model of "learning by observation."

Method or Model Framework

ZeroWBC proposes a two-stage learning framework. The first stage is "visual-tactile alignment pre-training," which uses large-scale first-person video datasets to map visual features from video frames and corresponding tactile signals (e.g., hand contact forces, joint torques) into a shared latent space via contrastive learning, thereby establishing cross-modal association priors. The second stage is "whole-body motion policy fine-tuning," where a Transformer-based whole-body motion decoder is introduced on top of the pre-trained model to map feature sequences from the shared latent space to whole-body joint commands for the humanoid robot (including torso, arms, legs, and end-effectors). The entire framework is trained end-to-end, with input being only first-person RGB videos and output being whole-body motion commands for the robot.

Key Technical Innovations

The core innovations of ZeroWBC are reflected in three aspects. First, it proposes a "zero-teleoperation" learning paradigm, completely eliminating the dependence on physical teleoperation devices or motion capture systems, requiring only that humans wear a first-person camera during daily activities to collect training data. Second, it designs a visual-tactile joint representation learning mechanism, which implicitly learns the contact patterns between hands and objects in videos, enabling the robot to infer force interaction information during manipulation, thereby generating more natural and stable whole-body motion trajectories. Third, it introduces a hierarchical design for the whole-body motion decoder, decomposing the high-dimensional joint space into three subspaces: torso balance, arm manipulation, and leg movement, and coordinating actions across subspaces through a cross-attention mechanism, effectively alleviating the curse of dimensionality and motion conflicts in whole-body control.

Experiments/Validation and Application Value

Experiments in simulation environments and on real humanoid robot platforms demonstrate that ZeroWBC generates smooth and natural whole-body motions in various daily manipulation tasks (e.g., opening doors, carrying boxes, wiping tables), with success rates significantly higher than baseline methods based on traditional motion planning or pure visual imitation learning. Notably, in zero-shot generalization tests, ZeroWBC maintains high task completion rates even with unseen object shapes and scene layouts, reflecting the strong generalization ability learned from first-person videos. The practical application value of this method lies in the fact that future humanoid robots can autonomously acquire new skills by watching everyday human videos (e.g., household chores, workshop operations) without the need for professional programming or teleoperation teaching, providing a low-cost, high-efficiency path for skill acquisition in scenarios such as home service and industrial assistance.
