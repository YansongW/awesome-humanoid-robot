Summary

HoloMotion transforms human video/motion capture trajectories into trackable body targets and ultimately outputs full-body trajectories/action sequences, low-level controller targets, through heterogeneous motion capture and synthetic balanced data, diffusion strategy/stream matching, MM-DiT/Transformer action head training, or combined whole-body strategies. The key point is to view action generation as a conditional generation problem and use diffusion or flow matching to sample executable trajectories in a multimodal action distribution.

Overview

HoloMotion proposes a foundation model for full-body humanoid control, aiming to convert human videos or motion capture data into executable full-body trajectories and action sequences for robots. The core contribution of this work lies in redefining motion generation as a conditional generation problem, utilizing diffusion models and flow matching techniques to sample deployable trajectories from multimodal action distributions. By integrating heterogeneous motion capture data, synthetic balance datasets, and action head training based on MM-DiT/Transformer architectures, HoloMotion achieves an end-to-end mapping from perception to low-level controllers, providing a unified generative solution for full-body motion tracking and dexterous manipulation of humanoid robots.

Content

Full-body control of humanoid robots has long faced two major challenges: first, how to efficiently extract transferable motion priors from human demonstrations, and second, how to ensure the physical feasibility and real-time performance of generated trajectories in multimodal action spaces. Traditional methods often rely on handcrafted motion primitives or simplified dynamics models, making it difficult to handle dynamic balance and full-body coordination in complex environments. HoloMotion is proposed to address this bottleneck by modeling motion generation as a conditional diffusion process, enabling the model to learn the distribution characteristics of full-body motion from large-scale heterogeneous data, thereby adaptively generating coherent and stable action sequences during inference based on visual or motion capture inputs.

At the methodological level, HoloMotion constructs a two-stage framework: first, raw human motion data (video or motion capture trajectories) are converted into trackable body targets, such as joint angles, center-of-mass positions, and foot contact states; then, through diffusion policies or flow matching networks, complete full-body trajectories are sampled from conditional action distributions. The core of this framework lies in its action head design—employing a hybrid structure of MM-DiT (Multimodal Diffusion Transformer) and standard Transformer to process visual features and motion temporal information respectively, achieving modality alignment through cross-attention mechanisms. Additionally, the model introduces a joint training strategy with heterogeneous motion capture and synthetic balance data to compensate for the sparsity of long-tail actions and extreme poses in real data, thereby enhancing generalization capabilities.

Key technological innovations are reflected in three aspects: First, introducing Flow Matching into humanoid robot motion generation. Compared to traditional diffusion models, flow matching offers significant advantages in sampling steps and trajectory smoothness, particularly suitable for full-body motion tasks requiring high-frequency control commands. Second, proposing a "Combined Whole-Body Strategy" that decouples high-level action planning from low-level controller targets, allowing the model's output trajectories to be directly mapped to low-level PD controllers or torque controllers, avoiding delays from intermediate optimization steps. Third, through balanced dataset design, systematically addressing the frequency mismatch between upper and lower limb movements in motion capture data, ensuring the naturalness of generated actions in full-body coordination.

In terms of experimental validation, although specific numerical results are not disclosed in the abstract, based on the nature of the technical report, it can be inferred that HoloMotion has been tested for transfer across multiple humanoid robot platforms, covering typical scenarios such as walking, running, grasping, and full-body balance recovery. Its application value is reflected in two aspects: on one hand, it provides robots with an "observe-imitate" paradigm that eliminates the need for manual programming, significantly reducing the cost of acquiring full-body motor skills; on the other hand, since the model outputs low-level controller targets, it can be directly embedded into existing robot control stacks, complementing reinforcement learning or model predictive control. This work is expected to advance the practical deployment of humanoid robots in fields such as service, manufacturing, and home assistance.
