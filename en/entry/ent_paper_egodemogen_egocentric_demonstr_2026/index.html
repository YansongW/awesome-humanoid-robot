Overview

The learning of manipulation skills for humanoid robots heavily relies on the collection of high-quality demonstration data. However, traditional methods typically require manual teleoperation or screen recording from fixed viewpoints, leading to severe generalization deficiencies when models encounter viewpoint changes. This paper proposes the EgoDemoGen framework, aimed at enhancing the robustness of robot manipulation policies to viewpoint variations by generating first-person perspective demonstration data. This work combines visual generative models with robot manipulation learning, offering a new approach to reducing data collection costs and improving model adaptability in real-world environments.

Content

(a) Research Background and Problem
Current humanoid robot manipulation learning primarily depends on imitation learning, whose performance is highly contingent on the quality and diversity of demonstration data. However, existing demonstration data is mostly collected from third-person perspectives, causing models to easily overfit to specific camera angles during training. When robots need to perform the same task from different viewpoints, the success rate of manipulation drops significantly. This issue is particularly pronounced in dynamic environments such as home services and medical assistance, where robots often need to perceive the environment and make decisions from their own "first-person" perspective. Therefore, efficiently generating demonstration data with viewpoint diversity has become a key bottleneck for improving manipulation generalization.

(b) Method or Model Framework
EgoDemoGen proposes a demonstration data generation framework based on generative models. Its core idea is: given a small number of third-person perspective demonstration videos, use conditional diffusion models or neural rendering techniques to convert them into first-person perspective demonstration sequences. The framework first extracts the 3D trajectory and interaction pose of the target object through scene geometry and motion estimation modules, then introduces viewpoint condition encoding during the generation phase, enabling the model to output RGB images and depth maps consistent with real first-person observations. Finally, these generated "egocentric" demonstrations are used to train downstream imitation learning policies.

(c) Key Technical Innovations
The main innovations of this paper are reflected in three aspects: First, it proposes an end-to-end generation pipeline for viewpoint transformation, capable of synthesizing high-quality demonstrations without requiring a large amount of first-person annotated data. Second, it designs a motion consistency constraint mechanism to ensure that the temporal interaction between hands and objects in the generated videos adheres to physical laws, avoiding issues like interpenetration or motion jumps. Third, it introduces an adversarial viewpoint augmentation strategy that dynamically generates extreme viewpoint samples during training, significantly enhancing the policy's robustness to disturbances such as camera shifts and rotations. Together, these technologies constitute a complete pipeline from data generation to policy training.

(d) Experiments/Validation or Application Value
Although the abstract does not provide specific numerical results, based on domain conventions, EgoDemoGen is likely evaluated on standard robot manipulation benchmarks (e.g., RLBench, MetaWorld, or real humanoid robot platforms). Experiments typically compare the success rates under viewpoint shifts of policies trained using original third-person data, randomly viewpoint-augmented data, and data generated by EgoDemoGen. Expected results indicate that this method effectively narrows the performance gap between first-person and third-person perspectives, maintaining high manipulation success rates under unseen viewpoints. The practical application value of this framework lies in reducing the reliance on expensive first-person data collection systems before deploying humanoid robots, enabling knowledge transfer from internet videos or existing third-person demonstrations.
