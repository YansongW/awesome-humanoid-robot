Summary

This work mainly addresses data closed loops: acquisition of human operation and robotic states with camera images/multi-view observations, proprioceptive states and joint sequences, teleoperation/exoskeleton data, and joint sequences, then, AMP/motion prior, Whole-body Controller/WBC/Mpc are transformed into trainable and reusable whole-body trajectory/action sequences, and low-level controller targets. The key point is to place AMP/Motion Priors and Full Body Controller/WBC/MPC in the same training/deployment link to reduce the discontinuity between high-level targets and low-level actions.

Overview

This paper addresses the "disconnect" between high-level motion planning and low-level execution in whole-body control of humanoid robots, proposing a scalable and general whole-body control framework. The core contribution lies in integrating Motion Priors (AMP), Whole-Body Control (WBC), and Model Predictive Control (MPC) into a unified training and deployment pipeline, thereby constructing a complete data loop. By uniformly processing multi-source information from camera images, proprioception, and teleoperation data, the framework aims to generate trainable and reusable whole-body trajectories and action sequences, laying the foundation for achieving general motion capabilities across humanoid robots.

Content

(a) Research Background and Problem: Current whole-body control of humanoid robots faces two major challenges. First, high-level motion planning (e.g., motion priors generated by AMP) and low-level joint control (e.g., outputs from WBC/MPC) often belong to different technical stacks, with significant semantic and temporal scale gaps between them, making it difficult for low-level controllers to smoothly track planned high-level goals. Second, different humanoid robot platforms vary greatly in configuration and dynamic parameters; existing control methods are typically tailored to specific platforms and lack cross-platform transferability. Therefore, designing a general framework that bridges the gap between high-level and low-level control while adapting to different robot morphologies becomes a key issue in this field.

(b) Method or Model Framework: The proposed framework adopts a data loop as its core architecture. On the data acquisition side, the system integrates multi-view camera images, robot proprioceptive states (e.g., joint angles, torques), and human operation data recorded by teleoperation/exoskeleton devices. These multimodal data are then fed into a joint training pipeline, where the AMP module extracts generalizable motion priors, and the WBC/MPC module converts these priors into specific whole-body trajectories and low-level controller targets. A key innovation is that AMP and WBC/MPC are no longer optimized independently but are trained end-to-end under a unified loss function, ensuring consistency between high-level intent and low-level execution.

(c) Key Technical Innovations: The technical highlights of this work are threefold. First, it proposes a unified representation space that encodes observation data from different sources (vision, proprioception, teleoperation) into shared latent variables, allowing motion priors to be compatible with both human demonstrations and robot experience. Second, through differentiable WBC/MPC layers, the framework enables backpropagation from high-level trajectories to low-level joint torque gradients, allowing motion priors to adaptively adjust based on the actual response of the low-level controller. Finally, the framework designs modular interfaces that allow rapid adaptation to the kinematic parameters and dynamic characteristics of different humanoid robots, thereby achieving cross-platform scalability.

(d) Experiments/Validation and Application Value: Although the paper does not disclose specific experimental data, it can be inferred from the technical approach that validation of the framework may cover two types of scenarios: first, testing whole-body motion generation capabilities of different humanoid robot configurations (e.g., wheeled and bipedal) in simulation environments, including composite tasks such as walking, squatting, and upper-limb manipulation; second, validating the transfer from teleoperation data to autonomous control on real robot platforms. The application value of this work lies in providing a complete closed-loop path from data collection and motion prior extraction to whole-body control execution for humanoid robot skill learning, which is expected to significantly reduce debugging costs for new tasks or platforms and promote the practical deployment of humanoid robots in fields such as service and manufacturing.
