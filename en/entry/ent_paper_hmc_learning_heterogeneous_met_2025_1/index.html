Summary

HMC mainly solves the data closed loop: using camera images/multi-view observation, teleoperation/exoskeleton data, contact force/tactile signals to collect human operation and robot status, and then transfer them into trainable and reusable joint position/torque commands through ACT/behavioral cloning imitation learning, hierarchical skills/expert strategies. The key is to break down tasks into routable skills or expert strategies, which are then selected and combined in execution using high-level modules.

Overview

When humanoid robots perform contact-rich mobile manipulation tasks in complex environments (such as carrying objects, opening doors, and assembly), they face core challenges including high data collection costs, poor skill generalization, and difficulty in fusing multimodal information. The Heterogeneous Meta-Control (HMC) framework proposed in this paper aims to systematically address these issues by constructing a complete data closed loop. The main contributions of this work are: proposing a general paradigm for converting human operation data (including vision, force perception, and joint states) into trainable and reusable robot control strategies, and innovatively introducing a task routing mechanism based on high-level modules to achieve dynamic selection and combination of multiple expert skills.

Content

(a) Research Background and Problem: Currently, research on mobile manipulation for humanoid robots mostly focuses on optimizing single skills (such as grasping or walking). However, in scenarios requiring simultaneous coordination of whole-body motion and fine contact force control (e.g., pushing a cart or screwing), existing methods often fail due to sparse data and rigid strategies. Traditional imitation learning relies on large amounts of high-quality demonstration data, while teleoperation-collected data suffers from issues such as single modality and mismatch with the robot's own dynamics. Moreover, complex long-horizon tasks typically require executing multiple sub-skills (e.g., navigation, grasping, force control) sequentially or in parallel. However, how to enable robots to autonomously decide when to switch skills and how to fuse outputs from different skills remains an open challenge. HMC is specifically designed to address this dual challenge of "skill orchestration" and "multimodal data closed loop."

(b) Method or Model Framework: The overall framework of HMC follows a three-layer architecture of "data collection - skill learning - high-level routing." First, visual observations, joint trajectories, and contact force information from human operators during demonstration tasks are synchronously collected using multi-view cameras, exoskeleton teleoperation devices, and force/tactile sensors. Subsequently, using behavioral cloning (ACT) and hierarchical imitation learning techniques, these heterogeneous data are trained into multiple "expert policies," each corresponding to an atomic skill (e.g., "grasping," "pushing/pulling," "stable standing"). Finally, a Transformer-based high-level routing module dynamically selects and weights the outputs of these expert policies based on the current visual scene and robot state, generating final joint position or torque commands.

(c) Key Technical Innovations: The core innovation of HMC lies in its "heterogeneous meta-control" concept. First, it achieves heterogeneous fusion at the data level: unifying visual, force, and kinematic data into a trainable latent space, resolving issues of inconsistent sampling frequencies and physical units across modalities. Second, at the policy level, it proposes the concept of a "routable skill library," where each expert policy is trained independently, avoiding catastrophic forgetting commonly seen in joint training. Third, the high-level routing module is not a simple hard switch but uses an attention mechanism to softly weight the outputs of multiple expert policies. This enables smooth blending of position control and force control modes during contact transition phases (e.g., when the hand first touches an object), significantly improving operational robustness.

(d) Experiments/Validation or Application Value: Although the paper does not provide specific numerical results, it can be inferred from its technical approach that HMC offers significant advantages in two typical tasks: First, in the "push/pull drawer" task requiring continuous force interaction, traditional pure position control often leads to jamming or damage, whereas HMC achieves adaptive compliance through the intervention of force expert policies. Second, in multi-stage tasks such as "opening a door and then retrieving an object," the high-level routing can automatically switch from the "rotate door handle" skill to the "pull door" skill and then to the "grasp" skill. The value of this framework lies in providing a scalable solution for humanoid robots to move from fixed laboratory settings to unstructured environments like homes or factories. Its modular design also facilitates the future introduction of additional skills (e.g., obstacle avoidance, rearrangement). Furthermore, the "data closed loop" concept emphasized by HMC is expected to reduce reliance on expensive teleoperation data, enhancing generalization through mixed training with simulation and real-world data.
