Summary

Halomi first reconstructs the scene, object, or motion representation from camera images/multi-view observations, then, the joint position/Torque Command and the whole body trajectory/action sequence are generated by ACT/behavior clone imitation learning, diffusion strategy/flow matching. The key point is to treat action generation as a conditional generation problem, and use diffusion or flow matching to sample executable trajectories in multimodal action distributions.

Overview

When humanoid robots perform mobile manipulation tasks in complex environments, they face the challenge of deep coupling between perception and action generation. Existing methods often treat visual perception and motion control separately, making it difficult to adapt to multimodal action distributions in unstructured scenarios. HALOMI proposes an end-to-end framework for learning active perception from human demonstrations. By modeling action generation as a conditional generation problem and utilizing diffusion models or flow matching to sample among multiple feasible trajectories, it significantly enhances the robustness and adaptability of humanoid robots in mobile manipulation tasks.

Content

Loco-manipulation for humanoid robots is a frontier challenge in robotics, with the core difficulty lying in simultaneously coordinating whole-body motion and end-effector operations while responding to environmental changes in real time. Traditional methods typically rely on handcrafted motion planners or pre-programmed action libraries, which have limited generalization capabilities in dynamic, unstructured environments. In recent years, the combination of imitation learning and visual perception has provided a new paradigm for robot skill acquisition. However, effectively extracting task-relevant representations from high-dimensional visual inputs and generating whole-body trajectories that satisfy physical constraints remain unresolved key issues. It is against this backdrop that HALOMI focuses on the joint learning of "active perception" and "conditional action generation."

The methodological framework of HALOMI consists of two core stages. The first stage is perception representation reconstruction: the system uses differentiable rendering or implicit neural representation techniques from monocular or multi-view camera images to recover scene geometry, object poses, or human motion representations. This process not only extracts static environmental information but also captures dynamic interaction features through temporal observations, providing structured input for subsequent action generation. The second stage is action generation: based on the perception representations obtained in the first stage, the system adopts Behavior Cloning and the ACT (Action Chunking with Transformers) architecture, treating action generation as a conditional probability modeling problem. Specifically, the model learns a mapping from perception representations to joint position/torque commands or whole-body trajectories, and samples from the action distribution via diffusion policies or flow matching, thereby avoiding mode collapse caused by unimodal prediction.

The key technological innovations of this framework are reflected in three aspects. First, it transforms action generation from a deterministic mapping into a conditional generation problem, enabling the model to express multimodal action distributions—for example, when grasping the same object, the robot may choose different gaits or arm postures, and the diffusion model can naturally cover these feasible solutions. Second, it introduces an active perception mechanism, where the action generation process inversely influences perceptual attention, allowing the robot to adjust its observation viewpoint or sensor configuration based on current task requirements, forming a perception-action closed loop. Third, it replaces traditional diffusion processes with flow matching, significantly reducing the number of sampling steps while maintaining generation quality, thereby improving real-time performance, which is particularly important for humanoid robots requiring high-frequency control.

In terms of experimental validation, HALOMI was systematically evaluated in both simulation environments and on real humanoid robot platforms. Simulation experiments were based on various mobile manipulation tasks (such as pushing carts, carrying objects, and opening doors), comparing baseline methods based on deterministic regression with HALOMI's generative framework. The results showed that the latter achieved significant improvements in task success rate, trajectory smoothness, and robustness against disturbances. Real robot experiments further verified that skills learned from human demonstration data can be directly transferred to physical platforms, and the active perception mechanism enables the robot to maintain stable operation in scenarios with occlusion or lighting changes. The practical value of this work lies in providing a scalable skill learning paradigm for humanoid robots in scenarios requiring flexible movement and fine manipulation, such as home services, industrial assembly, and disaster rescue.
