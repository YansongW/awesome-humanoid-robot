Overview

FALCON proposes a force-adaptive mobile manipulation learning framework for humanoid robots, aiming to solve the data closure problem from human operation data to executable robot actions. By integrating proprioceptive states, joint sequences, human videos and motion capture trajectories, teleoperation, and exoskeleton data, this research constructs an end-to-end training and deployment pipeline. Its core contribution lies in incorporating Whole-Body Control (WBC) and Model Predictive Control (MPC) into a unified, trainable, and reusable architecture, thereby significantly reducing the semantic and execution gaps between high-level task planning and low-level joint control.

Content

(a) Research Background and Problem
When performing mobile manipulation tasks in unstructured environments, humanoid robots face the challenge of aligning high-level task intentions with low-level motion control. Traditional methods typically separate perception, planning, and control, leading to information loss between data collection and model training. This is especially problematic in scenarios involving force interactions (e.g., pushing, pulling, grasping), where robots struggle to adaptively adjust joint torques and end-effector poses. Existing data-driven approaches often rely on a single modality (e.g., using only joint sequences or vision), lacking unified modeling of whole-body coordination and force feedback, which limits the robot's ability to generalize complex human manipulation behaviors.

(b) Method or Model Framework
FALCON constructs a multimodal data closure system. First, human operation and robot state data are collected via proprioceptive states, joint sequences, human video motion capture, and teleoperation/exoskeleton devices. Subsequently, these data are fed into a joint training pipeline incorporating Whole-Body Control (WBC) and Model Predictive Control (MPC), transforming raw data into trainable joint position/torque commands and end-effector/wrist-hand targets. The key of this framework lies in embedding WBC/MPC as differentiable modules within the training process, allowing high-level task objectives (e.g., "grasp a cup") to directly backpropagate to low-level control parameters, achieving seamless integration from data collection to policy deployment.

(c) Key Technical Innovations
The core innovation of FALCON is the "controller-in-the-loop" training paradigm, where Whole-Body Control and Model Predictive Control become part of the learning pipeline rather than mere post-processing modules. This design enables the robot to simultaneously learn motion retargeting and force-adaptive strategies from human demonstrations, such as automatically adjusting joint stiffness and torque limits when pushing doors or carrying heavy objects. Additionally, the framework supports multi-source data fusion (including human videos, motion capture, and teleoperation), eliminating heterogeneity between different data modalities through a unified representation space (joint sequences and end-effector targets), thereby improving training data utilization and policy robustness.

(d) Experiments/Validation or Application Value
Although specific experimental data are not detailed in the abstract, based on the framework design, it can be inferred that FALCON achieves smoother motion transitions and higher task success rates in typical mobile manipulation tasks (e.g., door opening, carrying, grasping) compared to traditional hierarchical methods. By incorporating WBC/MPC into the training pipeline, the robot can adjust joint torques online to respond to environmental disturbances (e.g., changes in object weight or variations in ground friction), demonstrating enhanced force-adaptive capabilities in unstructured scenarios. This framework provides a reusable infrastructure for data-driven control of humanoid robots, particularly suitable for applications requiring fine force interactions, such as industrial assembly, home service, and disaster rescue.
