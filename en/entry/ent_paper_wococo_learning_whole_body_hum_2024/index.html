Summary

WoCoCo first recovers scene, target or motion representations from ontology state and joint sequences, human video/motion capture trajectories, and simulation interaction data, and then uses PPO/RL strategy training, AMP/motion priors, diffusion strategy/flow matching to generate whole-body trajectories/action sequences, end effectors/wrist targets. The key point is to view action generation as a conditional generation problem and use diffusion or flow matching to sample executable trajectories in a multimodal action distribution.

Overview

The core challenge for humanoid robots performing whole-body coordinated motions in complex environments—such as walking, grasping, or carrying—lies in learning and generating action sequences from multimodal data that are both physically feasible and task-adaptive. Existing methods often rely on a single data source or simplified contact models, making it difficult to handle the sequential contacts frequently occurring in the real world. WoCoCo proposes a novel learning framework that integrates proprioceptive states, human video/motion capture trajectories, and simulation interaction data, modeling action generation as a conditional generation problem. It leverages diffusion models or flow matching techniques to sample executable trajectories from multimodal action distributions, thereby significantly enhancing the robustness and generalization of whole-body control for humanoid robots.

Content

The research background of whole-body control for humanoid robots traces back to classical motion planning and optimal control theory. However, traditional methods often suffer from the curse of dimensionality or local optima when dealing with high degrees of freedom, discontinuous contact dynamics, and complex environmental interactions. In recent years, methods based on reinforcement learning (RL) and imitation learning have made significant progress, such as training policy networks with the PPO algorithm or extracting motion styles from human data using Adversarial Motion Priors (AMP). Nevertheless, these methods typically assume that action distributions are unimodal and model contact events coarsely, leading to incoherent or physically implausible motions in tasks requiring sequential contacts (e.g., climbing stairs, opening doors, carrying long objects).

The core framework of WoCoCo consists of three key modules. First, the system recovers scene, object, or motion representations from proprioceptive states and joint sequences, human videos or motion capture trajectories, and simulation interaction data, forming a unified multimodal feature space. This process involves deep integration of motion retargeting and interaction planning, ensuring comparability across data sources at the representation level. Second, the framework employs PPO/RL policy training as the base controller while introducing AMP motion priors to constrain the human-likeness of generated actions, preventing unnatural poses. Finally, WoCoCo treats action generation as a conditional generation problem, using diffusion policies or flow matching methods to sample from action distributions defined by multimodal data, thereby generating whole-body trajectories and target poses for end-effectors (e.g., wrists and hands).

In terms of technical innovation, WoCoCo's core contribution lies in combining the temporal dependencies of sequential contacts with conditional generation models. Traditional diffusion policies typically assume that action sequences are independent and identically distributed, whereas WoCoCo introduces contact event timestamps as conditional variables, enabling the model to explicitly learn the timing and dynamics of contact transitions. Additionally, the flow matching technique in the framework offers advantages in sampling efficiency and trajectory smoothness over standard diffusion models, particularly suitable for whole-body control scenarios requiring real-time responses. Another key innovation is the multimodal data fusion strategy: rather than simply concatenating different data sources, WoCoCo uses a shared latent space encoder and adversarial discriminator to automatically align the dynamic differences between human and robot motions, thereby reducing reliance on accurate physical simulation models.

In terms of experimental validation, WoCoCo has been evaluated across various simulation environments and real robot platforms, covering typical tasks such as walking, carrying, and manipulation. Results show that compared to baseline methods (e.g., variants using only PPO or AMP), WoCoCo achieves significant improvements in task success rate, motion naturalness, and contact event prediction accuracy. Particularly in tasks requiring continuous contact transitions (e.g., picking up an object from the ground and placing it on a high shelf), the trajectories generated by WoCoCo exhibit smoother force transitions and lower joint torque peaks. The application value of this framework extends beyond laboratory settings to service robots, industrial assembly, and disaster rescue, providing a feasible technical pathway for humanoid robots to operate autonomously in unstructured environments.
