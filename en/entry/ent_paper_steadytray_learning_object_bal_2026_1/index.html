Overview

When performing everyday service tasks such as carrying a tray, humanoid robots need to simultaneously coordinate whole-body motion and object balance, posing a significant challenge to the integration of existing motion control and manipulation capabilities. This study proposes a framework called SteadyTray, which uses a residual reinforcement learning (Residual RL) method to enable bipedal humanoid robots to stably transport a tray and maintain the balance of objects on it while walking. The main contribution of this work lies in decoupling high-level motion planning from low-level balance adjustments, using a residual policy to learn fine-grained whole-body coordination actions on top of a base walking controller, thereby significantly enhancing the loco-manipulation capabilities of humanoid robots in unstructured environments.

Content

(a) Research Background and Problem: Loco-manipulation of humanoid robots is a frontier challenge in robotics, with the core issue being how to simultaneously ensure the stability of bipedal walking and the execution of upper-body manipulation tasks. Traditional methods typically treat walking and manipulation separately, making it difficult for robots to handle balance disturbances of objects in dynamic environments. Particularly in tasks like carrying a tray, the robot must not only maintain its own gait stability but also compensate in real-time for the sliding or tilting of objects on the tray, imposing extremely high demands on whole-body coordination control. Existing model-based control methods often rely on accurate dynamic modeling, but in practice, model errors and environmental uncertainties lead to a sharp decline in performance.

(b) Method or Model Framework: SteadyTray proposes a two-layer control architecture based on residual reinforcement learning. The lower layer uses a pre-trained base walking policy (e.g., trained via model predictive control or traditional reinforcement learning) to generate stable gait trajectories. The upper layer is a residual reinforcement learning policy that takes as input the robot's current state, perceptual information about objects on the tray (e.g., inertial measurement unit data), and walking commands, and outputs corrections to the base walking policy. These corrections act on the whole-body joints, particularly the posture adjustments of the upper body and torso, thereby achieving active compensation for the balance of tray objects without compromising walking stability. The key advantage of the residual design is that it avoids learning complex walking-manipulation coupling strategies from scratch, significantly reducing training difficulty.

(c) Key Technical Innovations: The technical innovations of this work are mainly reflected in three aspects. First, it introduces the concept of "residual manipulation," modeling the object balance task as incremental corrections to the base motion policy. This decoupling makes policy learning more efficient and transferable. Second, it designs a specialized reward function that simultaneously penalizes tray tilt angle, object acceleration, and abrupt changes in the robot's own joint torques, thereby achieving a balance between balance accuracy and motion smoothness. Third, it constructs training scenarios in a simulation environment that include various tray loads (e.g., objects of different masses and shapes) and ground disturbances (e.g., slopes, random pushes), enhancing the policy's generalization ability. Additionally, the framework supports zero-shot transfer to real robots without the need for retraining on physical hardware.

(d) Experiments/Validation or Application Value: Experiments are validated in both simulation environments and on real humanoid robot platforms. Simulation results show that SteadyTray significantly outperforms pure walking policies and direct end-to-end reinforcement learning methods in metrics such as tray tilt angle and object sliding distance, and is robust to load variations. In real robot experiments, the framework successfully enables the robot to stably transport a tray containing liquid on flat ground and slopes, with the amount of liquid spillage controlled at a very low level. The application value of this work lies in scenarios such as service robots, logistics handling, and home assistance, providing a feasible technical pathway for humanoid robots to perform fine-grained loco-manipulation tasks. Future work can further extend to more complex tasks such as multi-object transportation and dynamic obstacle avoidance.
