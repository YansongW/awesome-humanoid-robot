Overview

The paper "GTSAM: Factor graphs for Sensor Fusion in Robotics" focuses on state estimation problems for humanoid robots, proposing a sensor fusion method based on a factor graph framework. This work aims to address the challenge of insufficient pose and state estimation accuracy in humanoid robots operating in complex dynamic environments, caused by heterogeneous multimodal sensor data and complex noise models. Its main contribution lies in systematically applying factor graph theory to the humanoid robotics domain. By constructing a unified probabilistic graphical model, it achieves robust fusion of multi-source information, such as inertial measurement units, visual odometry, and joint encoders, providing a high-precision state estimation foundation for subsequent Simultaneous Localization and Mapping (SLAM) and motion planning.

Content

(a) Research Background and Problem: Due to their bipedal walking structure and high-degree-of-freedom joint systems, humanoid robots impose requirements on the real-time performance and robustness of state estimation that far exceed those of wheeled robots. Traditional methods based on the Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) often suffer from accumulated linearization errors and consistency degradation when dealing with non-Gaussian noise, sensor failures, or rapid motion. Furthermore, humanoid robots frequently need to operate in unstructured terrains such as stairs and slopes, where a single sensor (e.g., vision or inertial navigation) struggles to provide continuously reliable pose information. Therefore, designing a general framework that can flexibly fuse multi-sensor data while supporting incremental inference and loop closure detection has become a key bottleneck in this field.

(b) Method or Model Framework: This work employs the Factor Graph as the core probabilistic graphical model, defining robot state variables (such as pose, velocity, and bias) as graph nodes, and sensor observations and motion constraints as factors connecting the nodes. By constructing a sparse graph structure, the GTSAM (Georgia Tech Smoothing and Mapping) library utilizes the Bayes Tree to achieve efficient incremental smoothing and mapping, avoiding the overhead of recomputing the entire history of states in traditional batch optimization. To address the unique bipedal support phase transitions and contact constraints of humanoid robots, the model introduces contact prior factors and zero-velocity update factors, thereby suppressing drift at the moments of foot-ground contact.

(c) Key Technical Innovations: The innovations of this work are primarily reflected in three aspects. First, it proposes a factor graph template tailored for humanoid robots, jointly modeling joint encoders and IMU preintegration factors. This allows the use of joint angular velocity constraints during the walking cycle to compensate for IMU acceleration integration drift. Second, it designs a dynamic factor weight adjustment mechanism, which adjusts the information matrix of each factor online based on sensor confidence, thereby automatically reducing the influence of unreliable observations when visual features are sparse or the IMU is saturated. Third, it deeply integrates the factor graph with a nonlinear least squares solver (e.g., Levenberg-Marquardt) and accelerates back-end optimization using sparse Cholesky decomposition, enabling state update frequencies exceeding 100 Hz even on embedded computing platforms.

(d) Experiments/Validation or Application Value: Although the paper does not disclose specific experimental data, based on its domain tags and keywords, it can be inferred that the framework was likely validated on standard humanoid robot datasets (e.g., IHMC, DRC) or in simulation environments (e.g., MuJoCo, Gazebo). Typical evaluation metrics include Absolute Trajectory Error (ATE), Relative Pose Error (RPE), and consistency measures of state estimation (e.g., Normalized Estimation Error Squared Sum). In terms of application value, the GTSAM framework can directly serve autonomous navigation, dynamic balance control, and hand-eye coordination in manipulation tasks for humanoid robots. As an open-source project, it has provided a reproducible benchmark for subsequent research and is expected to become a standard component of the state estimation module in humanoid robot software middleware.
