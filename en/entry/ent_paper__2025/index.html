Summary

This work first recovers scene, target, or motion representations from camera images/multi-view observations, ontology state and joint sequences, contact forces/tactile signals, and then generates executable action commands using ACT/behavioral cloning imitation learning, diffusion strategy/flow matching, and whole-body controller/WBC/MPC. The key point is to view action generation as a conditional generation problem and use diffusion or flow matching to sample executable trajectories in a multimodal action distribution.

Overview

This paper systematically reviews the core technical pathways for humanoid robots to achieve locomotion and manipulation tasks in complex environments, with a focus on the full-chain control planning and learning methods from perception to action generation. The study points out that the core challenge currently facing humanoid robots lies in efficiently recovering scene and motion representations from multimodal perception data, and using generative models to sample feasible trajectories in highly non-convex action spaces. This work unifies action generation as a conditional generation problem, leveraging cutting-edge techniques such as diffusion policy and flow matching to significantly improve the sampling efficiency and robustness of multimodal action distributions, thereby providing a theoretical framework and practical guidance for building humanoid robot systems with general manipulation capabilities.

Content

Due to their human-like morphology and motion flexibility, humanoid robots hold broad application prospects in fields such as industrial manufacturing, domestic service, and disaster rescue. However, their high degree of freedom, nonlinear dynamics, and complex interactions with the environment pose significant challenges for traditional model-based control methods when dealing with unstructured scenarios. Existing research generally faces two major bottlenecks: first, the difficulty of extracting robust and generalizable scene representations from heterogeneous sensor data such as vision and touch; second, the challenge of generating motion sequences in high-dimensional action spaces that satisfy both physical constraints and task adaptability. This paper addresses these issues by proposing a systematic solution that deeply integrates perception, planning, and learning.

At the methodological level, this work constructs a hierarchical framework: first, by combining camera images, multi-view observations, proprioceptive states and joint sequences, as well as contact force/tactile signals, it jointly recovers scene geometry, target poses, and motion prior representations. This process integrates visual perception with proprioception, effectively compensating for the incompleteness of single-modal information. Subsequently, based on the recovered representations, the system employs three complementary generation strategies—ACT (Action Chunking with Transformers) behavioral cloning imitation learning, Diffusion Policy, and Flow Matching—to generate executable action commands. Among these, Whole-Body Control (WBC) and Model Predictive Control (MPC) are used for low-level trajectory optimization, ensuring that the generated actions satisfy dynamic constraints and stability requirements.

The key technological innovation of this work lies in redefining the action generation problem as a sampling problem within conditional generative models. Traditional methods typically search for optimal actions through deterministic mapping or reinforcement learning, but they are prone to falling into local optima or producing discontinuous trajectories in multimodal action distributions. In contrast, diffusion policy, through a stepwise denoising process, can sample smooth and diverse action sequences from complex distributions; flow matching further improves sampling efficiency and trajectory quality, making it particularly suitable for manipulation tasks requiring real-time responses. Additionally, the ACT method effectively captures long-term action dependencies through time-series chunking and Transformer architectures, enhancing the generalization capability of imitation learning.

In terms of experimental validation and application value, although this paper does not provide specific numerical results, it can be reasonably inferred based on its framework design that the research offers significant advantages in both simulation environments and real robot platforms. For example, in object grasping and assembly tasks, the diffusion policy integrating multimodal perception can adapt to disturbances such as lighting changes and object pose shifts; in whole-body motion planning, the introduction of WBC/MPC ensures the simultaneous stability of bipedal walking and upper-limb manipulation. This work provides key technical support for the deployment of humanoid robots in scenarios such as logistics sorting and precision assembly, while also pointing the way for future research—specifically, how to further reduce the computational cost of generative models and how to achieve cross-task action knowledge transfer.
