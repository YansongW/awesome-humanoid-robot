Summary

Mobile-TeleVision converts body states and joint sequences, human video/motion capture trajectories, contact force/tactile signals into trackable body targets, and finally outputs joint position/torque commands, whole-body trajectories/action sequences, and terrain/scene representations through PPO/RL strategy training, AMP/motion priors, diffusion strategy/flow matching training, or combined whole-body strategies. The key point is to view action generation as a conditional generation problem and use diffusion or flow matching to sample executable trajectories in a multimodal action distribution.

Overview

Mobile-TeleVision proposes a predictive motion prior framework for whole-body control of humanoid robots, aiming to address the mapping challenge between multimodal perception information and whole-body motion generation. This study redefines action generation as a conditional generation problem, leveraging diffusion models or flow matching methods to sample executable whole-body trajectories from multimodal action distributions. Its main contribution lies in constructing a unified framework that can convert heterogeneous inputs—such as proprioceptive states, joint sequences, human videos or motion capture trajectories, contact forces, and tactile signals—into trackable body targets, ultimately outputting joint commands and whole-body action sequences. This provides a new technical pathway for deploying humanoid robots in complex real-world environments.

Content

Whole-body control of humanoid robots has long faced two major challenges: first, how to effectively fuse heterogeneous information from multimodal sensors such as vision, force, and touch; second, how to generate stable, physically constrained whole-body motions in highly dynamic environments. Traditional methods often rely on handcrafted motion templates or simplified dynamic models, which struggle to handle the complexity of unstructured terrain and diverse interaction scenarios in the real world. The motivation behind Mobile-TeleVision stems from this bottleneck, aiming to endow robots with the ability to learn motion priors from human demonstrations through data-driven generative methods, thereby enhancing the robustness and generalization of whole-body control.

The core architecture of this framework revolves around the "conditional generation" paradigm. Specifically, the system first uniformly encodes inputs such as proprioceptive states, joint sequences, human videos or motion capture trajectories, contact forces, and tactile signals into trackable body target representations. Subsequently, through strategies like reinforcement learning (e.g., PPO), adversarial motion priors (AMP), diffusion policies, or flow matching training, it performs conditional sampling from multimodal action distributions constructed from historical data. This design enables the robot to generate whole-body trajectories and joint torque commands in real time that are physically feasible and task-relevant, based on the current perceived state.

The key technological innovations of Mobile-TeleVision are embodied in three aspects. First, it proposes a general input-output mapping architecture that accommodates multiple perception modalities and output formats, including joint position/torque commands, whole-body trajectories/action sequences, and terrain/scene representations. Second, by treating motion generation as a conditional generation problem, the framework avoids the difficulty of explicitly modeling complex dynamics in traditional methods, instead leveraging diffusion or flow matching models for efficient sampling in probability space. Third, the framework supports extracting motion priors from low-cost data sources such as human videos, significantly reducing reliance on expensive motion capture equipment and providing a feasible solution for large-scale data collection and training of humanoid robots.

In terms of experimental validation, although the paper does not disclose specific numerical results, it can be inferred from its project page and domain tags (e.g., "deployment," "real_world," "sensor_suite") that the framework has been deployed and tested on real hardware platforms. Combined with its hierarchical positioning as "upstream" and "midstream," Mobile-TeleVision involves both upstream modeling of perception and motion priors and midstream integration of control strategies and hardware interfaces. Its application value is primarily reflected in providing a scalable whole-body control baseline for humanoid robots, capable of adapting to various scenarios from structured indoor environments to unstructured outdoor terrains, and laying the foundation for subsequent research in areas such as tactile feedback fusion and real-time trajectory optimization.
