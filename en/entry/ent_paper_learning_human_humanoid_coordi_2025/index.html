Overview

Humanoid robots collaborating with humans to carry objects is a key scenario for service robots moving toward practical applications. However, existing methods often struggle to achieve smooth coordination due to the disconnect between high-level task planning and low-level motion control. The COLA (Coordination Learning Architecture) framework proposed in this paper effectively bridges the gap between high-level goals and low-level actions by integrating PPO/RL policy training with world models/video prediction into a unified training and deployment pipeline. This work jointly recovers scene and motion representations from proprioceptive states, human motion capture trajectories, and simulation interaction data, and introduces closed-loop error correction and human intervention mechanisms, providing a systematic solution for enhancing the robustness and naturalness of human-robot coordination.

Content

In humanoid robot collaborative carrying tasks, the core challenge lies in enabling the robot to understand human intentions and generate coordinated whole-body motions in real time. Traditional methods typically separate high-level task planning (e.g., setting target positions) from low-level motion control (e.g., generating joint trajectories), resulting in planning outcomes that are difficult to directly map to executable action sequences, especially in dynamic interaction scenarios where response delays or motion mismatches occur. COLA is specifically designed to address this "planning-control gap" by achieving end-to-end coordination from perception to action through a unified learning framework.

The COLA model framework comprises three core modules: a representation recovery module, a policy learning module, and a closed-loop execution module. First, the representation recovery module extracts high-level representations such as scene semantics, target poses, and motion primitives from multiple data sources—including robot proprioceptive states and joint sequences, human videos or motion capture trajectories, and interaction data from simulation environments. These representations serve as inputs for policy learning, enabling the model to understand the current interaction state and human intentions.

At the technical innovation level, COLA's key contribution lies in placing PPO/RL policy training and world models/video prediction within the same training and deployment pipeline. Specifically, the world model predicts future visual and state evolutions over several steps, providing forward-looking value estimates for the RL policy; the RL policy then generates whole-body trajectories and action sequences based on the world model's predictions, outputting targets for low-level controllers. This joint training approach allows the policy to simultaneously consider long-term rewards and short-term motion feasibility, significantly reducing the semantic and temporal gaps between high-level planning and low-level execution. Additionally, the framework integrates a closed-loop error correction mechanism, enabling adjustments to actions based on real-time sensor feedback during deployment and supporting human intervention signals to correct robot behavior.

In terms of experimental validation, COLA was tested on collaborative carrying tasks in both simulation environments and real humanoid robot platforms. Results show that compared to baseline methods (e.g., using RL policies alone or relying solely on world model planning), COLA achieves significant improvements in task success rate, human-robot coordination smoothness, and robustness to external disturbances. Particularly in scenarios requiring real-time adaptation to changes in human movement speed or path adjustments, COLA's joint training pipeline generates matching whole-body motions more quickly, reducing force interaction conflicts between humans and robots. This framework provides a deployable technical solution for fields requiring close human-robot collaboration, such as home services and warehouse logistics, and its design philosophy offers valuable insights for building general coordination capabilities in future humanoid robots.
