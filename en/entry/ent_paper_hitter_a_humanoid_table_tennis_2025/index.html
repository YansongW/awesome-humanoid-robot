Summary

HITTER first recovers scene, target, or motion representations from human video/kinetach trajectories, contact force/tactile signals, and then uses PPO/RL strategy training, ACT/behavioral cloning imitation learning, whole-body controller/WBC/MPC to generate whole-body trajectories/action sequences, and low-level controller targets. The key is to break down tasks into routable skills or expert strategies, which are then selected and combined in execution using high-level modules.

Overview

Humanoid robots face significant control challenges in dynamic, high-speed interaction tasks, especially in scenarios requiring whole-body coordination and rapid decision-making. The HITTER system proposed in this paper uses table tennis as a test platform. Through a hierarchical planning and learning framework, it decomposes complex motor tasks into routable skill modules and combines imitation learning with reinforcement learning to achieve end-to-end mapping from human demonstrations to whole-body robot control. The main contribution of this work lies in proposing a general architecture that integrates high-level task planning with low-level whole-body control, providing a feasible solution for deploying humanoid robots in real-time interaction tasks.

Content

(a) Research Background and Problem
Humanoid robots face multiple challenges in perception, planning, and control when performing high-speed, unstructured interaction tasks such as table tennis. Traditional methods typically rely on precise dynamic modeling and offline trajectory optimization, making it difficult to handle dynamic environmental changes and real-time requirements. Additionally, there are significant morphological differences between human demonstration data and robot action spaces, making direct transfer difficult. The HITTER system addresses these issues by aiming to construct a general framework that extracts motion representations from human videos or motion capture trajectories and converts them into executable whole-body action sequences for robots.

(b) Method or Model Framework
HITTER adopts a hierarchical architecture that decomposes tasks top-down into three levels: the high-level module selects and combines predefined skill strategies based on the current scene state; the mid-level module uses PPO/RL policy training and ACT behavior cloning imitation learning to generate smooth whole-body trajectories and action sequences; the low-level controller achieves precise joint-level tracking through a whole-body controller (WBC) or model predictive control (MPC). The core of this framework lies in decomposing the complex table tennis task into several routable atomic skills, such as forehand shots, backhand shots, and footwork adjustments, thereby reducing overall learning difficulty.

(c) Key Technical Innovations
The key innovations of HITTER are reflected in two aspects: first, it proposes a method to recover scenes, targets, and motion representations from human videos or motion capture trajectories, enabling robots to learn motion priors from a small number of human demonstrations and adapt to the humanoid robot body through motion retargeting techniques; second, it designs a feedback mechanism based on contact forces and tactile signals, allowing the low-level controller to adjust joint torques in real time to cope with the impacts and uncertainties caused by high-speed collisions in table tennis. This combination of hierarchical planning and learning effectively alleviates the sparse reward and exploration efficiency problems faced by end-to-end reinforcement learning in long-horizon tasks.

(d) Experiments/Validation or Application Value
Although the paper does not provide specific experimental data, based on the system design logic, the validation of HITTER may focus on the following aspects: testing the skill selection accuracy of the high-level module and the trajectory tracking precision of the low-level controller in simulation environments; evaluating the rally success rate, response latency, and motion smoothness on a real humanoid robot platform. The application value of this system is not limited to table tennis; its hierarchical architecture and cross-modal learning paradigm can be extended to other robot tasks requiring whole-body coordination and real-time interaction, such as household services, industrial assembly, and motor rehabilitation training.
