Summary

The implementation path of TrajBooster is to first encode camera images/multi-view observations, ontology states and joint sequences, teleoperation/exoskeleton data into multimodal representations, and then use ACT/behavioral cloning imitation learning, VLA multimodal action model, IK/action redirection to predict whole-body trajectories/action sequences, and end effector/wrist targets. The key point is to preserve the semantic understanding of VLM while adding robot states and action heads to avoid staying only at language planning.

Overview

When humanoid robots perform whole-body manipulation tasks in complex environments, they face the challenge of deeply coupling visual perception, motion planning, and semantic understanding. Existing methods often use Vision-Language Models (VLMs) for high-level task planning while handling motion control independently, resulting in a significant gap between semantic information and action execution. TrajBooster proposes a trajectory-centric learning framework that unifies multimodal observations, proprioceptive states, and joint sequences into a single encoding, and integrates behavioral cloning with Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models to achieve end-to-end mapping from scene understanding to whole-body trajectory generation. The core contribution of this work lies in preserving the semantic reasoning capability of VLMs while introducing robot state and action heads, enabling the model to directly output executable whole-body action sequences, thereby overcoming the limitation of traditional methods that only stay at the level of language planning.

Content

(a) Research Background and Problem
Whole-body manipulation for humanoid robots requires coordinating the arms, torso, and lower limbs while maintaining balance, with control complexity far exceeding that of fixed-base robotic arms. In recent years, Vision-Language Models have made significant progress in task understanding and scene reasoning, but most works use them to generate high-level instructions or symbolic plans, subsequently relying on independently designed motion planners (e.g., inverse kinematics solvers) to convert plans into actual actions. This "plan-then-execute" paradigm has two key issues: first, semantic information is diluted during transmission; second, motion planners struggle to adapt to real-time changes in dynamic environments. TrajBooster directly addresses this "semantic-action gap" by exploring how models can learn whole-body trajectories directly from visual and proprioceptive perception.

(b) Method or Model Framework
The overall architecture of TrajBooster centers on a multimodal encoder, which first uniformly encodes camera images (or multi-view observations), robot proprioceptive states (joint angles, end-effector poses), and demonstration data collected via teleoperation or exoskeletons into multimodal representations. On this basis, the model adopts two complementary learning paths: first, behavioral cloning imitation learning based on ACT (Action Chunking with Transformers) to capture temporal dependencies in demonstration trajectories; second, a VLA (Vision-Language-Action) multimodal action model that fuses visual features with language instructions to directly predict whole-body trajectories and end-effector goals. Additionally, the framework integrates inverse kinematics (IK) and action retargeting modules to map predicted abstract actions into specific joint spaces, ensuring the physical feasibility of output trajectories.

(c) Key Technical Innovations
The key innovations of TrajBooster are reflected in three aspects. First, it proposes a "trajectory-centric" learning paradigm, elevating action prediction from discrete step-level decisions to continuous trajectory-level generation, thereby better maintaining motion smoothness and temporal consistency. Second, while preserving the semantic understanding capability of VLMs, the model adds robot state encoding and action heads, enabling the Vision-Language Model to directly output joint-level or end-effector-level control signals, avoiding the information loss between language planning and motion control in traditional methods. Third, the framework supports learning from teleoperation data, using whole-body demonstrations collected via exoskeletons or teaching pendants to provide high-quality supervision signals, thereby reducing reliance on large-scale real robot data collection.

(d) Experiments/Validation or Application Value
Although the abstract does not provide specific quantitative experimental results, it can be inferred from the method design that TrajBooster is suitable for mobile manipulation tasks requiring whole-body coordination, such as grasping objects on the ground, opening doors, or carrying large items. Its application value is reflected in two aspects: first, end-to-end learning reduces the debugging cost of multiple independent modules in traditional pipelines; second, leveraging the semantic understanding capability of VLMs allows the robot to adaptively adjust whole-body actions based on natural language instructions or scene changes. The modular design of the framework also facilitates future extensions, such as incorporating more refined hand manipulation models or integrating tactile feedback. From the perspective of domain labeling, TrajBooster sits at the "mid-level" of intelligence, connecting high-level scene understanding with low-level motion control, and is expected to become one of the foundational frameworks for whole-body manipulation tasks in humanoid robots.
