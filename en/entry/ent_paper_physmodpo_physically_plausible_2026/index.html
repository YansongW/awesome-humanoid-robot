Overview

This paper proposes a framework called PhysMoDPO, aimed at addressing the issue that text-generated humanoid robot motions are not directly executable in the physical world. The primary motivation of the research is that existing text-based motion sequences often lack physical plausibility, leading to defects such as foot sliding, penetration, or violations of dynamic constraints when applied in simulations or on real robots. The core contribution of this work lies in introducing a preference optimization mechanism that "polishes" generated motions into physically executable movements, thereby bridging the gap between text-driven motion generation and physical simulation.

Content

Research Background and Problem

Motion generation for humanoid robots has long been a challenging intersection of robotics and computer animation. In recent years, methods for generating motions based on text descriptions have made significant progress, enabling the synthesis of diverse motion sequences from natural language. However, these generated motions typically focus only on visual plausibility while neglecting physical constraints, such as joint torque limits, ground reaction forces, and conservation of momentum. When these motions are directly transferred to physical simulation environments or real robots, they often result in motion failure or unnatural poses. Therefore, how to transform text-generated motions into executable movements that are both semantically consistent and compliant with physical laws has become a core challenge in current research.

Method or Model Framework

PhysMoDPO proposes a two-stage framework. In the first stage, existing text-to-motion generation models (e.g., based on diffusion or autoregressive methods) are used to produce initial motion sequences. In the second stage, a preference optimization strategy is introduced, constructing a "preference" evaluator within a physical simulation environment to rank and filter the physical feasibility of generated motions. This evaluator assigns preference labels to each motion sample based on physical simulation results (e.g., whether the robot falls, whether joint torques exceed limits, or whether ground contact is reasonable). These preference data are then used to fine-tune the motion generation model, gradually steering it to output physically more plausible motions.

Key Technical Innovations

The core technical innovation of this paper lies in introducing preference learning into the field of physical animation. Unlike traditional post-processing optimization based on reinforcement learning or physical simulation, PhysMoDPO directly incorporates physical constraints into the training phase of the generation model, avoiding cumbersome iterative optimization processes. Specifically, the method designs a lightweight physical preference reward function that efficiently evaluates the physical consistency of motions and optimizes the generation strategy using a contrastive learning framework. Additionally, the framework is model-agnostic and can be adapted to various existing text-to-motion generation architectures, thereby enhancing the generality of the approach.

Experiments/Validation or Application Value

Although the paper does not provide specific numerical experimental data, based on its technical approach, it can be inferred that PhysMoDPO is likely to be validated on standard humanoid robot motion datasets (e.g., Human3.6M or AMASS). Experiments may evaluate the effectiveness by comparing metrics such as the success rate, energy consumption, and motion naturalness of motions in physical simulation before and after optimization. In terms of practical applications, this work holds significant value for humanoid robot task execution (e.g., walking, grasping, interaction), enabling robots to perform complex motions driven by natural language instructions more safely and efficiently, thereby advancing the deployment of humanoid robots in service, rescue, and industrial scenarios.
