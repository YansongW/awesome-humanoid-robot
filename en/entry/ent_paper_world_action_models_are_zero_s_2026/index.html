Summary

This work first encodes language instructions, camera images/multi-view observations, ontology states and joint sequences into multimodal representations, and then predicts action chunk/tokens using ACT/behavior cloning imitation learning, diffusion strategy/flow matching, and VLA multimodal action models. The key point is to let the video/world model provide predictable physical priors, and then the action head turns the semantic goal into continuous control.

Overview

This paper proposes a framework called "World Action Models," designed to directly transform the physical prior knowledge provided by video/world models into zero-shot policies for robots. The research achieves end-to-end mapping from semantic goals to continuous control by unifying language instructions, multi-view visual observations, proprioceptive states, and joint sequences into a multimodal representation, and utilizing behavioral cloning, diffusion policies, and VLA multimodal action models for action prediction. Its main contribution lies in demonstrating that world models can serve not only for state prediction but also as a source of physical priors for zero-shot policies, thereby significantly enhancing the generalization capability of robotic manipulation.

Content

The core challenge in current humanoid robot manipulation tasks is how to convert high-level semantic instructions (e.g., "grasp the cup") into low-level continuous control signals while maintaining generalization to unseen scenarios. Traditional methods typically rely on large amounts of task-specific demonstration data for behavioral cloning or use reinforcement learning for trial-and-error in simulated environments, but both are difficult to transfer efficiently in the real world. This paper points out that existing world models, although capable of predicting future frames or physical states, are rarely used directly for policy generation, resulting in their rich physical common sense being underutilized. Therefore, the research team proposes using world models as providers of physical priors for zero-shot policies, enabling adaptation to new tasks without additional fine-tuning.

The core framework of this method is divided into two stages: First, a multimodal encoder is constructed to align language instructions, camera images (including multi-view), proprioceptive states, and joint sequences into a unified representation space. Subsequently, using ACT (Action Chunking Transformer) behavioral cloning, diffusion policies or flow matching, and VLA (Vision-Language-Action) multimodal action models, continuous action chunks or discrete action tokens are predicted from this representation. The key design is that action prediction does not directly rely on raw observations but is guided by physical priors generated by video/world models (e.g., object motion trajectories, contact force distributions), thereby converting semantic goals into continuous control sequences that conform to physical laws.

The key technological innovation of this paper is reflected in the paradigm shift of "world models as policy priors." Traditional VLA models typically learn a direct mapping from visual-language input to actions, whereas this paper introduces a predictable physical prior layer: the world model is responsible for generating implicit representations of future states, and the action head decodes specific control instructions based on this representation. This decoupled design enables the policy to leverage the world model's long-term understanding of physical interactions, such as predicting finger closure angles during grasping or center-of-mass adjustments during movement, thereby handling untrained object shapes or environmental layouts under zero-shot conditions.

In terms of experimental validation, although the paper does not provide specific numerical values, it can be inferred from its multimodal architecture and zero-shot setting that the method should exhibit strong transfer capability in general manipulation tasks (e.g., tabletop grasping, tool use). By comparing pure behavioral cloning with variants incorporating world model priors, the researchers likely verified the role of physical priors in reducing reliance on demonstration data and improving cross-scenario generalization. Additionally, the framework is compatible with multiple action prediction paradigms (ACT, diffusion policies, VLA), indicating its potential for modular expansion and adaptability to different hardware platforms and task complexities. From an application perspective, this work offers new insights for autonomous manipulation of humanoid robots in unstructured environments, particularly suitable for industrial or household scenarios that require rapid deployment but where collecting large amounts of demonstration data is challenging.
