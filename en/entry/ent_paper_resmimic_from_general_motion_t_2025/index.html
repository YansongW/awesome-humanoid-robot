Overview

Achieving whole-body coordinated locomotion and manipulation (Loco-Manipulation) for humanoid robots in complex environments has long been a core challenge in robotics. The difficulty lies in bridging the semantic gap between high-level task planning and low-level motion control. Existing methods typically treat motion tracking and manipulation control as separate processes, leading to significant performance degradation when policies are transferred from simulation to real-world scenarios. To address this, the paper proposes the ResMimic framework, which introduces a residual learning mechanism to integrate the policy network and control module into a unified training and deployment pipeline. This effectively reduces the discontinuities between high-level objectives and low-level actions, enabling efficient transfer from general motion tracking to whole-body loco-manipulation.

Content

In terms of research background and problem, whole-body loco-manipulation for humanoid robots faces two core challenges: first, how to extract robust motion representations from diverse heterogeneous data sources (e.g., human videos, motion capture trajectories, simulation interaction data); second, how to ensure that action sequences planned by high-level policies can be precisely executed by low-level controllers. Traditional methods often design motion retargeting and manipulation control as independent modules, leading to discontinuities between the abstract instructions output by the policy network and the joint-level commands at the motor level, resulting in issues such as action jitter and task failure. ResMimic is specifically designed to address this "policy-control discontinuity" problem.

At the methodological or model framework level, ResMimic constructs an end-to-end residual learning architecture. Its core process consists of two stages: first, the system recovers scene, object, or motion representations from proprioceptive states and joint sequences, human videos/motion capture trajectories, and simulation interaction data, forming a unified implicit representation; subsequently, the policy network and control module are jointly optimized within the same training pipeline. The policy network generates coarse-grained plans for whole-body trajectories, while the control module performs fine-grained corrections on these plans through residual learning. This design allows high-level semantic objectives (e.g., "grasp an object") to be directly mapped to low-level joint torque commands, avoiding information loss inherent in traditional hierarchical architectures.

Key technological innovations are primarily reflected in three aspects. First, the introduction of the residual learning mechanism enables the control module to focus on compensating for deviations between the policy network's output and real physical dynamics, rather than learning the entire control mapping from scratch, significantly improving sample efficiency and generalization capability. Second, ResMimic supports the fusion of multimodal data sources, simultaneously leveraging semantic information from human demonstration videos and physical constraints from simulation environments, thereby generating feasible actions even in the absence of accurate dynamic models. Third, the framework places the policy network and control module within the same training/deployment pipeline, achieving end-to-end gradient propagation from perception to action, fundamentally eliminating discontinuities between modules.

In terms of experimental validation and application value, ResMimic was evaluated on various whole-body loco-manipulation tasks for humanoid robots, including grasping objects during dynamic walking and operating tools after crossing obstacles. Experimental results show that compared to baseline methods (e.g., independently trained motion retargeting and controller combinations), ResMimic achieves significant improvements in task success rate, action smoothness, and robustness to external disturbances. Furthermore, the framework has low dependency on data sources; even when using only monocular video as input, it can generate whole-body action sequences with reasonable physical characteristics, providing a feasible path for deploying humanoid robots in unstructured environments. Its application value extends beyond laboratory settings to fields requiring whole-body coordinated operations, such as disaster rescue, home services, and industrial collaboration.
