Summary

Make Tracking Easy converts the body state with joint sequences, contact force/tactile signals into trackable body targets, and finally outputs whole-body trajectories/action sequences, low-level controller targets, and terrain/scene representations through PPO/RL policy training, ACT/behavior cloning imitation learning, diffusion policy/flow matching training, or combined whole-body policies. The key point is to view action generation as a conditional generation problem and use diffusion or flow matching to sample executable trajectories in a multimodal action distribution.

Overview

This paper proposes a neural motion retargeting framework called "Make Tracking Easy," aimed at addressing the core challenge of action tracking difficulty in whole-body control of humanoid robots. The primary motivation of this work is that traditional methods struggle to directly convert high-dimensional, multimodal proprioceptive data (such as joint sequences, contact forces, and tactile signals) into executable and stable whole-body trajectories. The core contribution lies in redefining action generation as a conditional generation problem, utilizing diffusion models or flow matching techniques to efficiently sample trackable body targets from multimodal action distributions, thereby significantly reducing the difficulty of learning and deploying whole-body control policies.

Content

Whole-body control of humanoid robots faces the dual challenges of action tracking accuracy and motion stability. Traditional control methods typically rely on precise dynamic models and handcrafted trackers, but in complex terrains or dynamic interaction scenarios, model errors and multi-joint coupling effects can easily lead to tracking failures. Although existing reinforcement learning or imitation learning methods can learn robust policies, they often require extensive engineering tuning to balance tracking weights across different body parts (such as the torso, limbs, and foot contacts). Therefore, how to automatically transform raw perceptual signals (e.g., joint angles, force sensor readings) into physically consistent body targets understandable by low-level controllers has become a key bottleneck in enhancing the locomotion capabilities of humanoid robots.

The "Make Tracking Easy" framework proposed in this paper centers on neural motion retargeting, constructing an end-to-end pipeline from perception to execution. This pipeline first encodes proprioceptive states, joint sequences, and contact force/tactile signals into a unified implicit representation, then maps them to trackable body targets (such as desired joint trajectories or center-of-mass positions) via a conditional generative model. This design avoids the cumbersome process of explicit kinematic inverse solving in traditional retargeting, allowing the system to flexibly adapt to different robot hardware morphologies and motion patterns.

At the model architecture level, the framework treats action generation as a conditional generation problem and employs diffusion policies or flow matching methods to sample executable trajectories from multimodal action distributions. Specifically, during the training phase, paradigms such as PPO/RL policy optimization, ACT behavior cloning, or diffusion policy training enable the model to learn to generate action sequences that satisfy physical constraints under given perceptual conditions. Here, diffusion models recover smooth whole-body trajectories from random noise through a gradual denoising process, while flow matching uses continuous normalizing flows to model the complex multimodal characteristics of action distributions, effectively handling multiple feasible strategies in humanoid robot motion (such as different gaits or contact patterns).

Key technological innovations are reflected in three aspects: First, a unified perception-action representation is proposed, fusing joint sequences, force/tactile signals, and terrain scene information into conditional inputs, enhancing the model's adaptability to dynamic environments. Second, the conditional generation framework enables efficient sampling of action distributions, avoiding the over-reliance on a single optimal trajectory in traditional methods and improving policy robustness. Third, the framework supports flexible combinations of multiple learning paradigms (RL, behavior cloning, diffusion policies), allowing the selection of optimal training strategies for different task requirements (e.g., fast walking, traversing complex terrains). In terms of experimental validation, the project page demonstrates tracking results on simulation environments and real humanoid robot platforms, indicating that the method outperforms baseline approaches in motion smoothness, contact stability, and task success rate, providing a reproducible technical pathway for the practical application of whole-body control.
