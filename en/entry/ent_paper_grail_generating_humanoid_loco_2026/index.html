Summary

Grail first recovers scenes, objects, or motion representations from camera images/multi-view observations, ontological states and joint sequences, human video/dynamic capture trajectories, and the visual representation of the object, end-effector/wrist-hand targets were generated using ACT/behavior clone imitation learning, diffusion strategy/flow matching, World Model/video prediction. The key point is to treat action generation as a conditional generation problem, and use diffusion or flow matching to sample executable trajectories in multimodal action distributions.

Overview

GRAIL proposes a mobile manipulation generation framework for humanoid robots, aiming to address the cross-modal mapping problem from unstructured environmental information to complex manipulation actions. The main contribution of this research lies in modeling action generation as a conditional generation problem. By integrating 3D asset reconstruction with video prior knowledge, the robot can sample executable trajectories from multimodal action distributions. This approach provides a new technical pathway for enhancing the autonomous manipulation capabilities of humanoid robots in real-world scenarios.

Content

Research Background and Problem
Mobile manipulation tasks for humanoid robots require the robot to simultaneously possess capabilities in environmental perception, object interaction, and whole-body coordinated control. Existing methods typically rely on precise dynamic models or large amounts of manually annotated demonstration data, making it difficult to handle the diversity of objects, dynamic environmental changes, and the multimodal nature of action distributions in real-world scenarios. The core challenge of GRAIL lies in how to recover 3D representations of scenes and objects from limited visual observations and leverage prior knowledge to generate manipulation trajectories that comply with physical constraints.

Method or Model Framework
The overall framework of GRAIL is divided into two stages: First, from camera images, multi-view observations, proprioceptive states and joint sequences, as well as human videos or motion capture trajectories, it recovers scene, object, or motion representations. This process integrates 3D reconstruction and visual perception techniques, transforming unstructured inputs into structured spatial and motion descriptions. Second, based on the recovered representations, it employs ACT (Behavioral Cloning) or imitation learning strategies, combined with diffusion policies, flow matching, and world models or video prediction methods, to generate target poses for the end-effector and wrist-hand. Action generation is explicitly treated as a conditional generation problem, sampling from multimodal action distributions via diffusion or flow matching models to obtain executable trajectories.

Key Technical Innovations
The technical innovations of GRAIL are mainly reflected in three aspects: First, it decouples visual perception from action generation, allowing the scene understanding module to be optimized independently of specific control strategies. Second, it leverages the generative capabilities of diffusion models and flow matching to effectively handle the multimodal distribution characteristics of manipulation actions, avoiding the generalization deficiencies caused by single action patterns in traditional methods. Third, it introduces world models and video prediction as prior constraints, ensuring that the generated trajectories maintain physical plausibility and motion smoothness over time. This design enables GRAIL to extract manipulation intentions from human demonstration videos and transfer them to the robot's own body.

Experiments/Validation and Application Value
Although specific experimental data are not detailed in the metadata, based on the framework design, it can be inferred that GRAIL is suitable for scenarios requiring direct generation of manipulation commands from visual inputs, such as home services, warehouse sorting, and industrial assembly. Its core advantage lies in reducing reliance on manual programming and precise modeling, thereby enhancing the robot's adaptability in unknown environments. This research provides a reusable paradigm for combining 3D visual understanding with generative action planning, offering valuable insights for advancing humanoid robots from the laboratory to practical applications.
