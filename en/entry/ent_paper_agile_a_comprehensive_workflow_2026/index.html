Summary

AGILE first recovers scene, target or motion representations from camera images/multi-view observations, ontology state and joint sequences, simulation interaction data, and then generates low-level controller targets using PPO/RL strategy training, whole-body controller/WBC/MPC, hierarchical skills/expert strategy. The key is to break down tasks into routable skills or expert strategies, which are then selected and combined in execution using high-level modules.

Overview

Humanoid robots performing mobile manipulation tasks in complex environments face highly coupled challenges in perception, planning, and control. Existing methods often treat visual perception and motion control separately, making it difficult to achieve end-to-end robust closed-loop systems. The AGILE workflow proposed in this paper constructs a unified framework from multimodal perception to low-level control, decomposing complex tasks into routable skill modules, and leveraging reinforcement learning along with whole-body control strategies for efficient execution. This workflow has been validated in both simulation and real-world scenarios for its generality and transferability in humanoid robot loco-manipulation learning, providing a systematic technical pathway for building intelligent embodied systems.

Content

Humanoid robot loco-manipulation requires the robot to simultaneously coordinate whole-body motion and end-effector operations, with the core challenge lying in the tight coupling of visual perception, motion planning, and low-level control. Existing research often focuses on individual aspects, such as optimizing walking stability or improving grasping accuracy alone, lacking a systematic solution that integrates scene understanding, task decomposition, and whole-body control into a unified workflow. AGILE addresses this gap by proposing an end-to-end comprehensive learning framework aimed at solving the complete mapping from high-dimensional visual input to low-level joint commands.

The methodological framework of AGILE is divided into three levels: perception representation recovery, policy training, and hierarchical control. First, the system recovers scene geometry, target poses, and motion representations from camera images, multi-view observations, proprioceptive states, and joint sequences, building a semantic and geometric understanding of the environment. Subsequently, reinforcement learning algorithms such as PPO are used to train low-level controllers, combined with whole-body controllers (WBC) and model predictive control (MPC) to generate smooth and stable joint targets. The key to this framework lies in decomposing complex tasks into several routable skills or expert policies, such as "navigate to target," "grasp object," "adjust posture," etc., each corresponding to an independent control policy.

The core technological innovation of AGILE is reflected in its hierarchical skill routing mechanism. The high-level module dynamically selects and combines low-level skills based on the current task state and perception results, achieving task-level behavior orchestration. This design not only reduces the sample complexity of end-to-end learning but also enhances the system's generalization ability to unknown scenarios. Additionally, the framework supports pre-training skills from simulation interaction data and then transferring them to real robots, effectively alleviating the high cost of acquiring real-world data. Vision-guided control, as a key component, ensures that perception errors do not directly accumulate into motion execution.

In terms of experimental validation, AGILE was tested on multiple mobile manipulation tasks in both simulation environments and real humanoid robot platforms, including object transportation, door opening, and obstacle avoidance navigation. Results show that this workflow significantly improves task success rates while reducing joint torque peaks and energy consumption during execution. Compared to baseline methods without hierarchical structures, AGILE achieves approximately a 20% improvement in task success rate and reduces the number of interaction episodes required for policy training. Its application value lies in providing a reusable technical paradigm for deploying humanoid robots in unstructured scenarios such as warehousing and home services, particularly suitable for scenarios requiring rapid adaptation to new tasks and environments.
