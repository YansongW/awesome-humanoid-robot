Overview

This paper introduces Genesis, a research effort in the field of humanoid robots and broader robotics—a physics engine that is both generative and general-purpose. The primary motivation is to address the limitations of existing simulation platforms in terms of diversity, realism, and data generation efficiency, by constructing a unified physical simulation benchmark to provide stronger support for robot learning and control. Its core contribution lies in proposing a framework that simultaneously supports high-fidelity physical simulation and large-scale automatic data generation, which is expected to significantly enhance the generalization capability of humanoid robots in simulation-to-reality transfer.

Content

(a) Research Background and Problem: With the rapid development of humanoid robot technology, the demand for high-precision, high-efficiency simulation environments is becoming increasingly urgent. Traditional physics engines often struggle to balance computational speed with physical realism, and lack general support for complex joint movements, flexible contacts, and diverse scenarios. Moreover, existing simulation platforms typically require extensive manual intervention when generating training data for reinforcement learning or imitation learning, resulting in low automation levels that limit the scale and efficiency of robot skill acquisition. To address these challenges, Genesis aims to design a unified engine that can both provide accurate physical interaction simulation and autonomously generate rich training data, filling a critical gap in the current simulation toolchain.

(b) Method or Model Framework: Genesis adopts a modular architecture design, deeply integrating the physics solver, rendering engine, and data generation pipeline. Its core is a differentiable physics-based simulation core that supports real-time computation of rigid bodies, soft bodies, and hybrid systems, and is optimized for typical humanoid robot tasks such as bipedal balance and whole-body motion control. The framework also incorporates a generative module that can automatically generate scene layouts, object properties, and task trajectories based on task descriptions or environmental constraints, significantly reducing manual configuration costs. The entire system is built on high-performance computing, supporting parallel simulation and distributed training, providing a scalable infrastructure for large-scale robot learning experiments.

(c) Key Technological Innovations: The main innovations of Genesis are reflected in three aspects. First, it achieves the unification of physical simulation and data generation, merging traditionally separate simulation and data annotation processes into an automated workflow, significantly improving R&D efficiency. Second, the engine features "generality," supporting not only humanoid robots but also various robot forms such as wheeled robots, quadruped robots, and robotic arms, with cross-platform migration enabled through a unified interface definition. Finally, Genesis makes a breakthrough in physical accuracy by introducing high-order approximations of contact dynamics and adaptive time-stepping algorithms, which, while maintaining real-time performance, significantly enhance the consistency between simulation results and the real world, providing more reliable support for sim-to-real transfer.

(d) Experiments/Validation or Application Value: Although the paper does not disclose specific quantitative experimental data, given its positioning as a "simulation benchmark," the validation of Genesis likely covers standard humanoid robot motion control tasks such as walking, running, jumping, and object manipulation. Compared with existing mainstream physics engines (e.g., MuJoCo, PyBullet), this work is expected to demonstrate advantages in simulation speed, physical fidelity, and data generation diversity. In practical applications, Genesis is poised to become an important tool for the humanoid robot research community, serving the complete R&D workflow from algorithm prototype validation to large-scale policy training, particularly suitable for reinforcement learning and imitation learning scenarios that require large amounts of diverse training data.
