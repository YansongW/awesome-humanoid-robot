Summary

The implementation path of SONIC is to first encode human video/motion capture trajectories and teleoperation/exoskeleton data into multimodal representations, and then use AMP/motion priors and VLA multimodal motion models to predict whole-body trajectories/motion sequences. The key point is to preserve the semantic understanding of VLM while adding robot states and action heads to avoid staying only at language planning.

Overview

SONIC proposes a motion tracking framework for full-body control of humanoid robots, aiming to address the gap between semantic understanding and physical execution in existing motion generation methods. By integrating human videos, motion capture trajectories, teleoperation data, and wearable exoskeleton signals, this study constructs a multimodal motion representation and introduces a VLA (Vision-Language-Action) multimodal action model, achieving end-to-end mapping from language planning to full-body trajectory prediction. Its main contribution lies in preserving the semantic understanding capability of Vision-Language Models (VLMs) while overcoming the limitation of traditional methods that remain at the language level by incorporating robot state encoding and action heads, providing a new technical pathway for natural and coherent full-body control of humanoid robots.

Content

Research Background and Problem

Full-body control of humanoid robots has long faced two major challenges: first, how to extract generalizable motion priors from diverse human motion data, and second, how to convert high-level semantic instructions (e.g., language descriptions) into low-level joint trajectories. Existing methods either rely on manually designed motion libraries or generate abstract plans solely through language models, lacking modeling of robot dynamics and physical constraints. SONIC addresses this gap by redefining the motion tracking task as a joint optimization problem of "semantic understanding + motion generation," aiming to enable robots not only to understand human intentions but also to generate full-body action sequences that conform to their own structural characteristics.

Method or Model Framework

The overall framework of SONIC consists of three stages: first, an encoder uniformly maps human videos, motion capture (MoCap) trajectories, teleoperation data, and exoskeleton sensor signals into a multimodal motion representation; second, using Adversarial Motion Priors (AMP) and the VLA multimodal action model, it learns motion distribution and semantic alignment in a latent space; finally, based on the robot's current state and action heads, it predicts full-body trajectories and action sequences. The core of this framework lies in coupling the semantic understanding capability of VLMs with the motion generation network, ensuring that the output not only includes joint angles but also implicitly contains task-related semantic constraints.

Key Technical Innovations

The key innovations of SONIC are reflected in three aspects: first, it proposes the concept of "super motion tracking" by integrating multiple heterogeneous data sources (video, MoCap, teleoperation, exoskeleton), significantly enhancing the coverage and robustness of motion priors; second, it designs a network architecture that preserves the semantic understanding capability of VLMs, adding a robot state encoder and dedicated action heads on top of the language model, avoiding the disconnection between language planning and motion execution common in traditional methods; third, it employs AMP as a motion prior constraint to ensure that the generated full-body trajectories are dynamically reasonable and exhibit natural human motion styles, thereby improving the affinity of human-robot interaction.

Experiments/Validation and Application Value

Although the paper does not disclose specific experimental data, the validation path can be inferred from the framework design: by comparing trajectory generation effects with and without VLM semantic guidance, it evaluates motion naturalness and task completion rate; it tests the tracking accuracy and real-time performance of full-body control commands on simulated and real humanoid robot platforms. In terms of application value, SONIC is expected to promote the deployment of humanoid robots in scenarios such as home services, industrial collaboration, and remote operation—for example, robots could directly learn complex manipulation skills by watching human demonstration videos or adjust full-body postures in real time through language instructions, eliminating the need for tedious programming or teaching processes.
