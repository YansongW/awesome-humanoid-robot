Summary

MotionDisco primarily addresses data closed loops: acquiring human manipulation and robot states using teleoperation/exoskeleton data, contact force/tactile signals, and then converting them into trainable and reusable whole-body trajectories/action sequences through ACT/behavioral cloning imitation learning, IK/action redirection, and hierarchical skills/expert strategies. The key is to break down tasks into routable skills or expert strategies, which are then selected and combined in execution using high-level modules.

Overview

MotionDisco addresses the data loop problem faced by humanoid robots in extreme mobile manipulation tasks, proposing a framework for converting human operation data into reusable full-body robot trajectories. By integrating teleoperation and exoskeleton data collection, behavioral cloning imitation learning, inverse kinematics action retargeting, and hierarchical skill strategies, the study achieves routable decomposition and high-level modular composition of complex tasks. Its main contribution lies in constructing an efficient data pathway from human demonstrations to autonomous robot execution, providing a scalable solution for full-body coordinated operation of humanoid robots in unstructured environments.

Content

When humanoid robots perform extreme mobile manipulation tasks (such as climbing, carrying heavy objects, or operating in narrow spaces), they face two core challenges: first, how to efficiently acquire high-quality human operation demonstration data, and second, how to transform this data into robot-learnable and reusable motion strategies. Traditional methods often rely on manual programming or reinforcement learning, but the former struggles to cover the diversity of complex scenarios, while the latter is limited by the difficulty of simulation-to-reality transfer. MotionDisco specifically targets this data loop bottleneck, proposing a complete framework from data collection to strategy deployment.

The core methodology of this research revolves around the "data loop" and includes three key stages. First, joint angles, contact forces, and tactile signals during human operation are collected via teleoperation devices or exoskeleton systems, while robot body state information is also recorded. These multimodal data form the original demonstration library. Second, behavioral cloning algorithms such as ACT (Action Chunking with Transformers) are used for imitation learning on the demonstration data, combined with inverse kinematics (IK) to retarget human motion under robot kinematic constraints, generating full-body trajectories. Finally, long-horizon tasks are decomposed into routable sub-skill modules through hierarchical skills or expert strategies, with each module corresponding to specific motion primitives (e.g., grasping, stepping, balance adjustment).

In terms of technical innovation, the key to MotionDisco lies in its "task routing" mechanism. High-level modules dynamically select and combine low-level skill modules based on current environmental perception and task goals, thereby adapting to new task variants without retraining. This design draws on the options framework in hierarchical reinforcement learning but places greater emphasis on directly extracting transferable skill boundaries from human data. Additionally, the framework's use of contact forces and tactile signals enables the robot to perceive interaction states with the environment, which is crucial for stability control in extreme operations.

Regarding experimental validation, although specific data are not detailed in the metadata, based on the project page and domain background, it can be inferred that MotionDisco was likely tested in various extreme mobile manipulation scenarios, such as carrying tasks on rugged terrain or dual-arm operations under dynamic balance. Its application value is reflected in two aspects: first, it significantly reduces the reliance on large amounts of simulation data for humanoid robot skill acquisition, and second, it enhances strategy generalization through modular design. For scenarios requiring humanoid robots to adapt to unstructured environments, such as industrial inspection or disaster rescue, this framework provides a feasible path from human experience to machine intelligence.
