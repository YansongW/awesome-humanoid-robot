Summary

Handoff transforms ontological states and joint sequences into trackable body targets, and through teacher-student knowledge transfer, diffusion strategy/flow matching, VLM semantic planning/routing training, or combined whole-body strategies, the HANDOFF can be implemented in the context of multi-agent systems, finally, the whole body trajectory/action sequence and low-level controller target are output. The key is to train the teacher's strategy with privileged information and distill the ability to use only the student's strategy of deploying observations.

Overview

To address the challenges of whole-body coordination and perception-action loops in complex tasks for humanoid robots, this paper proposes the HANDOFF framework. It employs a teacher-student knowledge distillation strategy to transfer whole-body control capabilities that rely on privileged information to a student policy that only uses deployable observations. This method innovatively integrates diffusion policy, flow matching, and large vision-language model (VLM) semantic planning, enabling the conversion from proprioceptive states and joint sequences to trackable body targets, ultimately outputting whole-body trajectories and low-level controller commands. HANDOFF provides an efficient and scalable technical pathway for humanoid robots from simulation training to real-world deployment.

Content

Whole-body control of humanoid robots has long been plagued by high-dimensional state spaces and complex dynamic coupling issues. Traditional methods either rely on precise dynamic models or require extensive manual parameter tuning, making them difficult to adapt to mobile manipulation tasks in unstructured environments. Although existing reinforcement learning policies perform well in simulation, their dependence on privileged information (e.g., object mass, ground friction coefficient, future contact forces) hinders direct transfer to real robots that can only access proprioceptive and visual observations. This "sim-to-real" gap is a key bottleneck limiting autonomous operation of humanoid robots.

The core framework of HANDOFF adopts a teacher-student knowledge distillation paradigm. First, a "teacher policy" is trained in simulation using privileged information, capable of generating optimal whole-body joint trajectories and body targets based on complete environmental states. Subsequently, through distillation, the decision-making capability of the teacher policy is compressed into a "student policy" that only accesses observations available during deployment (e.g., joint encoder readings, IMU data, RGB-D images). During distillation, HANDOFF introduces diffusion policy and flow matching techniques, enabling the student policy to efficiently sample from the trajectory distribution of the teacher policy rather than simply fitting the mean, thereby preserving action diversity and robustness.

In terms of technical innovation, HANDOFF proposes the intermediate representation of "trackable body targets," converting high-dimensional proprioceptive states and joint sequences into low-dimensional, physically interpretable reference points (e.g., center of mass position, end-effector pose, torso orientation). This design not only reduces the difficulty of distillation but also allows the student policy to seamlessly interface with low-level whole-body controllers (e.g., QP solvers or impedance controllers). Additionally, HANDOFF integrates large vision-language models (VLMs) for high-level semantic planning and routing training, enabling the robot to understand natural language instructions and decompose them into sub-task sequences, which are then executed stepwise by the whole-body control policy, achieving end-to-end integration from semantics to motion.

In experimental validation, HANDOFF was evaluated on various humanoid robot mobile manipulation tasks, including object grasping, door opening, and carrying. Results show that the distilled student policy, using only deployment observations, can replicate the task success rate of the teacher policy under privileged information, with motion smoothness and execution efficiency close to teacher levels. In zero-shot generalization tests, HANDOFF demonstrated good adaptability to unseen object shapes, ground material variations, and lighting conditions. The significance of this framework lies in providing a practical "simulation learning first, then distillation deployment" route for whole-body control of humanoid robots, significantly reducing debugging costs and safety risks for real robots, and potentially advancing the practical deployment of humanoid robots in scenarios such as home services and industrial assembly.
