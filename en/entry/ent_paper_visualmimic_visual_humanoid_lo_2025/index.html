Summary

VisualMimic first recovers scene, target, or motion representations from camera images/multi-view observations, ontology states and joint sequences, human videos/motion capture trajectories, and then generates executable action commands using teacher-student knowledge transfer and whole-body controllers/WBCs/MPCs. The key point is to train teacher strategies with privileged information and then distill the capabilities to the point where only student strategies with deployed observations can be used.

Overview

VisualMimic proposes a mobile manipulation framework for vision-based humanoid robots, aiming to solve the end-to-end control challenge from visual input to whole-body motion generation. By integrating multi-view images, proprioceptive states, and human motion data, this research achieves joint recovery of scene understanding, object recognition, and motion representation. Its core contribution lies in constructing a teacher-student knowledge distillation paradigm, enabling a student policy that relies only on observation information available during deployment to replicate the complex whole-body coordination capabilities learned by the teacher under privileged information, thereby significantly enhancing the operational robustness and generalization of humanoid robots in unstructured environments.

Content

Humanoid robot loco-manipulation has long faced two major challenges: first, how to extract scene and motion semantics in real-time from high-dimensional visual input; second, how to map abstract motion planning to whole-body joint control with high degrees of freedom and strong coupling characteristics. Existing methods mostly rely on predefined motion primitives or simplified dynamics models, making it difficult to adapt to dynamic environments and complex interaction tasks. VisualMimic addresses this bottleneck by proposing to unify visual perception, motion tracking, and whole-body control within an end-to-end learning framework, thereby avoiding information loss between perception and control modules in traditional pipelines.

The core framework of this method consists of two stages. In the first stage, the teacher policy is trained under privileged information (e.g., precise object poses, joint torques, future trajectories) using whole-body controllers (WBC) or model predictive control (MPC) to generate high-precision action sequences. In the second stage, the student policy can only access observations available during deployment (e.g., monocular or multi-camera images, proprioceptive sensor data) and transfers the decision-making capability of the teacher policy to the student network through imitation learning and knowledge distillation techniques. This design allows the student policy to avoid reliance on expensive state estimation or offline planning during inference, significantly reducing computational overhead and hardware dependency.

In terms of technological innovation, the key to VisualMimic lies in its motion representation and generation mechanism. The system extracts motion priors from human videos or motion capture trajectories, encodes them as implicit motion primitives, and then adapts them to the kinematic and dynamic constraints of humanoid robots through conditional generative models. Additionally, the framework introduces a multimodal fusion module capable of simultaneously processing images, joint sequences, and proprioceptive states, ensuring stable control performance under different observation noise and occlusion conditions. This design enables the robot not only to replicate predefined actions but also to dynamically adjust operational strategies based on real-time visual feedback.

In terms of experimental validation, VisualMimic was evaluated on various typical humanoid robot loco-manipulation tasks, including object grasping, tool use, dynamic obstacle avoidance, and complex terrain walking. Results show that the student policy after knowledge distillation approaches the teacher policy in success rate, motion smoothness, and response speed, while exhibiting strong robustness to sensor noise and lighting variations. The modular design of the framework also supports direct transfer to humanoid robot platforms with different configurations, demonstrating its potential for application in general robot control. From an industrial perspective, VisualMimic provides a feasible path to reduce the dependence of humanoid robot deployment on high-precision motion capture systems and offline computing resources, potentially accelerating its adoption in scenarios such as service, manufacturing, and rescue.
