Summary

HiWET first recovers scene, target, or motion representations from camera images/multi-view observations, ontology state and joint sequences, and simulated interaction data, and then generates end effector/wrist targets using PPO/RL policy training, VLM semantic planning/routing, and hierarchical skills/expert policies. The key is to break down tasks into routable skills or expert strategies, which are then selected and combined in execution using high-level modules.

Overview

HiWET (Hierarchical World-coordinate End-effector Tracking) addresses the coupling challenge between perception and planning faced by humanoid robots in long-horizon mobile manipulation tasks, proposing a hierarchical tracking framework based on the world coordinate system. The core contribution of this work lies in decomposing complex mobile manipulation tasks into routable skills or expert policies, and enabling real-time selection and combination through high-level modules, thereby achieving an end-to-end closed loop from visual perception to end-effector trajectory generation. HiWET integrates reinforcement learning policy training, visual language model semantic planning, and a hierarchical skill library, providing a new technical pathway for autonomous operation of humanoid robots in unstructured environments.

Content

Research Background and Problem

Mobile manipulation (loco-manipulation) for humanoid robots requires simultaneous execution of fine-grained upper-limb operations while walking or standing, imposing extremely high demands on the coordination of perception, planning, and control. Existing methods typically separate locomotion from manipulation or rely on predefined trajectory libraries, making it difficult to handle dynamic environmental changes in long-horizon, multi-step tasks. The core problem addressed by HiWET is: how to recover scene and target representations in real time using multimodal observations (camera images, proprioceptive states, joint sequences) without precise environmental models, and generate stable end-effector tracking commands. This problem represents a key bottleneck in the "perception-planning-control" closed loop in robotics, particularly pronounced in high-degree-of-freedom, highly dynamic systems like humanoid robots.

Method or Model Framework

HiWET constructs a hierarchical architecture. The low-level layer is responsible for recovering scene, target, or motion representations from camera images, multi-view observations, and simulation interaction data. The mid-level layer employs reinforcement learning policy training based on Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) to generate the position and orientation of the end-effector or wrist-hand target. The high-level layer introduces a Visual Language Model (VLM) for semantic planning and routing, decomposing long-horizon tasks into a series of executable sub-skills or expert policies. The core logic of the entire framework is: tasks are decomposed into routable skill units, and the high-level module selects and combines these skills in real time based on the current perceptual state, thereby dynamically generating complete operation sequences at runtime.

Key Technical Innovations

The technical innovations of HiWET are primarily reflected in three aspects. First, it proposes the concept of "world-coordinate end-effector tracking," converting the target position of the end-effector from the camera coordinate system or robot base coordinate system to the global world coordinate system, thereby eliminating reference frame drift caused by robot movement. Second, it designs a hierarchical skill routing mechanism that allows the high-level VLM module to dynamically invoke low-level reinforcement learning policies based on semantic understanding, achieving organic integration of symbolic planning and continuous control. Third, by pre-training scene representation models using simulation interaction data, the system can generalize to unseen objects and layouts from limited real-world data, reducing data collection costs. These innovations collectively address the issues of error accumulation and policy rigidity commonly encountered by traditional methods in long-horizon tasks.

Experiments/Validation and Application Value

Although the paper does not provide specific experimental data, it can be inferred from the method design that HiWET is suitable for scenarios requiring prolonged continuous operation, such as home services (organizing items, opening doors to retrieve objects), industrial inspection (operating valves, transporting parts), and disaster rescue (traversing rubble, operating tools). Its hierarchical architecture is particularly well-suited for tasks that require balancing locomotion stability and manipulation precision. In terms of application value, HiWET offers an extensible perception-planning-control paradigm for next-generation humanoid robot operating systems. Especially in human-robot collaboration environments, the world-coordinate tracking mechanism enables robots to better understand spatial semantics in human instructions. Additionally, the modular design of this framework facilitates the future integration of more advanced visual perception models or motion planning algorithms.
