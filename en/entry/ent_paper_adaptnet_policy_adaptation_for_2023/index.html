Overview

AdaptNet is a research work on physics-based character control for humanoid robots, aiming to address the limited generalization ability of traditional physics-based character animation methods when handling diverse locomotion tasks. This study proposes a policy adaptation framework that dynamically couples motion control policies with task environment features, significantly enhancing the locomotion stability and adaptability of humanoid characters under complex physical conditions. Its main contribution lies in designing a lightweight network structure that maintains real-time control efficiency while enabling rapid policy adjustments for different terrains, external disturbances, and motion goals.

Content

(a) Research Background and Problem: In the interdisciplinary field of robotics and computer animation, physics-based character control has always been a key challenge for achieving human-like locomotion. Traditional methods typically rely on manually designed kinematic models or offline-optimized trajectory libraries, making it difficult to handle dynamic changes in unstructured environments. Particularly for high-degree-of-freedom systems like humanoid robots, motion control must simultaneously balance stability maintenance, energy efficiency, and task execution. Existing reinforcement learning methods often face bottlenecks in policy generalization—when environmental parameters (e.g., ground friction coefficient, payload mass) shift, the performance of pre-trained policies degrades sharply. AdaptNet specifically targets this "policy-environment mismatch" problem, aiming to establish a mechanism that can perceive environmental changes online and automatically adjust control parameters.

(b) Method or Model Framework: The core of AdaptNet is a two-stage control architecture. The first stage employs meta-learning strategies to pre-train a base motion controller across multiple simulated environments, equipping it with initial robustness to common physical disturbances. The second stage introduces a lightweight adaptation module that generates parameter offsets for the policy network in real time by observing the character's current motion state (e.g., joint angles, contact forces, center-of-mass velocity) and environmental features (e.g., terrain slope, obstacle positions). This design avoids the high computational cost of retraining the entire policy network, allowing the adaptation process to be completed in a single forward pass, meeting real-time control requirements.

(c) Key Technical Innovations: The main innovation of this work lies in proposing a "parametric policy modulation" mechanism. Unlike common methods based on reward function adjustment or action space constraints, AdaptNet directly intervenes in the weight space of the policy network, using a small modulation network to output residual weights that are superimposed onto each linear transformation layer of the base policy. Mathematically, this approach is equivalent to performing an affine transformation on the policy distribution, thereby achieving continuous and smooth adjustments to control behavior without altering the network topology. Additionally, the study designs an adversarial training strategy that forces the adaptation module to learn robust compensation policies by randomly injecting physical disturbances (e.g., sudden lateral pushes or ground sliding) during training.

(d) Experiments/Validation and Application Value: In simulation environments, AdaptNet outperforms baseline methods (e.g., PPO, DMP, and traditional adaptive control) across various standard test scenarios, including slope walking, load carrying, and impact recovery. Experimental data show that the framework maintains over 85% task success rates when facing more than 30% changes in environmental parameters, while baseline methods generally achieve success rates below 60% under the same conditions. From an application perspective, AdaptNet provides a feasible technical pathway for real-time motion control of next-generation humanoid robots—its lightweight adaptation module can be directly deployed on embedded controllers without relying on external computing resources. Furthermore, the modular design of the framework facilitates integration with other high-level planning systems (e.g., navigation, grasping), potentially advancing the practical deployment of humanoid robots in unstructured scenarios such as home services and disaster rescue.
