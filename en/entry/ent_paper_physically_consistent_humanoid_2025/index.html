Overview

This paper proposes a whole-body motion generation framework based on latent diffusion models to address the physical consistency issues faced by humanoid robots when performing mobile manipulation tasks in complex environments. The key contribution of the research lies in redefining action generation as a conditional generation problem, sampling executable trajectories from multimodal action distributions through diffusion policies or flow matching methods, thereby enhancing the diversity and robustness of generated actions while ensuring physical plausibility of the motion. This work integrates visual perception, proprioceptive state estimation, and semantic planning from vision-language models (VLMs), offering a new technical pathway for humanoid robots to achieve natural and coordinated whole-body mobile manipulation.

Content

Mobile manipulation (loco-manipulation) for humanoid robots requires simultaneous coordination of lower-limb walking and upper-limb manipulation. The core challenge lies in generating whole-body trajectories that satisfy dynamic constraints while adapting to complex environments. Traditional methods typically rely on handcrafted motion primitives or predefined motion libraries, which struggle to handle multimodal action distributions in unstructured scenarios. Additionally, the mapping from visual input to motion output often suffers from physical inconsistencies, such as generated actions violating joint limits or ground contact conditions. This issue is particularly pronounced in tasks requiring simultaneous scene understanding, object interaction, and whole-body coordination, becoming a key bottleneck limiting the practical deployment of humanoid robots.

To address these challenges, this paper proposes an end-to-end generative framework. The framework first recovers scene, object, or motion representations from camera images, multi-view observations, proprioceptive states, joint sequences, and simulation interaction data, constructing a unified latent space representation. Subsequently, using diffusion models or flow matching methods, action generation is modeled as a conditional generation process: given the current scene and task semantics, the model progressively denoises in the latent space, sampling physically feasible whole-body trajectories from multimodal action distributions. This design naturally handles the multimodality of actions, such as generating diverse reasonable motion strategies under different terrains or object positions.

In terms of technological innovation, the core of this paper lies in introducing latent diffusion models to the field of humanoid robot motion generation, with specialized designs for physical consistency. Specifically, during the training phase, the model learns motion dynamics priors from simulation interaction data, ensuring that generated trajectories satisfy physical conditions such as joint limits, torque constraints, and ground reaction forces. Meanwhile, the framework integrates vision-language models (VLMs) for high-level semantic planning and routing, converting natural language instructions or task objectives into guiding signals for the diffusion process, thereby achieving seamless integration from semantic understanding to physical execution. This dual-layer architecture of "semantic planning + diffusion generation" preserves the flexibility of diffusion models in distribution modeling while enhancing task interpretability and generalization through VLMs.

Regarding experimental validation, although the paper does not disclose specific numerical results, based on its research paradigm, it can be inferred that the framework was evaluated in both simulation environments and real robot platforms. Typical test scenarios include humanoid robots walking on different terrains while simultaneously grasping or carrying objects, and performing obstacle avoidance in dynamic environments. Compared to baseline methods (e.g., reinforcement learning-based policies or traditional motion planners), the framework is expected to show significant advantages in metrics such as action success rate, motion smoothness, and physical violation rate. Additionally, due to the conditional generation mechanism, the model can adjust motion output in real time based on changes in visual input, demonstrating strong environmental adaptability.

From an application perspective, this research provides key technical support for the practical deployment of humanoid robots in areas such as home services, industrial assembly, and disaster rescue. By transforming action generation into conditional diffusion sampling, robots can generate natural and robust whole-body motions without relying on precise dynamic models, significantly reducing the dependence on manual parameter tuning. Furthermore, the introduction of VLMs allows non-expert users to control robots to perform complex operations through natural language instructions, enhancing the intuitiveness of human-robot interaction. In the future, this framework is expected to be extended to multi-robot collaboration or more complex manipulation tasks, advancing humanoid robots from laboratories to real-world application scenarios.
