Summary

TWIST mainly solves the data closed loop: using human video/kinetrap trajectories, teleoperation/exoskeleton data, contact force/tactile signals to collect human operation and robot states, and then transforming them into trainable and reusable whole-body trajectories/action sequences through heterogeneous kinetrap and synthetic balance data, PPO/RL strategy training, ACT/behavioral cloning imitation learning, and low-level controller targets. The key is to break down tasks into routable skills or expert strategies, which are then selected and combined in execution using high-level modules.

Overview

TWIST (Teleoperated Whole-Body Imitation System) proposes a whole-body teleoperation and imitation learning framework for humanoid robots, aimed at addressing the data closure problem in robot skill acquisition. By integrating multimodal data sources such as human video motion capture, teleoperated exoskeletons, contact forces, and tactile signals, the system unifies human operations and robot states into trainable and reusable whole-body action sequences. Its core contribution lies in constructing a complete technical pipeline from heterogeneous data collection and policy training to high-level task decomposition and skill composition, providing a feasible engineering solution for whole-body coordinated control of humanoid robots in complex environments.

Content

One of the core bottlenecks in current humanoid robot research is the scarcity of high-quality whole-body motion data and the insufficient generalization capability of policies. Traditional methods often rely on a single data source (e.g., pure motion capture or teleoperation), making it difficult to simultaneously achieve natural motion, force feedback, and real-time control accuracy. To address this challenge, the TWIST system proposes a multimodal data closure architecture: using human videos and motion capture trajectories as macro-motion references, exoskeleton teleoperation data for fine joint mapping, and contact forces and tactile signals to supplement ground interaction information, thereby constructing a complete data flow covering whole-body motion and force interaction.

At the methodological level, TWIST uses heterogeneous motion capture and synthetic balance data as preprocessing foundations, achieving motion robustness through PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization) and reinforcement learning policy training, while combining ACT (Action Chunking Transformers) and behavioral cloning imitation learning to convert raw data into reusable whole-body trajectories and low-level controller targets. This hybrid strategy retains the advantages of reinforcement learning in exploration and adaptation while leveraging behavioral cloning to improve training efficiency and action reproduction accuracy.

The key technological breakthrough of the system lies in the "task decomposition-skill routing" mechanism. TWIST decomposes complex manipulation tasks into several routable skill modules or expert policies, each corresponding to a specific set of whole-body motion patterns and force control strategies. The high-level module dynamically selects and combines these skills based on current task requirements and environmental states, enabling rapid deployment of new tasks without retraining. This design significantly enhances the system's scalability and transferability.

In experimental validation, the TWIST system has completed whole-body motion tracking and balance control tests on various humanoid robot platforms. Compared to pure reinforcement learning and pure behavioral cloning methods, TWIST demonstrates superior performance in motion naturalness, task completion rate, and disturbance resistance. Additionally, the system supports a smooth transition from teleoperation data to autonomous policies, providing technical support for the practical application of humanoid robots in scenarios such as industrial assembly and home services.

From an application perspective, the data closure and skill routing framework constructed by TWIST offers a reusable foundational model paradigm for the humanoid robot field. Its "behavioral foundation model" concept—training general whole-body control policies through large-scale heterogeneous data and then achieving task adaptation via high-level modules—has the potential to drive humanoid robots from laboratory demonstrations to real-world deployment. The system's open-source project page and related code also provide an important reference benchmark for subsequent research.
