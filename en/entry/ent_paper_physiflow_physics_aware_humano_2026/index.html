Summary

The implementation path of PhysiFlow is to encode language instructions, camera images/multi-view observations, ontological states, and joint sequences into multimodal representations, then diffusion strategy/flow matching, VLA multimodal action model, VLM Semantic Planning/routing are used to predict the whole body trajectory/action sequence, action chunk/token. The key point is to retain the semantic understanding of VLM, while increasing the robot state and action head, so as to avoid remaining in the language planning.

Overview

PhysiFlow proposes a physics-aware whole-body Vision-Language-Action (VLA) framework for humanoid robots, aiming to address the issue that existing VLA models in robot control remain at the level of linguistic planning and lack perception of physical states and action execution. This study integrates language instructions, multi-view visual observations, proprioceptive states, and joint sequences into a unified multimodal representation through multi-brain latent flow matching and robust tracking mechanisms, and generates whole-body action trajectories using diffusion strategies and flow matching. Its main contribution lies in explicitly introducing robot states and action heads while preserving the semantic understanding capabilities of VLMs, achieving an end-to-end mapping from high-level semantic planning to low-level physical action execution.

Content

Whole-body control of humanoid robots has long faced two major challenges: first, how to effectively fuse natural language instructions with high-dimensional visual and proprioceptive information; second, how to ensure that the generated action sequences are physically feasible and robust. Existing VLA models mostly focus on semantic alignment between language and vision, and the generated plans often lack consideration of robot dynamics constraints and real-time state feedback, leading to instability or infeasibility during action execution. PhysiFlow is specifically designed to bridge this "semantic-physical gap," aiming to construct a complete framework that can both understand complex semantic instructions and output physically consistent whole-body actions.

The core architecture of this method adopts a "multi-brain" latent space design: first, language instructions, multi-view camera images, proprioceptive states (such as joint angles and IMU data), and historical joint sequences are separately encoded into multimodal representations; then, these heterogeneous representations are mapped to a shared latent space via flow matching to achieve cross-modal alignment. Unlike traditional diffusion strategies, flow matching learns a continuous probability path from noise to target actions, enabling more efficient generation of smooth and physically reasonable action sequences. Additionally, the model introduces a VLM semantic planning module as a high-level router, responsible for decomposing complex tasks into sub-goals, while the action head converts these sub-goals into specific joint trajectories.

The key technological innovations of PhysiFlow are reflected in three aspects: first, it proposes a "physics-aware" action head design, which not only receives semantic outputs from the VLM but also fuses the robot's current proprioceptive states in real time, thereby introducing closed-loop feedback during action generation; second, it employs a multi-brain latent flow matching mechanism, allowing information from different modalities to retain their own characteristics in the latent space while achieving dynamic fusion through flow matching, avoiding information loss common in traditional multimodal fusion; third, it designs a robust tracking strategy, which predicts action chunks rather than single-step actions and incorporates temporal consistency constraints, enabling the model to maintain stable whole-body motion even in the presence of observation noise or execution deviations.

In terms of application value, PhysiFlow provides a complete pathway from semantic understanding to physical execution for humanoid robots in complex manipulation tasks. For example, in a task such as "pick up the red cup from the table and hand it to the user," the model first understands the instruction via the VLM and plans the grasping and handing path, then the action head generates continuous whole-body motion trajectories based on current joint states and visual feedback, including torso balance, arm extension, and hand grasping. Due to the introduction of the physics-aware mechanism, the generated trajectories can automatically avoid physically infeasible situations such as joint limits and self-collision. The modular design of this framework also makes it easy to transfer to humanoid robot platforms with different configurations, laying the technical foundation for achieving general manipulation capabilities.
