Definition and Positioning of Hardware-in-the-Loop Simulation

Hardware-in-the-Loop simulation belongs to the method type. Its domains include: assembly integration testing, software middleware. Value chain level: intelligence layer, midstream. A method for testing physical actuators and controllers against real-time simulation models before full integration. The English name is Hardware-in-the-Loop. The Korean name is Hardware-in-the-Loop.

Mathematical and Theoretical Foundations of Hardware-in-the-Loop Simulation

Hardware-in-the-Loop simulation is built upon relevant mathematical theories and physical laws. Understanding its assumptions, constraints, and derivation processes is a prerequisite for correctly applying this method. Specifically, attention must be paid to its input space, output space, objective function, and convergence or stability guarantees. In humanoid robots, which are high-dimensional, underactuated, and strongly coupled systems, Hardware-in-the-Loop simulation typically requires a balance between real-time performance, accuracy, and robustness.

Algorithm Steps and Implementation Key Points

When implementing Hardware-in-the-Loop simulation in practice, it is necessary to define initialization conditions, iteration rules, stopping criteria, and parameter tuning strategies. Choosing appropriate numerical methods, linear algebra solvers, and parallel computing strategies can significantly improve computational efficiency and stability. At the same time, engineering constraints such as model errors, sensor noise, and actuator saturation should be fully considered to ensure reliable operation of the algorithm on real platforms.

Typical Applications and Limitations

Hardware-in-the-Loop simulation can be applied to various aspects of humanoid robots, including motion planning, control optimization, state estimation, and learning algorithms. However, its computational complexity, dependence on model accuracy, and online adaptability remain key issues to address in practical deployment.
