Summary

The implementation path of SONIC is to first encode human video/motion capture trajectories and teleoperation/exoskeleton data into multimodal representations, and then use AMP/motion priors, VLA multimodal action models to predict whole-body trajectories/action sequences, and low-level controller targets. Overview

The whole-body control of humanoid robots has long faced two major challenges: how to efficiently extract motion information from human demonstrations, and how to seamlessly integrate high-level semantic understanding with low-level motion control. SONIC proposes a unified framework that fuses multimodal motion representations with a Vision-Language-Action (VLA) model. By encoding human videos, motion capture trajectories, teleoperation, and exoskeleton data, and introducing robot state and action heads, it achieves end-to-end control from semantic planning to whole-body trajectory prediction. This work aims to overcome the limitations of existing methods that only stop at language planning, providing humanoid robots with motion tracking capabilities that are both natural and robust.

Content

Significant progress has been made in whole-body control research for humanoid robots in recent years, but existing methods still face critical bottlenecks. On one hand, methods based on reinforcement learning or motion priors (e.g., AMP) can generate dynamic motions but struggle to understand semantic instructions in complex tasks. On the other hand, Vision-Language Models (VLMs) excel at high-level planning but lack the ability to model robot proprioceptive states and low-level actions. The core motivation of SONIC is to bridge this gap: enabling robots to both understand semantic instructions like "pick up the cup" and generate matching whole-body motion trajectories, including torso balance, arm coordination, and foot gait.

At the methodological level, SONIC constructs a complete pipeline from data encoding to action generation. First, it uniformly encodes human videos, motion capture trajectories, teleoperation, and exoskeleton data into a multimodal representation, covering information such as visual features, joint angles, and contact forces. This representation is then fed into a prediction module that integrates motion priors (AMP) with a VLA multimodal action model, outputting whole-body trajectories and action sequences. Finally, a low-level controller converts these trajectories into joint torque commands to drive the real robot. The key design here is that the semantic understanding capability of the VLM is fully preserved, while the newly added robot state encoding and action heads allow the model to directly output physically feasible motion instructions, rather than remaining at the level of language planning.

SONIC's technical innovations are mainly reflected in three aspects. First, it proposes a "large-scale motion tracking" paradigm, which significantly expands the scale and diversity of training data by integrating multiple heterogeneous data sources (videos, motion capture, teleoperation), thereby enhancing the model's generalization ability for complex motion patterns. Second, the model architecture introduces explicit robot state embeddings and action heads, enabling the VLM to perceive physical constraints such as joint limits and center-of-mass positions, avoiding the generation of infeasible actions. Third, by combining AMP's motion priors with VLA's action prediction, SONIC maintains motion naturalness while achieving precise responses to high-level semantic instructions, such as adaptively adjusting gait parameters based on language descriptions like "walk slowly" or "turn quickly."

In terms of experimental validation, SONIC was tested on various humanoid robot platforms, including simulation environments and real robots. Results show that this method outperforms baselines relying solely on AMP or VLM in whole-body motion tracking accuracy, task completion rate, and motion naturalness. Specifically, in tasks such as dynamic balance maintenance, walking on complex terrain, and object manipulation, SONIC generates trajectories closer to human demonstrations and exhibits stronger robustness to sensor noise and external disturbances. Additionally, ablation experiments confirm that multimodal data fusion and state-aware action heads are key components for performance improvement; the absence of either module leads to a significant decline in motion quality.

From an application perspective, SONIC provides key technical support for deploying humanoid robots in scenarios such as service, manufacturing, and rescue. Its unified framework allows robots to simultaneously handle language instructions and motion tracking without designing separate control strategies for different tasks. For example, in a home service scenario, a robot can autonomously plan a walking path, adjust arm posture, and maintain body balance based on the instruction "get water from the table," without any human intervention. This capability is expected to significantly reduce the programming complexity of humanoid robots, driving their transition from the lab to practical applications.
