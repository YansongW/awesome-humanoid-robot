Summary

This work mainly addresses the data closed loop: collecting human operations and robot states using camera images/multi-view observations, human videos/kinetach trajectories, teleoperation/exoskeleton data, and then converting them into trainable and reusable whole-body trajectory/action sequences through PPO/RL strategy training and hierarchical skills/expert strategies. The key is to break down tasks into routable skills or expert strategies, which are then selected and combined in execution using high-level modules.

Overview

This paper proposes a whole-body operation interface for humanoid robots, aiming to address the closed-loop problem between data collection and policy generalization in robotic manipulation tasks. The primary motivation is that traditional methods rely on teleoperation or exoskeleton devices to obtain robot state data, which are costly and difficult to scale; while human natural demonstration data are easy to acquire, they are hard to directly map to the robot's own body. The core contribution of this paper is the construction of a framework that converts robot-agnostic demonstration data (e.g., human videos, motion capture trajectories) into trainable and reusable whole-body trajectories and action sequences. Through reinforcement learning (PPO/RL) policy training and a hierarchical skill architecture, it achieves efficient task-level execution and composition.

Content

(a) Research Background and Problem: The whole-body manipulation capability of humanoid robots is key to performing complex physical interaction tasks (e.g.,搬运, assembly, home services). However, current research faces a fundamental bottleneck: how to efficiently acquire and utilize diverse operational data to train robot policies. On one hand, directly collecting robot state data (e.g., joint torques, end-effector poses) requires expensive teleoperation or exoskeleton systems, limiting data scale; on the other hand, human natural demonstration data (e.g., RGB videos, motion capture trajectories) are abundant and easy to obtain but lack a direct correspondence with the robot's kinematic and dynamic models. Existing methods mostly focus on a single data source or specific tasks, making it difficult to achieve a general mapping from human demonstrations to whole-body robot control. This paper specifically addresses this "data closed-loop" problem by proposing a solution that integrates multimodal observations and hierarchical policies.

(b) Method or Model Framework: The framework proposed in this paper centers on the "data closed-loop," with a process comprising three main stages. First, multiple data sources such as camera images, multi-view observations, human videos, and motion capture trajectories are used to collect human manipulation behaviors and robot state information. Second, reinforcement learning algorithms like PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization) are employed to train policies on these heterogeneous data, transforming them into trainable and reusable whole-body trajectories and action sequences. Finally, a hierarchical skill and expert policy mechanism is introduced, decomposing complex tasks into a series of routable basic skills (e.g., grasping, moving, placing). A high-level module dynamically selects and combines these skills based on environmental states during execution, thereby achieving efficient task-level performance.

(c) Key Technical Innovations: The technical innovations of this paper are mainly reflected in three aspects. First, it proposes a "robot-agnostic" approach to utilizing demonstration data, which does not rely on the hardware configuration of a specific robot. Instead, it directly extracts motion features from human demonstrations and adapts them to the robot's body through reinforcement learning policies, significantly reducing data collection costs. Second, it constructs a hierarchical skill and expert policy architecture that decouples low-level continuous action control from high-level task planning, enabling policies to be not only reusable but also capable of flexible task composition in complex scenarios. Third, through multimodal observation fusion (vision, motion capture, exoskeleton data), it enhances the policy's ability for scene understanding and vision-guided control, improving robustness in unstructured environments.

(d) Experiments/Validation or Application Value: Although this paper does not provide specific experimental data, it can be inferred from the framework design that the method is applicable to various humanoid robot manipulation scenarios, such as home services, industrial assembly, and logistics handling. By converting human natural demonstrations into executable whole-body trajectories for robots, this interface is expected to significantly lower the programming and deployment barriers for humanoid robots. Furthermore, the hierarchical skill architecture endows the policy with good transferability—the same skill library can be adapted to different humanoid robot morphologies, requiring only fine-tuning of the low-level mapping through reinforcement learning. This paradigm holds significant value for advancing humanoid robots from laboratories to practical applications, especially in scenarios requiring rapid adaptation to new tasks and environments.
